Fake Remote Work

Are your remote employees actually working — or just pretending to? And are you sure you're not doing the same?

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocative Headline
⏱ 4 min
Microsoft Work Trend Index · 2023
"85% of leaders say the shift to hybrid work has made it harder to be confident that employees are being productive. Meanwhile, 87% of employees say they work just as hard or harder from home."
Teacher Script: Put the stat on screen and ask: "So who's lying — the bosses or the employees?" Let that sit for 5 seconds. Then: "Have you ever pretended to be working when you weren't? Be honest — I won't tell anyone." If students stay silent, share a relatable example yourself. The goal is to crack the professional armor in the first 60 seconds.

Provocateur
👨‍⚖️
Roberto · 52
CEO · Querétaro
"If you need to hide what you're doing during work hours, you're stealing from your employer. Simple as that. I don't care if you're 'productive' — you made a commitment when you accepted the salary. Fake remote work isn't a gray area. It's fraud. And I've fired people for it."
Teacher Note: Roberto is the provocateur because he uses the word "fraud" and "stealing" — language most employees will instinctively reject. Push students: "Is Roberto right? Is fake remote work actually fraud?" Then: "What's the difference between working slow and faking it?"

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever worked remotely — even for a day? What was the hardest part about staying focused?
Push for specifics: time of day, type of distraction, how they dealt with it. Avoid: "Yes, it was hard." Target: "I've noticed that I tend to lose focus around 3pm when there's no one checking on me."
Q2
Have you ever felt like a colleague wasn't really working remotely — but you couldn't prove it?
Model answer: "Yes — there was someone on my team who was always online but never responded to messages for hours. We all assumed he was doing something else, but his numbers were fine, so nobody said anything." Push: "Did you say anything? Should someone have?"
Q3
Be honest — have you ever been "at work" but not really working? What were you doing instead?
This question breaks the professional mask. Most students will laugh nervously. Give them permission: "I'm not your boss — this is English practice." Target answers that use past continuous: "I was watching Netflix during a meeting" or "I was texting the whole afternoon."
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Do you think companies have the right to monitor their remote employees — cameras, screen tracking, keyloggers? Where's the line?
Push back if they say "yes, fully." Try: "What if your company installed a camera in your home office — would that be okay?" Target B2.1 language: "I think it depends on the industry" / "There should be a limit" / "That crosses the line."
Q5
Should salary be based on hours worked or results delivered? Which system is more fair — and which is easier to fake?
Model: "I think results-based is more fair, but it's also easier to fake in creative roles. At least with hours, there's some accountability — even if it's not a perfect measure." Target: comparative structures and modals (should, would, might).
Q6
Is there a difference between "working slow" and "faking it"? Can you draw a clear line between the two?
Key vocab to target: intention, commitment, deliberate, output. Push: "If someone is paid to be available 8 hours and they're not actually available — is that the same as theft, like Roberto said?"
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If your company installed software to track every app you open during work hours, what would you do? Would you stay?
Target: second conditional (would + base verb). Model: "If my company did that, I'd probably start looking for another job — not because I'm faking it, but because it would feel like I'm not trusted." Push: "But if you have nothing to hide, why would it bother you?"
Q8
If you could design your company's remote work policy from zero, what would it look like? Would you include any monitoring?
Push for specifics: "3 days a week?" / "Core hours?" / "Results-only?" Good target for modals + conditionals: "I'd probably require one check-in per day and measure people by deliverables, not hours."
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Have companies earned the right to demand 8 hours of visible work from their employees — or did they lose that right when they started paying as little as possible?
This is intentionally one-sided to provoke disagreement. Let the student push back or agree — both are valid. Target: present perfect for experience, modals for obligation. Watch for students who suddenly become very passionate. That's exactly what you want.
Q10
Here's the uncomfortable one: on a scale of 1 to 10, how productive are YOU during a typical 8-hour workday — remote or in the office? Be honest.
This is the self-reflection question. Most adults will say 5 or 6 and feel slightly guilty. The goal isn't to make them confess — it's to get real, personal English. Push: "What happens during the hours when you're NOT productive? What's going on in your head?"

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One Final Dilemma
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
Your company has always trusted you completely — no tracking, no monitoring. Then you discover that a colleague who does exactly your same job earns 20% more than you. Starting next Monday, you could "fake it" for 2 hours a day and use that time for a freelance project. Nobody would ever find out. Do you do it?
Homework Loop: "Record a 60-second WhatsApp voice message answering this question — in English. No script. Tell me what you'd actually do and why. I want to hear your real opinion, not the 'correct' answer."
