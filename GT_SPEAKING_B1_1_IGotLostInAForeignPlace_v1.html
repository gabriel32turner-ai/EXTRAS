§ 01 · The Trigger
🪝 When Getting Lost Changes Everything
Read aloud · First reaction only · No debate yet · 4 min max
01
4 min
🗺️
Booking.com Travel Report + Travel + Leisure Survey 2023
72%
Of travelers say getting lost abroad was one of their most memorable travel experiences — even though it was scary at first
1 in 3
Tourists say they found their favorite restaurant, neighborhood, or friend by accident — because they were lost
54%
Of people say getting lost abroad made them feel more confident and independent after the experience
2 hrs
The average time travelers spent truly lost on their most memorable trip — long enough to panic, short enough to laugh about later
Sources: Booking.com Traveler Report 2023 · Travel + Leisure Annual Survey · Lonely Planet "Why Getting Lost Is the Best Travel Plan"
🪝 Opening Question — Read this, then point at someone
"You are in a city you don't know. Your phone has no battery. You don't speak the language. You need to find your hotel before dark. What do you do first?"
Teacher Mechanics — 4 min Read slowly. Let the "before dark" detail create urgency. Point at one student: "What do you do — right now?" No judgment. 2–3 reactions max. Say: "We come back to all of this in §4."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The 72% "memorable experience" stat reframes being lost as positive — but the trigger presents it as scary. Both are true. That tension is the activation.
  • If nobody has traveled abroad: "Has anyone been lost in a city in Mexico they didn't know well? The feeling is the same." The experience is universal even if the travel isn't.
  • Watch for students who have a strong story — they will carry §4 Block 1. Note them now.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The "no battery, no language" detail is intentional — it strips away modern safety nets and creates a real problem students feel immediately.
  • Best activation if quiet: "Raise your hand if you've ever been lost somewhere — in Mexico or abroad — and didn't know how to get back."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Retention: "Next Travel lab we talk about food you tried and regretted. Same trip energy — different disaster."

"Who do you agree with most — and who do you think made the best decision? Why?"

🔁 Processing Question — Pairs → Group
"Who do you agree with most — and who do you think made the best decision? Why?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The provocateur is Karla. Her position — that getting lost is irresponsible and you should always plan — will clash with anyone who has had a great accidental discovery. Push: "Is Karla wrong? Or is she just a different kind of traveler?"
  • Read all 4 before opening debate. The contrast between Tomás (chaos as adventure) and Karla (plan everything) drives the session.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Read Tomás with enthusiasm. Read Karla with firm confidence. The tonal contrast sparks the reaction before students even process the words.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Testimonial trigger: "One sentence — which of these four travelers are you? Be honest."
§ 04 · The Conversation Engine
🗣 10 Questions. 4 Blocks. 23 Minutes.
Personal → Opinion → Hypothetical → Provocative · Point and push · No round-robin
04
23 min
⚡
Rule: At least 2 sentences. Say what happened — then say how you felt. Or say your opinion — then say why.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Block 1 is storytelling-heavy — the best B1.1 output comes from narrative. If a student gives a one-sentence answer, push: "And then what happened? How did you feel when you found the hotel?"
  • Block 3 second conditional: watch for "If I would get lost" — note and recycle at end only. Don't interrupt stories.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • When a student tells a travel disaster story with detail: let it run 45 seconds. Then: "Now say the best part of that story in one English sentence."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • After Q10: "You just told travel stories in English. That's a real social skill — not just grammar. Next lab we talk about travel food disasters."
§ 05 · The Exit Shot
💣 One Question. No Easy Answer.
5 min · No chips · Position + reason · Then the loop
05
5 min
EXIT
💣 Exit Shot
You are traveling alone in a city you don't know. You have 2 hours before your bus. You get lost. A local stranger offers to show you the way — but it means going through a part of the city that looks a little dark and empty. Do you follow them?
3 sentences minimum. Yes or no first. Then tell me what you would actually feel in that moment — not what sounds brave.
📌 B1.1 Model Answer — Reveal AFTER student speaks
"Honestly, I don't know. I would look at the person very carefully first — their face, their eyes. If they seemed kind, I would probably follow them, because I was scared anyway and they were the only person who offered help. But I would text my location to a friend first, just in case."
🔁 Retention Loop — WhatsApp Homework "Before next session — record a 60-second voice note in English. Your answer to today's Exit Shot. No preparation. Just talk. Send it to [teacher number]."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The scenario is ambiguous by design — no clear right answer. Trust vs safety. Practical need vs fear. That tension produces the most honest voice notes.
  • Follow-up: "What would you do differently if you were traveling with a friend instead of alone?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The model answer is honest and practical — "text my location first." Point out: "That last sentence — 'just in case' — is B1.1 English that works in a real situation."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Closing: "You just described a real decision moment — in English. That's useful language. Not textbook. Real."
