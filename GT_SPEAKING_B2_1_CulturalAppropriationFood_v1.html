Cultural Appropriation
in Food

When a French chef wins a Michelin star for "elevated" tacos, is that a tribute — or is something being taken without credit?

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocative Headline
⏱ 4 min
The New York Times · Food Section · 2021
"A white chef in Portland opened a burrito cart after a trip to Mexico. Local Latina food vendors — some of whom had been selling burritos for decades — were pushed out of the market within months. The cart was featured in every food blog in the city. The women who taught themselves the same recipes were not."
Teacher Script: Read the quote slowly. Then ask: "What's your reaction? Is this a story about food — or about something bigger?" Let students name the issue before you name it. Then: "Does this story feel familiar? Has something like this ever happened with Mexican food — or any food from your culture?" Mexican students often have strong feelings about how their food is represented (or misrepresented) abroad.

The Opinion Wall
Phase 3 · Who do you agree with?
⏱ 10 min
👨‍🍳
Tomás · 34
Chef · Guadalajara
"I love it when foreign chefs get inspired by Mexican food. It means our cuisine is respected globally. The problem isn't inspiration — it's when they get the credit and we don't. Give us a mention, learn the real history, bring us into the conversation. Inspiration with acknowledgment is called cuisine. Inspiration without it is called theft."
👩‍🎓
Adriana · 29
Cultural Studies grad student · CDMX
"Food has always traveled. Tomatoes came to Italy from the Americas. Chili went from Mexico to Thailand. Pasta was influenced by China. The idea of 'pure' national cuisine is a myth. What we should care about is economic justice — who profits, who gets the platform, who gets told their version is 'authentic' and who gets told theirs is 'street food.'"
🧑‍🏫
Omar · 47
Anthropology professor · Monterrey
"Cultural appropriation in food becomes a problem when it erases the original source. When a foreign chef's version of your dish is considered more legitimate than yours — that's not fusion, that's a power imbalance. The question isn't 'can you cook it?' The question is 'why does your version get the Michelin star and the original gets ignored?'"
Provocateur
🍜
Isabelle · 41
Food critic · Paris
"I'm tired of this conversation. Cuisine is not property. No country owns a dish. Mexicans didn't invent the chile — it existed in the Americas long before nations did. The idea that a French chef can't cook tacos is as absurd as saying a Mexican chef can't make croissants. We should be celebrating the best version of a dish — not policing who is allowed to cook it."
Teacher Note: Isabelle is the provocateur because she rejects the premise entirely and frames the debate as "policing" — language that will irritate students who care about this. Push: "Is Isabelle ignoring the economic and power dimension — or is she making a genuinely valid point about the freedom of culinary creativity?" Mexican students will often have a personal stake here, which is exactly what makes it rich territory.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever eaten a "Mexican" dish abroad — or seen one described on a menu — and felt like it had nothing to do with the real thing? What was missing?
Rich territory for Mexican students. Most have a "fake Mexican food" story — Tex-Mex, hard shell tacos, cheese on everything. Let them vent. Then shift: "Is a bad version of your food offensive — or just bad cooking?" Target: present perfect for experience + past simple for the specific story.
Q2
Have you ever cooked or eaten food from another culture and felt you were doing something meaningful — connecting to another place or people through food?
Flips the script — makes the student the "outsider" cooking another culture's food. Push: "Does that feeling of connection justify cooking it — or do you feel like you need permission?" This is the emotional counterpoint to the critical questions.
Q3
Can you think of a dish from your own culture that has been adopted — or changed — by another country? Does the new version bother you, or do you see it as a compliment?
Mexico is rich with examples: Taco Bell, "Mexican pizza," matcha tacos in Japan. But also consider the reverse: mole, pozole, and mezcal have been elevated globally in ways that sometimes benefit Mexican producers. Push: "Is there a difference between adapting a dish and distorting it?"
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is there a real difference between "cultural exchange" and "cultural appropriation" when it comes to food — or is it just about who profits?
The definitional question. Adriana and Omar both offer different answers. Push: "What's the key variable — intent, acknowledgment, or money?" Target: present simple for beliefs + modals for judgment (should, ought to, must). Don't let students give vague answers — press for the specific factor that makes the difference.
Q5
Should foreign chefs be required to credit the culture a dish comes from — the way musicians credit a sample? Is a recipe more like art or more like a technique?
The music sampling analogy is useful and accessible for most students. Push: "If a musician has to pay royalties for sampling 3 seconds of a song — shouldn't a chef do something equivalent?" Target: conditional + should. Key vocab: credit, acknowledge, intellectual property, inspiration vs. theft.
Q6
Is Isabelle right — does "cultural ownership" of food make any sense? Or does the history of how food travels make ownership impossible to define?
Steel-manning Isabelle. The historical point is real — food has always traveled across cultures. Push: "If tomatoes are from the Americas and now define Italian food — does Italy 'own' pasta with tomato sauce?" The goal is intellectual honesty, not comfortable answers. Target: modals for uncertainty + present perfect for historical patterns.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If a Japanese chef opened a high-end taquería in Mexico City, learned the recipes authentically, credited the origins, and employed local cooks — would that still be appropriation? Where exactly is the line?
Target: second conditional + nuanced position-taking. This scenario removes most of the easy objections — the chef is respectful, knowledgeable, and gives back economically. Push: "Is the remaining discomfort rational — or is it something else?" This is where the conversation gets genuinely interesting.
Q8
If a Mexican chef opened a very successful French restaurant in Paris — and the French press started crediting a "Mexican twist" on classical cuisine — would that be appropriation or contribution? Does direction matter?
The power-direction flip. Push: "Does the concept of 'appropriation' only work in one direction — from powerful cultures toward less powerful ones?" Target: second conditional + modals. This question often shifts students who have held a rigid position — in a productive way.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Is the anger around cultural appropriation in food sometimes more about marketing and visibility than about actual harm — or is visibility itself the harm?
Meta-question about the debate itself. The argument: a foreign chef getting Michelin stars for "elevated" tacos doesn't physically harm anyone — but it does reshape what the world considers "legitimate" cuisine. Push: "Can something be unfair without causing direct harm?" Target: present simple for principles + conditional for consequences.
Q10
Have you ever eaten at a restaurant that was doing something "wrong" with a food you care about — and said nothing because you didn't want to seem difficult? Why didn't you say something?
The personal accountability closer. Brings the abstract debate into a real, everyday moment. Push: "Is staying silent about that a small version of the same problem?" Target: past simple + reflection on choice. The student who has a strong opinion about cultural appropriation but never corrects a bad taco needs to sit with that tension.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One Final Dilemma
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
A well-known American food magazine publishes a feature on "the best tacos in the world." Every single restaurant on the list is in the US or Europe, run by non-Mexican chefs. The magazine says they chose based purely on quality and innovation. You're a food journalist with a platform and 50,000 followers. Do you write about it — and if so, what exactly do you say?
Homework Loop: "Record a 60-second WhatsApp voice message. Tell me: do you write about it? And give me your first paragraph — in English — out loud. No editing. Your real reaction."
