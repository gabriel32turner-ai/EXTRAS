Scrolling Until 3 AM
You know you should sleep. But you keep scrolling. Why does this happen — and what is it actually doing to your life?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
A real headline. A real conversation.
⏱ 4 min
BBC Science Focus · 2023
"Scientists now have a name for it: 'revenge bedtime procrastination.' After a long, stressful day where you had no free time, you stay up late scrolling — even when you're exhausted — because it's the only time of day that feels like yours."
Teacher Script
"Before we start — raise your hand if you went to bed later than you planned last night because of your phone. Now: did you feel guilty about it the next morning? Today we're going to talk about something everyone does and almost nobody talks about honestly."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real talk. No right answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever stayed up really late because of your phone — and regretted it the next morning? What happened?
Almost everyone says yes. Push for a specific story: what were they watching or doing? Why couldn't they stop? "I was watching videos of people building cabins in Canada at 1:30 AM" is more interesting than "I was scrolling Instagram." Specific stories unlock better vocabulary.
Q2
Have you ever checked your phone's screen time report and been surprised — or shocked — by the number?
Great real-world hook. Most people have done this. Ask: "What did you do after you saw that number?" — because the gap between seeing the problem and changing behavior is exactly where the conversation lives.
Q3
Have you ever tried to use your phone less at night and failed? What was the hardest part?
Model answer: "I tried to stop at 10 PM for a week. The hardest part wasn't boredom — it was silence. My brain didn't know what to do without stimulation. I ended up just staring at the ceiling. That was scarier than the phone."
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
In your opinion, is late-night scrolling a personal problem — or is it the app companies' fault for making their products so hard to stop?
The responsibility question. Both sides are valid at B1.2. Don't let them say "both" without explaining how — push: "If it's the company's fault, should the government regulate it? If it's personal, what should you do differently?"
Q5
Do you think people who scroll until 3 AM are lazy, stressed, or just lonely? What do you think is really going on?
Watch for empathy vs. judgment. The "lazy" answer is easy and often wrong. Push toward the "revenge bedtime procrastination" idea from the trigger: it's not laziness — it's a response to a day that had no free time in it. That reframe matters.
Q6
Should schools teach children how to use phones in a healthy way — or is that the parents' job?
Great B1.2 opinion structure: "I think it's the parents' job because..." / "I think schools should because..." Push for a reason, not just a position. This question also works well for parents in the group who have kids with phones.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your doctor told you that your phone use was directly causing your sleep problems — and gave you three months to fix it?
Force them to make a plan. Vague answers like "I would use it less" aren't enough. Push: "Specifically — what would you change first? Where would you charge your phone? What would you do instead?" The plan reveals the real obstacles.
Q8
What would you do if you had one completely free evening with no work, no family responsibilities, and no phone — just you and the night? Would you be okay?
The silence test. Many adults realize they have no idea what they would do without a screen. If they struggle to answer — that IS the answer. Great vocabulary moment: "I would read / cook / draw / just sit / feel anxious." All honest options.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest: is your phone the last thing you look at before you sleep and the first thing you look at when you wake up? What does that say about your relationship with it?
For most people the answer is yes. The follow-up is what matters: "Is that okay with you?" Don't rush them. The moment when they say "no, actually it's not okay" — and they realize they've been saying that for months without changing anything — is the most valuable moment in the class.
Q10
If you could send a message to yourself ten years from now, would you say: "I wish I had scrolled more" — or something else? What would you actually regret?
The closer. Nobody says they'll regret not scrolling more. Let that silence land. Then ask: "So what would you regret not doing instead of scrolling?" That question redirects from problem to value — the most powerful conversation a language class can have.
