Red Flags vs. Deal-Breakers

Not every red flag is a deal-breaker. But how do you know the difference — and what does your list say about you?

🪝
The Trigger
Open the Conversation
⏱ 4 min
The Atlantic — "The Rise of the Deal-Breaker List"
"Dating apps have given people an unprecedented ability to filter out partners before ever meeting them. Psychologists warn that our deal-breaker lists have become so long that we're filtering out perfectly compatible people based on superficial incompatibilities we'd adapt to in real life."
🎙 Teacher Script

Read the headline. Then pivot immediately: "Before we talk about relationships — do you even know the difference between a red flag and a deal-breaker?" Let students try to define both. Most will confuse them. That confusion is the hook. Then ask: "Give me one of yours. What's something that's a deal-breaker for you — no exceptions?" The first real answer unlocks the whole room.

The Core Distinction — Red Flag vs. Deal-Breaker
🚩 Red Flag 🚫 Deal-Breaker
Definition A warning sign — something that could become a problem. Requires attention. An absolute limit — if this exists, the relationship ends or never starts.
Can you work with it? Maybe. It depends on context, pattern, and communication. No. It's non-negotiable, regardless of other positives.
Example They cancel plans once in a while with vague excuses. They are dishonest with money — hidden debts, lies about finances.
Emotional response "I'm watching this carefully." "I'm out. No second chances."

The Conversation Engine
23 Minutes of Real Talk
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever ignored a red flag in a relationship — romantic, friendship, or professional — and regretted it later? What was the flag?
Allow vulnerability here. If students hesitate, normalize it: "Most adults have at least one. You don't need to give details — just the type: dishonesty? Temper? Bad communication?" The first person who shares usually unlocks the group.
Q2
Have you ever ended a relationship — or a job — because of something that felt like a deal-breaker at the time? Looking back, was it really?
Model answer: "I ended a job because my boss would criticize me publicly. At the time it felt like a personal attack. Looking back, it was still a deal-breaker — but now I understand it was about his management style, not about me."
Q3
Have your deal-breakers changed as you've gotten older? Something that was non-negotiable at 22 that you'd overlook now — or vice versa?
High engagement question. Most adults will say their list has shifted. The direction of that shift (stricter or more flexible) reveals a lot about their experiences. Let them explore it without judging either direction.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
What's the line between having standards and being too picky? How do you know when your list is healthy — and when it's protecting you from real connection?
This is Renata's question in conversation form. Push them: "Is there a difference between a standard that protects you and a standard that keeps everyone out?" Both can look identical from the outside.
Q5
Can a deal-breaker apply to friendships and work — or is it only a relationship word? What are your professional deal-breakers?
This pivots the conversation to work, which is more comfortable for professional students. Common answers: dishonest bosses, no recognition, micromanagement, toxic teams. Let them list theirs. Then ask: "Have you ever stayed in a job past a deal-breaker?"
Q6
If someone you're interested in has a genuine deal-breaker for other people — like they never want children, or they have a different religion — but you're not sure it matters to you yet, do you stay or leave?
Model answer: "I think you stay and have the honest conversation early. A deal-breaker you discover on month 6 is more painful than one you discover on week 2. The earlier, the better — even if it's uncomfortable."
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
Imagine you've been in a relationship for 2 years and you discover your partner has hidden debt — significant debt they never mentioned. Is that a red flag or an automatic deal-breaker?
This polarizes groups every time. Some say it depends on the amount and the reason. Others say the hiding is the deal-breaker, not the debt itself. Both are valid — let both sides make their case.
Q8
If you could show your 22-year-old self your current deal-breaker list, what would they think? And what would your 60-year-old self change about today's list?
Creative and personal. The time-travel frame helps students articulate things they wouldn't otherwise say. Let the answers be as specific or abstract as they want.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Renata said most deal-breakers are "wounds wearing disguises." Do you think that's true for you — even a little? Is any of your list actually about your past, not your future?
The most uncomfortable question in the session. Don't pressure. But also don't rescue it too quickly. Silence here is productive. If a student says "maybe" — follow with: "What would have to change for that item to move from deal-breaker to red flag?"
Q10
Honestly: have you ever been someone else's red flag? What was it — and did you know it at the time?
The perspective-flip closer. Most people only think about red flags in others. Being asked to reflect on themselves is unexpected and powerful. Model answer: "Yes. I was inconsistent with my time and attention. I knew, but I justified it. It was a pattern."

The Exit Shot
One Question. No Easy Answer.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Final Dilemma
You've been with someone for 3 years. Everything is good — compatibility, values, communication. Then you find out they had one episode of infidelity 5 years before they met you, with their previous partner. They told you voluntarily. They've never done it again. Is that a deal-breaker — or does the past stay in the past?
