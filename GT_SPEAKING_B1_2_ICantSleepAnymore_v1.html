I Can't Sleep Anymore
You lie in bed, your brain won't stop, and the alarm is in 5 hours. Why is everyone so tired — and why does nobody talk about it?
🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocation & Opening
⏱ 4 min
Gallup World Poll · 2023
"Mexico is one of the most sleep-deprived countries in Latin America. 6 out of 10 Mexican adults say they don't sleep well — and most say work stress is the main reason."
Teacher script: Read it. Then ask simply: "How did you sleep last night?" Wait for real answers. Then: "When was the last time you slept really well — like you woke up and actually felt good? What was happening in your life at that time?" Let this question open the personal stories naturally.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever gone through a period in your life when you couldn't sleep well? What was happening at that time?
Teacher Tip Almost everyone has a specific period. Push for it: "Was it a job? A relationship? Money? A family situation? How long did it last — and what changed?" This is where the best personal vocabulary comes out.
Q2
Have you ever stayed up very late looking at your phone or watching something — and then regretted it the next day at work?
Model Answer "Yes — too many times. I tell myself 'just one more video' and suddenly it's 1:30am. Then I wake up at 6 and I feel terrible all day. I know exactly what I'm doing. I do it anyway. That's the worst part."
Q3
Have you ever received a work message at night — after 10pm — that kept you awake or stressed? What did you do?
Teacher Tip Very common in Mexican work culture. Push: "Did you respond immediately? Why? What would happen if you didn't? Has anyone in your company ever actually set a rule about this?"
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
In your opinion, is poor sleep mostly a personal habit problem — or a work and stress problem? Can you separate the two?
Teacher Tip Great for pushing nuanced thinking at B1.2. "If you fixed your phone habit, would you sleep well? Or would you just lie in bed worrying about work instead? Which one is harder to fix?"
Q5
Do you think it is normal to wake up at night and think about work — or is that a sign that something is wrong?
Model Answer "I think it is common — but not normal. Common means many people do it. Normal means it is healthy. We have accepted something that is actually harming us because everyone does it. That doesn't make it right."
Q6
What do you think about apps and podcasts that help you sleep — things like sleep sounds, meditation, or ASMR? Do they really work, or is it just a business?
Teacher Tip Fun and accessible topic. "Have you ever tried any of these? What happened? Is there something a little strange about using a screen to fix a screen problem?" Keeps it light before the deeper questions.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your doctor told you that your sleep problems were seriously affecting your health and you had to make one big change immediately?
Model Answer "I would probably stop checking my phone after 9pm. I know that's the main problem. I just need a reason big enough to actually do it. A doctor saying 'this is damaging your heart' might be exactly that reason."
Q8
What would you do if your company said: "No work messages after 8pm — it is now company policy"? Would you feel relieved, or would you find it difficult?
Teacher Tip Interesting because many people feel anxious about the idea of "disconnecting" — even when they want to. "Would part of you feel worried that you might miss something important? What does that say about your relationship with work?"
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Do you think you use your phone late at night because you enjoy it — or because you are avoiding something? What are you avoiding?
Teacher Tip The deepest question of the session. Give real time. Common honest answers: avoiding thoughts about work, about relationships, about problems they don't want to face. No judgment — just space.
Q10
When you lie in bed and your brain won't stop — what does it think about most? Work? Money? The future? Relationships? And when was the last time you actually did something about that?
Model Answer "Money, mostly. I run through numbers in my head at 2am. And the honest answer to your second question is: I have never really done anything about it. I just wait for morning. Which means the problem is still there the next night. It's a terrible cycle."

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · Final Dilemma · No right answer
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot
Your doctor tells you this: "You are sleeping an average of 5 hours a night. In 3 years, if nothing changes, you will have serious health problems — high blood pressure, heart risk, cognitive damage. The fix is simple: be in bed at 10pm every night with no phone. But that means giving up the only 2 hours of the day that are truly yours." What do you choose — and can you actually do it?
📱 WhatsApp Homework Record 60 seconds in English: What do you decide — and what is the hardest part about actually doing it?
