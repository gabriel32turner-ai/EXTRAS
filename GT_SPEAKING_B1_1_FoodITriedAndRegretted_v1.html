§ 01 · The Trigger
🪝 The Science of Disgust and Curiosity
Read aloud · First reaction only · No debate yet · 4 min max
01
4 min
🤢
National Geographic Food + Condé Nast Traveler Survey 2023
88%
Of travelers say trying local food is one of the most important parts of visiting a new place — even if it goes wrong
1 in 2
Tourists say they have eaten something abroad that made them feel sick — but half of them say they would try it again
74%
Of people have eaten something out of politeness — because a local or host offered it — and immediately regretted it
3 days
The average time it takes for a food disaster to become a "funny story" — the same meal that ruined a day becomes the best story of the trip
Sources: National Geographic "The World's Most Challenging Foods" 2023 · Condé Nast Traveler Annual Survey · Airbnb Experiences Food Report
🪝 Opening Question — Read this, then point at someone
"A local family invites you to dinner during your trip. They put something on your plate that smells very strange and looks like nothing you have ever eaten. They are all watching you. What do you do?"
Teacher Mechanics — 4 min Read slowly and with a little drama. Let "they are all watching you" land. Point at one student: "What do you do?" No judgment. 2–3 reactions. "We talk about all of this in §4."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The 74% "ate out of politeness" stat is the most relatable — this happens in Mexico too, not just abroad. Use it: "Has anyone here eaten something at a family dinner in Mexico that they didn't want to? Same energy."
  • The "3 days" stat reframes the experience — the disaster becomes the story. That's the emotional arc of the session.
  • If nobody has traveled: "Has anyone eaten something at a wedding, a party, or a family dinner that was really bad? Same feeling — just closer to home."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • This topic generates laughter immediately — food disaster stories are universally funny in retrospect. Let the energy be playful. The honesty follows the laughter.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Retention: "Next Travel lab we look at tourist traps — when you pay a lot for something that turns out to be terrible. Food traps are part of it."

Challenge Mode Available

💡 10 Nouns — Food, Taste & Regret
🔵 10 Verbs — What Brave Eaters Do
⚡ Production Challenge — No preparation
Pick 3 words. One sentence each about a food experience — good, bad, or ugly. Be specific. No writing. Just talk.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • "Disgusting" — strong emotional word. Model it with appropriate face: "It was disgusting — I wanted to spit it out but everyone was watching." The physicality makes it memorable at B1.1.
  • "Brave" as a positive — eating something scary takes courage. Use it to reframe: "Being a brave eater is a real travel skill."
  • Best production trigger: "Which 3 words describe the worst thing you ever ate? Don't hold back."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "Food vocabulary is social vocabulary. If you're ever at a dinner with English speakers — this is what you need to describe the food and your face."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "These words are not just for travel. They're for every dinner, every party, every time someone asks 'did you like it?' in English."

🔁 Processing Question — Pairs → Group
"Who do you agree with most — and who do you think is the bravest? Why?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The provocateur is Ingrid. Her position — that refusing strange food is disrespectful to the culture — creates immediate friction with students who have strong food preferences. Push: "Is she right? Is saying 'no thank you' really disrespectful?"
  • All 4 voices are sympathetic and real. The debate is warm, not confrontational — food is a safe emotional topic that still generates strong opinions.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Read Bernardo with disgust and drama. Read Ingrid with firm conviction. The contrast generates immediate laughter and reaction.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Testimonial trigger: "One sentence — which of these four describes you at a dinner table with strange food? Be honest."
§ 04 · The Conversation Engine
🗣 10 Questions. 4 Blocks. 23 Minutes.
Personal → Opinion → Hypothetical → Provocative · Point and push · No round-robin
04
23 min
⚡
Rule: At least 2 sentences. Name the food. Describe the taste. Describe your face. That's a complete answer.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • This is the most naturally narrative-friendly session in this lote. B1.1 students love telling food stories — the physical reality of taste and texture gives them concrete vocabulary to work with.
  • The conv-bar rule is key: "Name the food. Describe the taste. Describe your face." This 3-part structure generates longer, richer answers at B1.1.
  • Q9 is playful and self-revealing. Use it as a comedy moment — then make it serious: "But seriously — what does that say about you as a traveler?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • When a student makes a disgusted face while describing food — celebrate it: "That face! Now say that face in English. 'It was disgusting. I wanted to spit it out.' Try it."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • After Q10: "You just told food stories in English — with adjectives, emotions, and facial expressions. That's real communication. Not grammar. Communication."
§ 05 · The Exit Shot
💣 One Question. No Easy Answer.
5 min · No chips · Position + reason · Then the loop
05
5 min
EXIT
💣 Exit Shot
You are at a business dinner with an important client from another country. They order a dish you find disgusting. They order the same thing for you — as a gift, to share their culture. Everyone is watching. Do you eat it?
3 sentences minimum. Yes or no first. Then tell me what goes through your head in that moment.
📌 B1.1 Model Answer — Reveal AFTER student speaks
"Yes, I eat it. At a business dinner, you do not say no to a client's gift — that's a rule I know. I take one bite, I smile, and I say 'this is very interesting.' I don't lie — 'interesting' is true. Then I drink a lot of water and change the subject."
🔁 Retention Loop — WhatsApp Homework "Before next session — record a 60-second voice note in English. Your answer to today's Exit Shot. No preparation. Just talk. Send it to [teacher number]."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The business dinner context adds a professional dimension — it's not just personal preference, it's a career decision. That raises the stakes perfectly for the Exit Shot.
  • Follow-up: "What would you do if you actually felt sick after eating it — in front of the client?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The model answer introduces a great B1.1 social strategy: "'interesting' is true — not a lie." Point it out: "This is a real English skill — the polite word that's still honest. 'Interesting.' Remember that."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Closing: "You just made a professional decision about food — in English. That's not a small thing. That's a real skill you'll use."
