The Best Food I Ever Ate

Close your eyes. Think about the most delicious thing you ever ate. Where were you? What was it?

🪝
The Trigger
Open the Conversation
⏱ 4 min
A simple question for the class
"Close your eyes for 5 seconds. Think about the best food you ever ate in your life. Where were you? Who was with you? Can you still remember the taste?"
🎙 Teacher Script

Actually ask them to close their eyes for 5 seconds. It's fun and it activates the memory. Then open your eyes and ask: "What did you see? What was the food?" Go around the room quickly — just the name of the food, where, who. Then say: "OK — now let's talk about food." High energy, zero intimidation.

The Conversation Engine
23 Minutes of Real Talk
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
What was the best food you ever ate? Where were you and what was it?
The core question of the session. Let students answer with as much or as little detail as they want. Help with adjectives: "Was it sweet, spicy, salty? Hot or cold? Soft or crunchy?" Describing food is great vocabulary practice.
Q2
Did you ever eat something very strange or different on a trip? What was it? Did you like it?
Model answer: "In Veracruz, I ate a seafood soup I never tried before. I was nervous, but it was amazing. I ordered a second bowl." Fun and engaging — students love sharing food adventures.
Q3
When was the last time you ate something really delicious? What was it and who cooked it?
Easy past simple practice. Most students will name a family dish or a recent restaurant visit. Ask follow-up: "Did you finish everything?" or "Did you go back for more?"
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think street food is better than restaurant food — or the opposite?
Ricardo's debate in simple form. No right answer. Help students express their preference: "I prefer... because..." / "For me, the best food is..." Let them argue gently — this topic is always fun.
Q5
Do you like to cook? What do you cook well — or what is terrible when you cook?
Self-deprecating answers are always the most engaging. Model answer: "I'm very good at eggs. But when I try to cook rice, it's always a disaster. I don't know why." Students love laughing at their own kitchen failures.
Q6
Is it important to try the local food when you travel — or is it OK to eat what you know?
Some students always try local food. Others want their comfort food. Both are valid. Model answer: "I always try to eat local food. That's the best part of travel for me. But my husband always looks for a McDonald's. I don't understand him."
Block 3 — Future Plans
Q7
Is there a food from another country you really want to try? What is it?
Easy "want to" / "would like to" practice. Common answers: Japanese sushi, Italian pasta, Thai food, French cheese. Let students dream about food from other places. It's simple vocabulary and very motivating.
Q8
Are you going to cook something special this week or weekend? What are you going to make?
Direct "going to" practice. Keep it real and personal. Even "I'm going to order pizza" is a perfect A2.2 answer — it's honest and uses the target structure.
Block 4 — Used To & Reflection
Q9
Did you use to eat differently when you were a child? What did you love then that you don't eat now?
Fun "used to" practice. Model answer: "I used to love sweet cereals for breakfast. Now I can't eat that — too much sugar. But I miss them a little." Almost everyone has a childhood food they abandoned as an adult.
Q10
Did you use to dislike a food when you were young — but now you love it? What changed?
Great closing question. Model answer: "I used to hate vegetables — all of them! Now I love salads. I don't know when that happened. Getting old, I think." Light and fun — great energy for session close.

The Exit Shot
One Question. Simple but Real.
⏱ 5 min
🎯 Final Question
If you could eat only one dish for the rest of your life — breakfast, lunch, and dinner, every single day — what would you choose? And why that one?
🎙️
Voice Note Homework
Record a 60-second voice note in English on WhatsApp. Answer this question: what is your one dish — and why? Describe the food: what does it taste like? Who makes it? When do you eat it? Your teacher will listen and give you feedback before the next session.
