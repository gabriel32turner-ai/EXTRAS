Overtourism:
When Cities Hate Tourists
Barcelona sprays tourists with water guns. Venice charges entry fees. Amsterdam bans new hotels. At what point does a visitor become an invader?
🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocation & Opening
⏱ 4 min
The Guardian · 2024
"Residents of Barcelona, Venice, and Kyoto have taken to the streets protesting tourists — not because they hate visitors, but because they can no longer afford to live in the cities they grew up in. The tourism industry generated $9.5 trillion in 2023. Local residents saw almost none of it."
Teacher script: Read it. Then: "Have you ever been to a place that felt completely overwhelmed by tourists — including you being one of them? How did it feel?" Pause. Then: "Today we're asking a question that gets uncomfortable fast: when does tourism stop being a cultural exchange and start being a form of colonization?"

Isabella · 34
Travel Blogger · CDMX
Travel is one of the most transformative things a human being can do. Restricting it based on income or nationality is elitism dressed up as environmentalism. The problem isn't tourists — it's poor urban planning and governments that took the money for decades without investing it back into local communities. Don't blame the visitor. Blame the system.

Architect · Barcelona (resident)
I was born in the Barceloneta neighborhood. My family has lived there for three generations. Today I can't afford to rent in my own city because Airbnb turned every apartment into a hotel. My neighbors are gone. The local market is a souvenir shop. My city has become a theme park — and I can't afford the ticket. Tell me again how this is good for locals.

Alejandro · 28
Economist · Monterrey
The anti-tourism movement is mostly led by wealthy Europeans who are angry that their vacation spots are now accessible to the middle class. When only rich people traveled, nobody called it "overtourism." Now that budget airlines and Airbnb exist, suddenly everyone's culture is being "destroyed." The destinations haven't changed — just the economic class of the people visiting them.
🌿
Daniela · 41
Sustainable Tourism Consultant · Oaxaca
The solution isn't no tourism — it's better tourism. Oaxaca has found a real balance: cap visitor numbers in sensitive areas, invest tourism revenue directly in local infrastructure, and make sure the money actually reaches the community. The problem is never tourism itself — it's extractive tourism that takes everything and leaves nothing. There's a better model. We just don't choose it often enough.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever visited a place that was so crowded with tourists that it ruined the experience? Where was it — and what did you do?
Teacher Tip Push for vivid detail: "Was it a beach? A ruin? A neighborhood? What specifically made it feel ruined — the crowds, the noise, the prices, the lack of locals?" Rich storytelling vocabulary opportunity here.
Q2
Have you ever felt like a tourist somewhere and been aware that your presence was part of the problem — that you were contributing to exactly what you disliked?
Model Answer "Yes. I went to Tulum a few years ago and I realized that every Instagram photo I took, every overpriced restaurant I ate at, was part of a machine that was pushing local families further and further from the coast they grew up on. I felt complicit — and I went anyway."
Q3
Has a place in Mexico that you loved changed significantly because of tourism — either positively or negatively?
Teacher Tip Great for local knowledge and real context. Likely examples: Tulum, Oaxaca, San Miguel de Allende, Sayulita, Bacalar. Let them lead the topic — it will be more authentic than any example you could give.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Should cities have the right to limit the number of tourists who can visit — the way national parks cap visitors? Or does that go too far?
Teacher Tip Push them to commit: "If yes — who decides the cap? Based on what criteria? If no — then what's the alternative when a city is genuinely overwhelmed?" No easy middle ground allowed.
Q5
Is Airbnb good or bad for cities? Can you defend both sides?
Model Answer "Good: it gives homeowners income, makes travel more affordable, and offers cultural immersion that hotels don't. Bad: it turns residential neighborhoods into hotel districts, drives up rents, and pushes out the people who made the neighborhood worth visiting in the first place. Both are true simultaneously."
Q6
Do you think there's a difference between being a traveler and being a tourist? Or is that distinction just snobbery?
Teacher Tip Classic travel debate. The "I'm a traveler, not a tourist" identity is common among educated professionals. Push: "If everyone who goes somewhere believes they're doing it the right way — who is actually causing the problem?"
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If your city — Celaya, Monterrey, Guadalajara, wherever you're from — became as popular as Tulum or Oaxaca overnight, what would you gain and what would you lose?
Model Answer "I'd gain better restaurants, better infrastructure, more international connections. I'd lose the price of my apartment, the ability to park anywhere, the sense that the city is actually mine. I'm not sure the trade is worth it — but I'm also not the one who gets to decide."
Q8
If you found out that a place you love and visit regularly was planning to charge a $500 peso entry fee to limit visitors — would you still go? Would you think the fee was fair?
Teacher Tip The personal cost question changes abstract opinions fast. "Does the fee feel fair if it goes directly to local communities? Does it feel different if it just goes to the government? What if only people with lower incomes were priced out?"
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Have you ever posted a photo of a beautiful place on social media — knowing it was already overcrowded — without thinking about what that post would do?
Teacher Tip The personal complicity question. Almost everyone has. "Were you aware at the time? Do you feel any responsibility for the places you promote online? Should you?" Don't let them off the hook with "it was just one post."
Q10
If travel is a privilege — not a right — should people be required to demonstrate some level of cultural awareness or responsibility before visiting sensitive destinations?
Model Answer "Part of me says yes — especially for culturally or ecologically fragile places. But then I have to ask: who decides the criteria? And who enforces it? The moment you start gatekeeping travel, you are making a political statement about whose movement is more legitimate than someone else's. That's a door I'm not sure we want to open."

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · Final Dilemma · No right answer
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot
You discover that the small coastal town you've visited every summer for 10 years — the one your family loves, with the restaurant where everyone knows your name — has voted to restrict tourist access. New rules: maximum 200 visitors per day, no new vacation rentals, entry fee of $800 pesos per person. Your family of 4 can still afford to go. But the friends you've always gone with — a family of 4 with a tighter budget — probably can't anymore. Do you still go?
📱 WhatsApp Homework Record 60 seconds in English: Do you go — and what does your answer say about how you really think about access, privilege, and the places you love?
