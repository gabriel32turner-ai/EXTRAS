Meetings Are
a Waste of Time

Two hours. Fifteen people. One decision. Was that really necessary?

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
Harvard Business Review
"The average office worker spends 31 hours per month in meetings that they consider unproductive. That's almost one full week of work — every month — doing nothing useful."
Teacher script: Read the stat. Then: "31 hours. That's one week every month. Think about your last meeting at work. Was it useful? Or was it a waste of time?" Wait for two quick reactions. Then: "OK — tell me about your worst meeting. How long was it? How many people? What happened?"

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Past Experience
Q1
What was your worst meeting at work? How long was it? How many people were there?
Teacher tip: Help with past simple: "The meeting was..." / "There were [number] people..." / "It lasted [time]..." Push for the specific moment that made it terrible: "What was the worst part?"
Q2
Did you ever fall asleep, look at your phone, or think about other things in a meeting? What happened?
Model answer: "Yes! Last month in a very long meeting. I looked at my phone under the table. My boss saw me and asked me a question. I didn't know the answer. It was very embarrassing. I said sorry."
Q3
Did you ever have a meeting that was actually useful and important? What did you decide in that meeting?
Teacher tip: Flip the question — explore the positive side. Push: "Why was that meeting different? What did the boss do? How long was it?" Great for comparing what makes a meeting good vs. bad.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
What is the maximum time for a good meeting — 30 minutes, 1 hour, or 2 hours?
Teacher tip: Force a specific number. Most will say 30 minutes or less. Ask: "Why 30 minutes? What can you do in 30 minutes that you can't do in 15?"
Q5
Do you think it's OK to look at your phone in a meeting?
Model answer: "I think it depends. If the meeting is important and I am part of it — no, I don't look at my phone. But if I am in a meeting and nobody needs me — yes, I look. My time is also valuable."
Q6
Is it better to have a meeting in person, or is it the same on Zoom or Teams?
Teacher tip: Great comparison question. Push: "What can you do in person that you can't do online? What is easier online?" Most will say in person is better but more tiring.
Block 3 — Future Plans
Q7
Are you going to have a meeting this week? What is it going to be about?
Teacher tip: Direct "going to" practice with real immediate content. Follow up: "Is it going to be useful? How long is it going to be?"
Q8
If you are a manager in the future, how are you going to run your meetings differently?
Model answer: "I'm going to have a maximum of 30 minutes. I'm going to send the topic before the meeting — by email. And I'm not going to invite people who don't need to be there. Only the people who are really necessary."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — do YOU talk too much in meetings, or do you usually stay quiet?
Teacher tip: Most will say "quiet." Ask: "Why? Are you quiet because you don't have anything to say — or because you feel uncomfortable speaking in front of people?" Very relatable at this level.
Q10
Your boss says: "We are going to have a meeting every day at 7 AM for 30 minutes." Is that good news or terrible news?
Teacher tip: Fun scenario. Most will say terrible — 7 AM! Push: "What are you going to do? Are you going to complain? Are you going to come every day? Or are you going to look for a new job?"

Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
Your boss calls a 3-hour meeting for tomorrow morning. 15 people are going to be there. You know from experience that nothing is going to happen in this meeting. What are you going to do?
