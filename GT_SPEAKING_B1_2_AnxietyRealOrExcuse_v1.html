§ 01 · The Trigger
🪝 The Numbers We Don't Talk About
Read aloud or project · First reaction only · No debate yet · 4 min max
01
4 min
⚠️ Sensitivity Note — Read Before Starting This topic may be personally relevant to some students. The goal is a professional, honest conversation — not therapy. If a student shares something deeply personal, acknowledge it briefly and redirect to the language: "That takes courage to say in English. Can you say that again in a full sentence?" Never probe for clinical details. If anyone seems distressed, end the topic and check in privately after class.
🧠
WHO Global Mental Health Report + APA Stress in America Survey 2023
1 in 4
Adults worldwide experience an anxiety disorder at some point in their life — making it the most common mental health condition globally
264M
People globally diagnosed with anxiety disorders — more than the entire population of Brazil
56%
Of Mexican adults report feeling anxious "often" or "always" — but fewer than 1 in 10 have ever spoken to a mental health professional
3×
More likely: younger workers (under 35) report clinical anxiety at 3 times the rate of workers over 55 — and are 3 times more likely to call it a reason to miss work
Sources: WHO World Mental Health Report 2022 · APA Stress in America 2023 · INEGI Encuesta Nacional de Bienestar Mental · Harvard Business Review "The Anxiety Economy" (2023)
🪝 Opening Trigger — Read this, then point at someone
"A 28-year-old engineer calls in sick on Monday. She tells her boss she has anxiety and cannot come in. Her boss — 55 years old — thinks: 'We all have anxiety. I had it at 28 too. I came in anyway.' Who is right?"
Teacher Mechanics — 4 min Read the scenario once, slowly. Point directly: "First reaction — who is right?" Don't express your opinion. Wait for the tension. After 2–3 responses, say: "Hold every opinion you have. They all come back in §4."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The generational tension in the trigger (28-year-old vs 55-year-old boss) is intentional — it maps directly onto most corporate groups where there is an age gap between junior and senior employees.
  • The 56% Mexico stat is the most relevant — most students will recognize it immediately. Use it: "More than half of Mexican adults feel anxious often or always. How many of you would say that's true for you?"
  • If a student has personal experience with anxiety — let them speak briefly, then redirect to the language. Never ask for clinical details.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The "spicy" label is earned by the core question: is anxiety a real medical condition or a cultural trend? Both positions have defenders in the room — usually across generational lines. That friction is the engine.
  • Best activation if the group is cautious: "Nobody is diagnosing anyone here. We're talking about a word that has changed meaning in the last 10 years. That's a language and culture conversation."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Retention: "Being able to talk about mental health in English — clearly, professionally, without drama — is a real skill. HR managers at international companies talk about this all the time."

💡 10 Nouns — Mental Health, Stress & Work
🔵 10 Verbs — How People Deal (or Don't)
⚡ Production Challenge — No preparation
Each person picks 3 words. One sentence each about something you have experienced personally — at work or in life. No writing. Just talk.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • "Anxiety" vs "stress" — clarify the distinction: stress usually has a clear cause (a deadline, a meeting); anxiety is often more general, sometimes without a specific trigger. The distinction matters for §4.
  • "Therapist" — some students may not know this word or may confuse it with "psychiatrist." Clarify: a therapist or psychologist talks with you; a psychiatrist can also prescribe medication.
  • Best production challenge trigger: "Which 3 words describe how you feel at work on a typical Monday morning?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "These words appear in every HR conversation about employee wellbeing at international companies. If you work with American or European colleagues, you will hear these words in meetings."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Knowing how to talk about mental health professionally — in English — is a competitive advantage. Very few people can do this clearly, even in their first language."
§ 03 · Opinion Wall
🧱 4 Real Voices. 4 Different Generations.
Read each card · Pairs discuss 90 sec · Group shares · Same question each time
03
10 min
Processing Question — Same for all 4 voices
"Who do you agree with most — and who would your parents agree with? Why?"
Don't vary the question
🔁 Processing Question — Pairs → Group
"Who do you agree with most — and who would your parents agree with? Why?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The processing question ("who would your parents agree with?") is intentionally cross-generational — it creates distance from personal identification and makes the debate easier to have without defensiveness.
  • The provocateur is Don Ernesto. His position will generate the most friction with younger students — but many older students in the group may quietly agree with him. That split is the best possible debate fuel.
  • Valentina and Alejandro represent the two poles of the pro-mental-health position — one personal, one professional. Mariana is the nuanced middle ground.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Best activation: ask before reading — "Quick show of hands: who has ever told their boss they were stressed? Who has ever called in sick because of a mental health day?" The gap between hands tells you everything about the group's starting point.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Testimonial trigger: "In one sentence — which of these four sounds most like someone you have worked with? Not you. Someone you know."
§ 04 · The Conversation Engine
🗣 10 Questions. 4 Blocks. 23 Minutes.
Personal → Opinion → Hypothetical → Provocative · No round-robin · Point and push
04
23 min
⚡
Rule: Minimum 2 sentences per answer. Position first. Real example second. Respectful but honest — this is a hard topic.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Monitor the emotional temperature throughout — this topic can go deep quickly. If a student becomes visibly emotional, slow down: acknowledge briefly, then redirect gently to the language dimension.
  • Q9 (the most personal) — give it the most time and silence. Don't rush to Q10 if Q9 generates a real conversation.
  • Block 3 second conditional: "If anxiety were more accepted at work..." — watch for "If anxiety would be accepted." Note and recycle at end only.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • When vocab from §2 appears naturally — especially "overwhelmed," "cope," or "trigger" — celebrate it: "You just used a word from today's list in a real sentence about a real experience. That's B1.2."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • After Q10: "You just had a conversation that most people avoid — even in Spanish. You had it in English. That is a real skill, and it's one that makes you stand out professionally."
  • Upsell: "The next Speaking Lab in this series looks at toxic positivity — when 'just be positive' becomes its own problem. Same depth. Same honesty."
§ 05 · The Exit Shot
💣 One Question. No Easy Answer.
5 min · No chips · Just position + reason · Then the loop
05
5 min
EXIT
💣 Exit Shot
A colleague on your team tells you they have anxiety and asks if they can skip the next all-hands presentation because speaking in front of people makes them feel physically sick. You are the team lead. If they skip, you have to present their part. You already have too much work. What do you say?
Answer in 3 sentences. Be real — not HR-correct. What do you actually say to this person, and what do you think privately?
📌 B1.2 Model Answer — Reveal AFTER student speaks
"I would say yes — they can skip it this time. But privately, I'd feel frustrated. Not because I don't believe anxiety is real — I do — but because I'm already overwhelmed and now I have to cover for them with no warning. What I wish I could say is: 'I support you, and I also need support right now.' But most workplaces don't have space for that conversation."
🔁 Retention Loop — The WhatsApp Homework "Before our next session — record a 60-second voice note in English. Your answer to today's Exit Shot. Don't write it first. Don't rehearse. Just talk. Send it to [teacher number]."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The "HR-correct vs what you actually think" framing is essential — it gives students permission to be honest rather than performing empathy. The gap between the two answers is the most interesting moment of the session.
  • Follow-up only: "What would you do if this happened again next month — same colleague, same request?" Second conditional activates naturally.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The model answer validates both positions — empathy AND frustration — without resolving the tension. That honesty is what B1.2 sounds like: nuanced, personal, real. Point it out.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Closing: "What you said in the last 5 minutes — in English — is something most people can't say clearly even in their first language. That's what this program builds."
  • Referral trigger: "If a colleague is taking a traditional English class doing grammar exercises and can't have this conversation — that's the difference between their program and this one."
