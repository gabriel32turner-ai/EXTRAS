🪝 Phase 1 — The Trigger
The Headline
Read it aloud. Then ask the first question.
4 min
01
BBC Health
"Experts say it takes an average of 66 days to form a new habit. Breaking a bad one takes much longer — and most people fail at least 8 times before they succeed."
— Which means every time you've tried and failed, you were statistically on track
The Guardian · Psychology
"The most common bad habits people report: checking their phone constantly, eating late at night, procrastinating, and going to bed too late. Sound familiar?"
— If you said yes to all four — welcome to the club. You're in the majority.
Launch line: "Quick question — what time did you go to bed last night? And what time did you want to go to bed?" [Let them answer.] "That gap — that's a bad habit. Today we talk about the things we do that we know we shouldn't. No judgment. Just honesty."

5 Colloquial Phrases — Real Habit Talk
Things people actually say · Click to hear
Colloquial
"Just one more."
Said before every bad decision — one more episode, one more cookie, one more drink
"I'll start on Monday."
The most repeated sentence in the history of bad habits — said on Tuesday
"I deserve this."
The most effective excuse for any bad habit — usually said after a hard day
"I know, I know."
What you say when someone points out your bad habit — and you have no defense

The classic self-deception — said about something that is, in fact, pretty bad
After phrases, ask: "Which of these sentences did you say this week?" Most students laugh and admit to at least two. Use that moment of recognition to launch the Opinion Wall: "Okay — let's see what other people think about bad habits."

Provocateur rationale: Fernando eliminates the concept of habit as something beyond our control — reducing it to simple choice and responsibility. Facilitation move: After the student picks their opinion, ask: "And Fernando — is he 100% wrong? Or does he have even a small point?" Don't let them dismiss him without engaging.
🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Personal and honest. Show tips only after the student answers.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
What is your worst bad habit right now? Be honest — the one you know you should stop but don't.
The direct opener. Most B1.1 students enjoy this because it's personal and there's no wrong answer. Common answers: phone, late nights, junk food, procrastination. Model: "My worst habit is looking at my phone in bed. I know it affects my sleep. I do it anyway." Activate: habit, screen, stress, trigger.
Q2
Have you ever successfully stopped a bad habit? How long did it take — and did it come back?
Present perfect + past simple combination. Model: "Yes — I stopped drinking soda for 6 months. Then I had one at a birthday party. And then I was back to one a day." The relapse story is very common and very B1.1 appropriate. Activate: give up, try again, willpower, promise.
Q3
Have you ever had a bad habit that affected someone else — your partner, your family, your coworkers?
This adds a social dimension — bad habits as a relationship issue. Common: coming home late, being on the phone at dinner, procrastinating on shared tasks. Model: "Yes — I used to complain all the time at home. My partner finally said: 'Please stop. You're making everything feel negative.'" Activate: complain, admit, stress.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think bad habits are a choice — or do some people really can't control them?
Fernando's position. Most B1.1 students will give a nuanced answer: "Some are a choice, some are not." That's the right answer — push for examples. "Which of your bad habits do you think is a choice — and which one do you really struggle to control?" Activate: willpower, temptation, admit.
Q5
Is it OK to have bad habits — as long as they only affect you?
Simple, direct opinion. Diego's position. Most say yes. Push: "But what about your health, your energy, your mood? Don't those affect other people too?" Good for modals: "You should be able to... but maybe you shouldn't..." Activate: excuse, stress, trigger.
Q6
Do you think phones and social media have created new bad habits that didn't exist before — or were we always like this?
Light societal question — B1.1 friendly. Most students find this easy and opinionated. Model: "I think phones made everything worse. Before, if you were bored, you had to sit with it. Now you just scroll. The boredom was actually useful." Activate: scroll, screen, habit, trigger.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your doctor told you to stop one habit immediately — or face serious health problems?
Second conditional with real stakes. Most say they would stop — but push: "And would you? Really? Or would you try for two weeks and then go back?" Some students are more honest than others. The honest ones produce the best conversation. Activate: stress, give up, try again, willpower.
Q8
What would you do if a good friend told you: "Your habit is affecting our friendship — I can't keep doing this with you"?
Emotional and personal. What's the habit? Complaining, being late, canceling plans, being on the phone? The friend as a mirror — would that be enough to change? Model: "If my best friend said that to me, I think I would really try. Because it's easier to fail yourself than to fail someone who loves you." Activate: promise, admit, try again.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
What excuse do you use most for your bad habits? "I'm stressed," "I deserve it," "just this once," or something else? Be honest.
Very personal and a little funny. Students love this because everyone recognizes their own excuse. Give them a moment to think before answering. Model: "My excuse is always 'I'm stressed.' And it's true — but it's also just an excuse." Activate: excuse, stress, temptation, deserve.
Q10
If someone who loves you could see all your bad habits clearly — what do you think they would ask you to change first?
The closing self-reflection. Gentle but personal — asks them to see themselves through someone else's eyes. Most students already know the answer. Give them space. The silence before they answer is part of the practice. Activate: admit, promise, habit, stress.
B1.1 grammar check: Q2 = present perfect + past simple. Q7–Q8 = second conditional. Keep the conditionals simple: "What would you do if...? / I would..." If students say "I will stop" instead of "I would stop" — model once and move on.

Energy note: This is a high-laughing-per-minute topic for B1.1. The honesty is funny and universal. Keep the energy up in Blocks 1–2 and get quieter for Q9 and Q10.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. One honest answer.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
Your partner, your family, or your best friend — the person who knows you best — makes you a deal: "If you stop [your worst habit] for 30 days, I will do one thing you've always wanted me to do." They name your habit. They name their thing. Is that enough for you to stop?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Tell me your worst bad habit. When did it start? Have you ever tried to stop? What happened?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: The Exit Shot uses the one person who knows the student best as the mirror — and turns change into a relationship exchange. The "they name your habit" detail is the uncomfortable part. Most students feel a small jolt of recognition: "Oh no, I know exactly which habit they'd pick."

Homework note: The recording prompt is a storytelling task in three simple past + present perfect steps. Very natural for B1.1. Almost everyone can speak for 60 seconds about their own bad habits.

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about the things we enjoy but feel a little guilty about — our guilty pleasures. A lighter version of today's topic, but just as honest."
