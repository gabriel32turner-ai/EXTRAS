I Got Scammed Once

Provocateur rationale: Mateo places partial blame on the victim — specifically targeting greed as the scammer's tool. This is uncomfortable because it's partly true and feels unfair. Students who were scammed will feel defensive. Students who have never been scammed may secretly agree. Both reactions produce good conversation.

Facilitation move: After they pick their opinion, ask: "And what do you think about Mateo's? Is he wrong — or is he at least a little bit right?" Don't let them dismiss him too quickly.
🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Personal stories first. Opinions second. Show tips only after the student answers.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever been scammed — online, in a store, or by a person you knew? What happened?
The big opener. At B1.1, don't worry if the story is short — the vocabulary is the practice. Model: "Yes — I bought concert tickets online and they were fake. I arrived and they said my tickets didn't exist. I lost 800 pesos and felt terrible." Activate: scam, lose, trust, fall for.
Q2
Did you ever see a scam coming — and ignore the warning signs anyway? Why?
This is the hardest question to answer honestly. Most people have ignored a warning sign because they wanted the deal to be real. Model: "Yes — the price was too cheap. But I really wanted it. So I convinced myself it was okay." Activate: warning sign, ignore, deal, too good to be true.
Q3
Have you ever lent money to a friend or family member — and they never paid you back? Was it a scam, or just a bad situation?
Like Ricardo's story — the scam inside your own life. Many students have this experience. The interesting part: is not paying back a loan the same as stealing? Let them decide. Activate: trust, promise, regret, lie.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think it's the victim's fault when they get scammed? Even a little bit?
Mateo's argument. Most B1.1 students will say "no, never" — push them: "What if the person really wanted something for nothing? What if the price was obviously too cheap?" A nuanced answer is fine: "Maybe a little — but scammers are professionals. It's not fair to blame the victim." Activate: victim, trick, warning sign.
Q5
Is it OK to not pay someone back if you really don't have the money? Or is that the same as stealing?
Simple, direct opinion question. Most say "it depends" — push for specifics: "What if it's your best friend? What if it's $500 pesos? What if it's $5,000?" Use modals: "You should tell them. You shouldn't just disappear." Activate: promise, trust, lie, regret.
Q6
Do you believe in buying things from strangers online? Or do you always go to a real store?
Light and practical. Most students have bought online — and most have had at least one bad experience. Good for present perfect: "Have you ever bought something online that was fake or never arrived?" Activate: trust, deal, warning sign, scam.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if a stranger called you and said you won a prize — but you had to pay a small fee first to get it?
Second conditional, very B1.1 friendly. Almost everyone knows this is a scam — but the interesting question is HOW they would respond. Model: "I would hang up immediately. But I'd also feel a small part of me wondering: what if it's real?" Activate: trick, fall for, scam, too good to be true.
Q8
What would you do if you found out your best friend lied to you about money — maybe said they were broke but they weren't?
Personal and emotional. This question is about betrayal, not just money. Model: "I would be more hurt by the lie than by the money. I could forgive money problems. I'm not sure I could forgive the dishonesty." Activate: lie, trust, regret, promise.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Have you ever done something a little dishonest with money — kept change that wasn't yours, didn't pay for something small, said you lost something to get money back? Be honest.
The self-reflection question. Very B1.1 — keeps it simple and personal. Most people have a small story here. The point is not to judge — it's to recognize that the line between "scammer" and "regular person" can be thinner than we think. Give them space. Don't rush.
Q10
After being scammed or tricked — how long does it take you to trust again? Does it change you permanently?
The closing self-reflection. Simple but emotionally deep for B1.1. Model: "I think I became more careful — maybe too careful sometimes. I don't trust as easily now. I'm not sure if that's good or bad." Activate: trust, regret, warning sign, never again.
B1.1 grammar check: Q1–Q3 = present perfect and past simple. If students say "I scammed once" instead of "I was scammed" or "I got scammed" — model it naturally and move on. Q7–Q8 = second conditional. Keep it simple: "What would you do if...?" / "I would..."

Energy note: This topic can generate strong emotions for students who have had real losses. If a student shares something that feels painful, slow down. Acknowledge it. Don't rush to the next question.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. Personal. No wrong answer.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
Someone offers you a business opportunity: you invest $10,000 pesos, and in 3 months you get $25,000 back. The person is a friend of a friend — not a stranger. The numbers sound good. The person seems honest. Do you do it?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Tell me about a time someone tried to trick you — or a time you almost made a bad decision with money. What happened? What did you do?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: The scenario is the classic "friend of a friend" scam setup — one of the most common in Mexico. The numbers are tempting but not absurd. The "seems honest" detail removes the obvious red flag. Many students will recognize this from real life.

Homework note: The recording prompt is a storytelling task — exactly what B1.1 students need. Past simple narrative practice in a real emotional context. Almost everyone has a story.

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about money, debt, and financial stress — the conversations we avoid having. The English you'll need when money becomes a real topic at work or in your personal life."
