Morning Person
or Night Person?
What time do you wake up? What time do you go to bed? What does that say about you?
A2.1Daily Life & Lifestyle🟡 Medium50 min
🪝 Trigger 💉 Vocab 🧱 Opinions 🗣 Conversation 💣 Exit Shot
🪝 Phase 1 — The Trigger
The Question
Read it. Then ask the student.
4 min
01
A simple fact
"Scientists say about 25% of people are true morning people, 25% are true night people, and 50% are somewhere in the middle. But 90% of jobs start at 9am or earlier."
— Most night people wake up early every day — against their biology. No wonder they're tired.
The question for today
"What time did you wake up today? Was that YOUR choice — or the alarm clock's?"
— There's a difference between waking up and GETTING up. Let's talk about mornings and nights.
Launch line: "Quick question — morning person or night person? Just one answer." [Student answers.] "What time do you normally wake up? And what time do you go to bed?" [Two quick answers.] "Okay — today we talk about sleep, mornings, and energy. I think you have strong opinions about this." This topic always generates immediate, personal responses.

🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Short answers are fine. Honest answers are better. Show tips after the student responds.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
What time did you wake up today? Was it easy or difficult to get up?
Past simple — concrete and personal. Model: "I woke up at 6:45. It was terrible. My alarm rang three times. I pressed snooze twice. Then I rushed. I almost didn't eat breakfast." Three simple past sentences. Very accessible for A2.1.
Q2
When was the last time you slept really well? What was different that night?
Past simple — positive sleep memory. Model: "Last Saturday. I didn't set an alarm. I woke up at 9am. I felt amazing. I read in bed for 30 minutes. I wasn't in a rush. It was perfect." The contrast with everyday life is the point. Activate: alarm, rush, energy.
Q3
Did you ever have a problem at work because you were late or very tired in the morning?
Past simple — a real consequence story. Model: "Yes. Two months ago I was late to an important meeting. My boss was angry. I didn't sleep well the night before. It was a bad day." Activate: traffic, rush, mood, schedule.
Block 2 — Opinion (Simple Present)
Q4
Are you a morning person or a night person? What do you think is better?
Core opinion question. Model: "I'm a night person. I think mornings are terrible. But I work in the morning, so it doesn't matter what I think." Or: "I'm a morning person. I love the quiet. I think morning people are more productive." Both are great A2.1 opinions.
Q5
Do you think you need coffee or breakfast to function in the morning? Or can you start without it?
Fun, personal opinion. Model: "I need coffee. Without coffee, I can't think. I can't speak. I can't be nice to people. Coffee is not optional. It's medicine." Or: "I don't drink coffee. I just need water and 10 minutes of quiet." Activate "I need my coffee first."
Q6
Is it okay for a company to start work at 7am — or do you think that's too early?
Opinion about work schedule. Fernando vs. Diego territory. Model: "I think 7am is too early. The human body isn't ready. You don't work well when you're half asleep. 9am is better." Or: "I don't mind. I start at 7:30. You adapt. It's okay." Both are natural A2.1 responses.
Block 3 — Future Plans (Going to / Will)
Q7
Are you going to change your sleep schedule? What are you going to do differently?
Going to — personal future plan. Model: "I'm going to try to sleep earlier. I'm going to stop looking at my phone in bed. I'm going to drink less coffee at night." Three "going to" sentences — excellent A2.1 production. Or: "No. I'm not going to change. I'm a night person. I accept it." Both are honest.
Q8
What will you do tomorrow morning to start the day better?
Will — tomorrow plan. Very concrete. Model: "Tomorrow I will wake up 15 minutes earlier. I will eat breakfast. I will not rush. I will listen to music in the car instead of thinking about work." Or: "I will do the same as always. Set the alarm. Press snooze. Rush. Nothing changes." Both are honest and natural.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — what time do you really want to wake up? Not your alarm time. YOUR ideal time.
Self-reflection — simple and very personal. The gap between ideal and real is the interesting part. Model: "My ideal time? 9am. Slowly. With no alarm. Coffee first, then emails. But my job starts at 8. So... no." Honest and a little funny. Give space for the answer.
Q10
Do you think your mood in the morning affects the rest of your day? Give me an example.
Provocative self-reflection with a story. Model: "Yes. When I wake up late and rush, I feel angry all morning. Last Tuesday, I was late. I arrived to work in a bad mood. I was rude to a colleague. It was not her fault. It was the morning." Activate: mood, rush, angry, concentrate.
A2.1 grammar check: Past simple (Q1-3), simple present (Q4-6), going to/will (Q7-8), simple present + past (Q9-10).

Energy note: This topic generates immediate, enthusiastic responses at A2.1 because everyone has a strong, lived opinion about mornings. Keep a conversational, warm tone — laugh about shared morning struggles. Diego types and Fernando types usually create natural "debate" energy that drives the whole session.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One scenario. One honest answer.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
Your company is going to change the work schedule. Starting next month, everyone is going to start at 7am and finish at 4pm. No exceptions. You are a night person. Your commute is 45 minutes. What are you going to do? Are you going to accept it — or are you going to talk to your boss?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Tell me about your mornings. Are you a morning person or a night person? What time do you wake up? What do you do first? And what is the hardest part of your morning?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: Concrete workplace scenario using "going to" twice. The commute detail (45 min) makes it more specific and harder to ignore. For night people, this scenario is personally painful — they imagine it immediately. "Accept it or talk to your boss?" forces a decision and uses target grammar naturally.

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about bad habits you can't change. Very similar energy — personal, honest, a little funny. I think you'll enjoy it."
