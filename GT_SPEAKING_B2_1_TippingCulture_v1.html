Tipping Culture:
Generous or Exploitative?
Should customers be responsible for paying workers a living wage? Or has tipping become a system that benefits nobody except the people who designed it?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
A real headline. A real debate.
⏱ 4 min
Forbes · 2024
"Tip fatigue is real. Americans are now being asked to tip for everything from coffee to self-checkout kiosks. 72% say they feel pressured to tip even when service doesn't warrant it — and 41% say it's making them go out less often."
Teacher Script
"Last time you paid a bill at a restaurant — did you feel comfortable choosing your own tip amount? Or did the screen make you feel guilty? Today we're looking at tipping: is it a beautiful act of generosity, or is it a broken system that lets employers off the hook — and puts customers in an uncomfortable position?"

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real talk. No right answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Think about the last time you left a tip — or didn't. What was going through your mind? Was it automatic, or did you actually think about it?
The automatic vs. deliberate distinction is interesting. Most people tip automatically — which suggests it's guilt, not gratitude. Push: "When was the last time you thought: this service genuinely deserves extra?" Rarity of that thought says something.
Q2
Have you ever felt pressured to tip — at a coffee shop, a food truck, or somewhere that surprised you? What did you do?
The tablet-rotating moment is universally recognized. Ask what they actually pressed — and whether they'd have made the same choice without the screen. The gap between those two answers is the whole conversation.
Q3
Have you or someone you know ever worked a job where tips were part of the income? What was that experience like?
Personal connection to the worker side. Dramatically shifts the conversation if they've experienced it. If not: "Imagine your salary depended entirely on whether strangers were in a good mood that day. How would that feel?" Good empathy builder.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Is it the customer's responsibility to make sure service workers earn enough to live on — or is that entirely the employer's job?
Model answer: "Morally, it's clearly the employer's job. But the system was designed to put that burden on the customer — and workers became dependent on it before anyone asked their opinion. Now we can't just stop tipping without hurting the people we're trying to help."
Q5
Should tipping be abolished and replaced with higher menu prices that include a fair wage? Would you pay more if it meant servers earned a stable income?
The rational solution — but it's failed in practice multiple times. Several US restaurants tried it and customers complained about high prices. The psychology: a $25 entree plus no tip feels expensive. A $20 entree plus $5 tip feels normal. Same math, different feeling. Why?
Q6
Renata says tipping for bad service removes the only power customers have. Do you agree — or is withholding a tip just punishing the wrong person?
The accountability vs. solidarity question. Bad service is often caused by management failure, understaffing, or a terrible kitchen — not the server. So who's actually responsible? Push them to locate the problem precisely before they locate the punishment.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
If you found out that a restaurant had raised its prices by 20% and completely eliminated tipping — paying all staff a fair guaranteed wage — would you go there more, less, or the same?
Most people say more — then pause and admit they might actually feel something is missing. The control element of tipping matters to some people. Follow up: "Would you trust that the service would still be good without the incentive?"
Q8
If you traveled to Japan — where tipping is considered rude and insulting — and had exceptional service, would you tip anyway? Why or why not?
The cultural relativism question. Some people tip in Japan out of habit and genuinely offend workers. The impulse reveals whether tipping is about the worker or about the tipper's identity and comfort. Great moment for self-reflection without judgment.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
When you tip generously, is it really about the worker — or is it partly about how it makes you feel about yourself?
The ego question. Nobody tips purely for the worker. There's always a self-image component: being seen as generous, being a "good person," avoiding social shame. That's not wrong — it's human. But naming it changes the conversation from moral to psychological.
Q10
If tipping was always private — no screen, no worker watching, just a sealed envelope left on the table after you left — would you tip the same amount you usually do?
The closer. The honest answer separates gratitude from performance. Most people tip less when unobserved — not because they're bad people, but because social pressure was doing most of the work. If they say "the same" — ask them to be sure. Then ask why they're sure.
