The Last Photo
on My Phone
Look at the last photo on your phone right now. That photo tells a story. Today — you tell it in English.
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
A real fact. A real conversation.
⏱ 4 min
Google Photos · 2024
"The average person takes 15 photos every day. But they look at old photos only 2 times per year. We photograph everything — and we remember almost nothing."
Teacher Script
"Okay. I want you to take out your phone. Go to your photos. Look at the last photo you took. Don't change it — the real last one. Now: what is in that photo? Who took it? Why? You have 30 seconds to look at it. Then you are going to tell me the story. Ready?"

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real stories. No wrong answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
Look at the last photo on your phone right now. What is in the photo? Who took it and why?
THE signature activity of this class. Let every student open their phone and show the real last photo. No preparation — that's the point. Food photos, screenshots, memes, kids, documents — everything is valid and everything is interesting. "Tell me the story of this photo in English. 3 sentences."
Q2
What was the last photo you deleted from your phone? Why did you delete it?
Funny and revealing. "I deleted a photo of my ex." "I deleted a bad selfie." "I deleted 500 photos because my storage was full — I didn't look at any of them." The reason for deleting often tells you more than the reason for keeping. Great past simple practice.
Q3
Did you take a photo of something important or funny last week? What was it?
Opens the conversation beyond just the last photo. Push for the story behind the photo, not just the description. "What happened before the photo? What happened after?" That narrative structure is exactly what A2.1 students need to practice.
Block 2 — Opinion (Present Simple)
Q4
Do you think it's okay to take photos of your food and post them online? Or is it a little strange?
Lighthearted but real debate. Almost everyone has an opinion. "I do it." "I don't do it but I understand." "It's a little narcissistic." All valid. Keep the tone fun — this is not a serious ethics question, it's a casual observation about modern life.
Q5
Do you think it's good to take a lot of photos — or do photos stop you from enjoying the moment?
Don Aurelio's position lives here. Both sides are valid. Ask: "At a concert or a birthday party — do you take photos or enjoy the moment?" Most people honestly do both, and feel mildly guilty about it. That tension is a great conversation.
Q6
Is it okay to post photos of your children on social media — or is that dangerous?
Very relevant for Mexican parents in the group. Many post children's photos daily; some are concerned about privacy or safety. No judgment — the conversation about what's okay and what's risky is real and practical. Keep grammar simple: "I think it's okay because..." / "I think it's dangerous because..."
Block 3 — Future Plans (Going to / Will)
Q7
Are you going to delete photos from your phone this week? How many do you have right now?
Very concrete future plan. Let them check their photo count — the number is always surprising. "I have 14,000 photos." "I'm not going to delete anything. I never do." The gap between "I should delete" and "I'm going to delete" is honest and funny.
Q8
Are you going to print any of your photos — like a real photo on paper? Or will they stay digital forever?
The printed vs. digital question opens up a nice nostalgic moment. "My parents have albums with real photos. I have nothing physical." "I want to print photos but I never do." Great going to + will comparison in real context. Don't force grammar rules — just ask and let it flow.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — do you look at your phone more than you look at the people you are with?
The honest mirror question of the tech world. Most people say yes with some guilt. Don't shame — normalize the conversation. "My phone is in my hand almost always. Even at dinner with my family." That admission is the start of something more interesting. Don Aurelio's point comes alive.
Q10
If your phone and all its photos disappeared today — what memory would you lose? And would that be okay?
The emotional closer. "I would lose photos of my kids when they were small." "I would lose photos of my grandma." "I would lose 8,000 photos of food and that would be fine." The answer that reveals what actually matters to them — not what they photograph, but what they're afraid to lose. Silence after this is good.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. Real situation. What are you going to do?
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot
Tonight, you are going to have dinner with your family. One rule: no phones on the table — nobody can take photos, check messages, or look at the screen. Are you going to like that? What are you going to talk about with no phone in your hand?
