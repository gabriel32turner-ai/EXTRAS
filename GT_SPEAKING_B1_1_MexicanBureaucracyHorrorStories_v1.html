Mexican Bureaucracy
Horror Stories

The wrong document, the closed office, the five-hour line — the true Mexican shared experience.

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
World Bank · Government Efficiency Index · 2023
"Mexico ranks 114th out of 190 countries for ease of dealing with government processes. The average Mexican spends over 200 hours per year on bureaucratic procedures."
Teacher script: Read the stat. Then say: "200 hours. That's 8 full days of your life every year — just doing paperwork and waiting in lines." Pause. Then: "Who here has a good government office horror story? I want the worst one in the room." The competition starts immediately.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
What is the worst bureaucracy experience you have ever had? Tell the full story.
Teacher tip: Let this run. The stories from Mexican students about SAT, IMSS, tenencia, INE, passport, or notarías are endless and hilarious. Push: "How many times did you have to go back? How many hours total? What was the final result?"
Q2
Have you ever waited more than 3 hours in a line for something — at a government office, bank, or hospital?
Model answer: "Yes. At the IMSS clinic. I arrived at 6 AM to get a number. They gave me number 47. At 2 PM they called number 31. I had been there for 8 hours. I left without seeing the doctor. I couldn't take it anymore."
Q3
Have you ever paid someone — officially or unofficially — to help you do something faster with the government?
Teacher tip: The "gestor" or "mordida" question. Don't judge. Just let the story come. Very common and very honest. Follow up: "Did it work? How much? Would you do it again?"
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Is Diego right? Are people often their own problem — or is the system genuinely broken?
Teacher tip: Most students will defend themselves. But push: "Have you ever arrived at a government office without all the documents? Have you ever not read the requirements carefully?" Honest answers reveal both sides.
Q5
Do you think it's OK to pay a "gestor" to do your government paperwork for you?
Model answer: "Yes, absolutely. My time is worth money too. If I pay 500 pesos and save 3 days of work, that's a good deal. I'm not doing anything illegal — I'm paying someone for a service. The government made it so complicated that this job even exists."
Q6
Do you think things have gotten better in the last 10 years — or is it the same as always?
Teacher tip: Push for specifics: "Is the SAT online system better? Is IMSS worse? What about your state or city — is your local government faster or slower than before?"
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you waited 4 hours in a line and then the person at the window said your appointment was for tomorrow?
Model answer: "I would take a very deep breath. Then I would very calmly ask for the supervisor. Then I would very, very calmly explain what happened. And if nothing worked — I would go home and cry. And then come back tomorrow."
Q8
What would you do if you could change one thing about government offices in Mexico — just one?
Teacher tip: Strong hypothetical that produces real opinions. Common answers: appointments that actually work, clear lists of required documents, polite staff. Ask: "Why that specific thing? Has it happened to you?"
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Have you ever been rude to a government worker — even if they didn't deserve it — because you were so frustrated?
Teacher tip: Very honest question. Give silence. Most will admit they raised their voice or were short with someone. Ask: "Did you feel bad after? Did it actually help or make things worse?"
Q10
Be honest: do you actually think things will get better — or have you accepted that this is just how Mexico works?
Teacher tip: The exit question of the session. Expect a mix of dark humor and genuine frustration. Don't resolve it — let the ambiguity sit. The students who say "I've accepted it" AND "I'm still angry about it" at the same time are being the most honest.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
You need an urgent document. You have been to the office 4 times. They keep sending you back for different reasons. Your boss needs the document tomorrow morning or you lose an important contract. It's 3 PM on Friday. The office closes at 4. What do you do in the next 60 minutes?
