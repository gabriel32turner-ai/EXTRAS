The boss who ignored you, the tasks you hated, the day you wanted to quit — and what you actually learned.

The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
LinkedIn Workforce Report · 2023
"85% of professionals say their first job taught them something important — but only 34% say they actually enjoyed it."
Teacher script: Read the stat slowly. Then ask: "OK — hands up. Who here had a good first job?" Wait. Then: "And who had a terrible one?" Let the room react. Say: "Today we're talking about the bad ones. They're always more interesting."

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
What was your first job? How old were you, and how long did you stay?
Teacher tip: Don't rush this. Let students describe the job in detail. Push: "What exactly did you do every day? What time did you start? How much did they pay you?" The specifics make the story real and generate the most vocabulary.
Q2
Have you ever cried, felt sick, or wanted to run away because of a job situation?
Model answer: "Yes, at my first job. My boss shouted at me in front of everyone because I made a small mistake. I went to the bathroom and cried. I was 22. I didn't say anything to anyone — I just went home and cried again."
Q3
Have you ever quit a job without another job waiting? What happened?
Teacher tip: Follow up: "Were you scared? How long did it take to find the next job? Did you regret it?" Students who haven't done this often say they wish they had — that's also a great conversation.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think a bad first job is actually good for you — like Sofía says?
Teacher tip: Divide the room. Half defend the "suffering builds character" idea. Half argue that bad experiences just damage confidence. Make each side give a real example from their own life.
Q5
Is it OK to quit a job after only 2 weeks if it's terrible?
Model answer: "I think yes — if the job is truly bad, like abusive or dishonest. But if it's just uncomfortable or difficult, I think you should stay at least 3 months. You can't really know a job in two weeks."
Q6
Do you think companies should train new employees better — or is it the employee's job to figure things out?
Teacher tip: Reference Mateo's opinion. Ask: "Have you ever started a job with zero training? What did you do? Who helped you — if anyone?"
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your first week at a new job was a complete disaster?
Model answer: "I would try to stay calm and not make a decision in the first week. I would talk to one colleague I trust and ask if it's always like this — or if it was just a bad week. Then I would decide."
Q8
What would you do if your boss asked you to do something at work that felt wrong or unfair?
Teacher tip: Keep it simple. Push for a concrete action: "Would you say no? Would you ask why? Would you do it and complain later? Would you do it and say nothing?"
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Have you ever made a really bad mistake at work that you still think about?
Teacher tip: Give students 15 seconds of silence before asking for answers. This is a vulnerable question. When someone shares honestly, don't move on too fast — ask: "What did you do after? Did anyone find out?"
Q10
Be honest: have you ever pretended to work when you weren't really working?
Teacher tip: Everyone laughs — that's the answer. Don't moralize. Just let the stories come. The best part is when students describe exactly what they were actually doing instead of working. It produces great natural language.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
You are 22 years old again. It's your second week at your first job. Your boss is terrible, the pay is bad, and every morning you don't want to go in. But you need the money. What do you do — and what do you tell your younger self?
