Fake Beggars on the Street
You see someone asking for money on the corner. You don't know if they're really in need. What do you do — and what does your answer say about how you see poverty?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One idea that makes the "fake or real" question more complicated.
⏱ 4 min
The Guardian · 2022 · Poverty & Homelessness Report
"Studies consistently show that most people who beg on the street are in genuine need. But because of a small number of visible cases of fraud, many people now assume the opposite — and give nothing."
In Mexico, CONEVAL estimates that over 36 million people live in poverty. Street begging is often the last visible symptom of a much deeper system of unemployment, addiction, disability, migration, and family breakdown. The debate about "fake beggars" is real — but researchers note that the fear of being deceived stops many people from helping anyone, including those who are genuinely desperate.
Teacher Script Read the quote. Then ask directly: "Quick show of hands — who gives money to people begging on the street? Who doesn't? Who used to but stopped?" Don't comment on the answers yet. Then: "The person who stopped — what changed your mind? I want to start there." That's your hook into the real conversation.

"I never know if they're really in need."
The most common reason people give for not helping — and the starting point of this whole conversation.
"I gave money once and he spent it on alcohol."
The experience that changes many people's behavior permanently — even if it only happened once.
"I feel bad walking past them."
The honest admission that most people make — and then do nothing about anyway.
"Better to give to an organization than directly."
The solution many people land on — practical, but it doesn't help the person in front of you right now.
"I don't want to be tricked."
The fear that stops the kindness — even when the risk of being deceived is small.

The Opinion Wall
Four honest reactions to the same situation. Which one is closest to yours?
⏱ 10 min
❤️
Lucía, 32
Social Worker · León
"I give money when I can. Maybe some people are lying — I don't know. But if I give 20 pesos to 10 people and only 3 really need it, those 3 people matter. I would rather be wrong sometimes than always walk past someone who is suffering."
😒
Héctor, 44
Sales Representative · Celaya
"I stopped giving money years ago. I saw a guy begging on Monday and then I saw him buying beer on Tuesday with the money people gave him. I'm not paying for someone's addiction. I donate to a real charity instead. At least I know where my money goes."
🤔
Carmen, 50
Teacher · Irapuato
"I don't give cash, but I don't just walk past either. Sometimes I buy them food. Sometimes I just stop and talk to them for a minute. A lot of people on the street are very lonely. Money isn't always what they need most."
😤
Diego, 38
Business Owner · Salamanca
⚡ Provocateur
"I'll tell you what I really think: some of these people make more money begging than I made in my first job. It's a business — not a real emergency. And by giving them money, we're just making the problem bigger. I know that sounds hard. But it's the truth."
Discussion prompt: "Which of these four people do you agree with most — even if you're not proud of it? Which one says something you've thought but never said out loud?" Diego is the provocateur — his "begging is a business" argument is something many people think privately and rarely say. It's uncomfortable because it mixes real frustration with a lack of empathy. Let the class decide how much of it is true.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Be honest — especially with the ones that are uncomfortable.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever given money to someone begging on the street? Have you ever regretted it — or felt good about it?
Teacher Tip Both sides are valid — guilt from giving and guilt from not giving are both common. Push: "Tell me about a specific moment. What happened? What did you do? How did you feel after?" Present perfect for the question, past simple for the story.
Q2
Have you ever seen someone begging on the street and felt suspicious — like they weren't really in need? What made you think that?
Teacher Tip Get specific: what did they see that made them doubt? Phone, clean clothes, acting? This helps explore how we read "visible poverty" — and how often those signals are misleading. Vocabulary: assume, judge, doubt.
Q3
Have you ever spoken to someone begging on the street — not to give money, just to talk? What happened?
Teacher Tip Carmen from the Opinion Wall does this. It's a less common experience — but when students have it, the story is always interesting. If no one has done it, ask: "Why not? What stops you?" The answer is often discomfort or not knowing what to say.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
In your opinion, is it better to give money directly to a person on the street, or to donate to an organization? Why?
Teacher Tip Both positions are defensible. Direct giving has immediacy — the person gets it now. Organizational giving has accountability — but it also has administrative costs and delays. Push for the values behind the preference: is it about control? Trust? Convenience? Guilt reduction?
Q5
Do you think giving money to someone with an addiction makes things worse for them — or is it not your responsibility to decide how they spend it?
Teacher Tip The addiction question is real and difficult. Harm reduction research suggests that giving money rarely makes addiction worse — most people with addiction are not begging. But the "I don't want to pay for alcohol" feeling is real. Don't resolve it — explore the tension between compassion and paternalism.
Q6
What do you think about Diego's idea that begging is a "business"? Does that change how you feel about giving?
Teacher Tip Diego's "business" framing is provocative but partially accurate — some people do organize begging strategically. But the majority are genuinely vulnerable. The question is: does the existence of fraud justify withholding help from everyone? Most students will say no — but they still feel the hesitation. That hesitation is worth naming.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you saw a child — maybe 8 years old — begging alone on the street with no adult nearby?
Teacher Tip Second conditional — and it raises a different issue: child safety vs. poverty vs. possible exploitation. This is where the "fake beggar" conversation gets most serious. Some children are sent out by adults to beg. Would they give money? Call someone? Stop and talk? Push for a specific action.
Q8
What would you do if you found out that a person you regularly gave money to was actually not poor at all — just pretending?
Teacher Tip Second conditional + emotional response. Push: "Would you stop giving to anyone after that? Or just that person?" The interesting question is whether one deception should change the response to everyone. Research says it does — even though it probably shouldn't.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Be honest: when you walk past someone begging and do nothing, is it because you don't believe they need help — or because it's easier not to stop?
Teacher Tip The honesty question. Most people know the answer is "easier not to stop" — but they justify it with "I'm not sure if they're real." This gap between the real reason and the stated reason is the most interesting territory of the session. Don't push them to confess — just invite the honesty.
Q10 · Self-Reflection
If you were ever in a situation where you had no money and had to ask strangers for help — how do you think you would feel? And do you think people would help you?
Teacher Tip The empathy inversion. This question is about perspective — what it would actually feel like to be on the other side. Most people haven't imagined it concretely. Give them a moment. Then ask: "Would you look clean enough for people to believe you? Would that matter?" The answer often surprises them.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. Record your answer in English.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · B1.2 Fake Beggars
You pass the same man every day on your way to work. He always asks for money for food. Today you have 50 pesos in your pocket — that's your coffee money. He looks worse than usual. You're not 100% sure he's going to spend it on food. Do you give it to him?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. What do you do — and what do you tell yourself in that moment to justify your decision?
Retention Hook The 50 pesos detail makes it real and low-stakes enough to be honest — it's not a large amount, but it's also something concrete. "He looks worse than usual" adds emotional weight without being manipulative. The "what do you tell yourself" ending is what makes it stick — it's not about the money, it's about the internal narrative. End: "Send me your voice note. And I want to hear the justification — not just the yes or no."
