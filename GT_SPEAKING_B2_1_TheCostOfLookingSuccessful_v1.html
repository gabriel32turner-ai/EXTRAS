B2.1 Money & Finance 🟡 Medium 50 min
The Cost of Looking Successful
The watch, the car, the suit. Is your image an investment — or the most expensive thing you own?
🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocation & Opening
⏱ 4 min
The Wall Street Journal · 2024
"Americans spend an estimated $2.4 trillion annually on image-related goods — luxury items, professional attire, and status symbols — much of it financed by debt, driven by the belief that looking successful is the first step to being successful."
Teacher script: Read the headline. Then ask directly: "Do you think your colleagues judge your professional credibility by what you wear, drive, or carry? When was the last time something you bought was partly about what others would think?" Don't rush. This question sits uncomfortably with most people — and that discomfort is the point.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever bought something specifically because of how it would look to colleagues, clients, or your social circle? What was it?
Teacher Tip Almost everyone has a story. Don't let them generalize. "Which item specifically? How much did it cost? Did it actually change anything professionally — or just how you felt?"
Q2
Has your appearance ever opened a door for you professionally — a deal, an introduction, a promotion? Or do you think it was really your work?
Teacher Tip Interesting because most people believe both are true simultaneously. Push: "What percentage of the door was you vs. your appearance? Be honest."
Q3
Do you feel pressure to upgrade your car, phone, or wardrobe when you get promoted — even if the old ones work perfectly well?
Model Answer "Yes — and I'm embarrassed to admit it. When I got my last promotion I bought a new phone that was twice the price of my old one. It worked exactly the same. But I felt like the old one didn't match the title on my business card anymore."
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is investing in your professional image a smart career move — or is it mostly a social pressure we've all normalized?
Teacher Tip Great for a mini-debate. Play devil's advocate: "If a surgeon shows up in a cheap suit but gives you the best diagnosis — do you trust them less?" See where they land.
Q5
Do you think companies judge employees differently based on how they dress or what they drive — even informally, even unconsciously?
Model Answer "I think they do — and most companies would deny it. But watch who gets the client-facing roles, who gets invited to leadership dinners, who gets introduced to executives. It's rarely the person who looks like they don't care about appearances."
Q6
At what point does caring about your professional image become a form of financial self-harm?
Teacher Tip Open-ended. Push for a concrete threshold. "Is it when you're spending more than X%? When you go into debt for it? When you feel anxious if you can't keep up?"
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If you couldn't buy any new clothes, accessories, or technology for one full year — same phone, same car, same wardrobe — how would that affect your professional confidence?
Model Answer "I think I'd be fine for 3 months. By month 6, I'd start feeling self-conscious in certain meetings. By month 12, I'd probably negotiate for an exception. Which tells me my confidence is more tied to my stuff than I'd like to believe."
Q8
If two candidates had identical CVs and interview performance — but one was dressed in a premium suit and the other in clean but basic clothes — which one would you hire? Be honest, not politically correct.
Teacher Tip The honesty is the point. Most people will admit the suit influences them unconsciously. Follow up: "And what does that say about the system we've all agreed to play?"
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
How much of your professional image spending is about genuine confidence — and how much is about fear of being underestimated?
Teacher Tip This is the vulnerability question. Give it space. The most honest answers are always the most linguistically rich — encourage them to sit with the discomfort before answering.
Q10
If the people whose opinion you care about most stopped existing tomorrow — no colleagues, no LinkedIn, no clients — would you still spend the same amount on your professional image?
Model Answer "Honestly? No. I'd probably wear the same three outfits every day and drive something half the price. Which means most of what I spend on image isn't for me — it's for them. And 'them' is a group of people who are probably too busy worrying about their own image to notice mine."

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · Final Dilemma · No right answer
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot
You've been offered two jobs. Job A pays $80,000 pesos a month, has a prestigious corporate title, requires you to look the part at all times — business dinners, client events, premium wardrobe — and you estimate the image cost at $12,000 pesos per month. Job B pays $65,000, is remote, casual dress, zero image pressure, and the work is genuinely interesting. After image costs, Job A leaves you with $68,000. Job B leaves you with $65,000. Which job do you take — and what does your answer tell you about what you actually value?
📱 WhatsApp Homework Record 60 seconds in English: Think of one thing you own right now that you bought partly for status. If you could have that money back today — what would you do with it?
