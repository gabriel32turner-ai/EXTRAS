Quiet Quitting

The new way of working — or just laziness with a fancy name?

🪝
Phase 1 · The Trigger

Read this and react

4 min
"50% of US workers are quiet quitting. They show up, do the bare minimum, and go home. They are not lazy — they are tired of giving 110% for nothing."
— Gallup Workplace Report
Teacher script: "Have you heard about quiet quitting? It's everywhere on LinkedIn. Some people say it's the smartest thing workers can do. Others say it's a slow career killer. Today we'll talk about both sides — and your honest opinion." Let the student react in 2-3 sentences. Don't correct. Just activate.
💉

Who do you agree with?

10 min

Read the 4 opinions. Choose one. Explain: "I agree with [name] because..."

👩‍💼

Software Engineer
"It's not 'quitting' — it's just doing your actual job. The problem is that companies started expecting unpaid extra work as 'normal.' Setting boundaries shouldn't be controversial. It should be standard."
👨‍💼
Roberto, 55
CEO of a small company
"As a boss, I see it clearly. When my team disengages, the company loses. But when my team overdelivers and I don't reward them, I lose them. Quiet quitting is not a worker problem — it's a leadership problem. Bad bosses create quiet quitters."
Key move: Roberto is the surprise — a CEO who blames bosses, not workers. Push the student to react: "Do you trust a boss who says this?" Camila reframes the whole concept ("it's not quitting"). Daniel is the old-school voice. Sofia is the lived experience. Each opinion targets a different angle — the conversation should naturally flow into "well, it depends on your situation..."

Phase 4 · The Conversation Engine

Now you talk

23 min
Golden rule: Student speaks 80% of the time. At B2.1, push for longer answers and follow-up questions. Don't accept short responses — ask "Why?" and "Can you give an example?" Recast errors silently. Encourage the use of the new vocab and colloquial phrases naturally.
Block 1 · Personal experience
1Have you ever been a quiet quitter? When? Be honest.
Teacher tip: Vulnerability opener. Most professionals have done it at least once. If they say no, ask: "Not even after a bad performance review or no raise?" Push gently.
2Tell me about a time you went above and beyond at work. Did the company reward you?
Model answer: "Once I worked weekends for two months on a big project. We delivered on time. My boss said 'great job' — and that was it. No raise, no promotion. After that, I stopped working weekends."
3How easy is it for you to set boundaries with your boss? Why?
Teacher tip: Cultural angle. In Mexico/LATAM, saying no to a boss is harder than in the US. Explore that — it makes the conversation rich and personal.
Block 2 · Opinion
4Is quiet quitting smart strategy or career suicide? Defend your answer.
Model answer: "I think it depends on the company. In a company that rewards effort, quiet quitting is suicide. In a company that exploits people, it's the only smart option. The problem is most companies pretend to reward effort but actually don't."
5Whose fault is quiet quitting — workers, bosses, or the system?
Teacher tip: No right answer. Push for a specific position. If the student says "all of them," ask "Yes, but who is MOST responsible? Pick one."
6Is hustle culture dead, or is it just being criticized by lazy people?
Model answer: "Hustle culture is not dead — but it's losing power. The new generation grew up watching their parents work themselves to exhaustion for nothing. They saw the result and said 'no thanks.' That's not lazy. That's smart."
Block 3 · Hypothetical
7If your boss asked you to work this weekend without extra pay, what would you say?
Grammar focus: Second conditional. Push for longer answer with a real explanation. Model: "I would say no, politely. I would explain that I have personal commitments. If it happened often, I would start looking for a new job."
8If you were a CEO, how would you stop your team from quiet quitting?
Model answer: "I would actually reward people who work hard — with money, not just words. I would also give people real boundaries: no emails after 7 PM, no weekend work. Most quiet quitters just want to feel respected."
Block 4 · Provocative
9Be honest — would you respect a coworker who openly says "I'm just here for the paycheck"?
Teacher tip: The honesty test. Many people sympathize with quiet quitters in theory but judge them in practice. Push the contradiction: "So you support it for yourself but not for your coworker?"
10In 10 years, will quiet quitting be normal or will hustle culture come back?
Model answer: "I think quiet quitting will become the new normal. The economy is changing, AI is doing more of the work, and people don't believe in lifetime loyalty anymore. The companies that survive will be the ones that respect their workers' time."

Phase 5 · The Exit Shot

Take this home

5 min
Think about this tonight
"Your company offers you a 30% raise — but you have to work every weekend for the next year. No exceptions. Your kids are 8 and 10 years old. Do you take the deal? Why or why not?"
📱 Homework: Send me a 1-minute voice message on WhatsApp with your answer. In English.
Retention loop: The Exit Shot puts the abstract debate into a concrete personal dilemma. Money vs family. There is no right answer — but the student will think about it all week. Their WhatsApp answer becomes the warm-up for the next session.
