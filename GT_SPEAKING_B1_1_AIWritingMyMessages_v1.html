AI Writing My Messages

Is it OK to use AI to write your texts, emails, and messages — or is that a little bit dishonest?

🪝
The Trigger
Open the Conversation
⏱ 4 min
The New York Times — 2024
"More and more people are using AI to write their texts, emails, and even love messages. Some people say it saves time. Others say: if AI wrote it, it isn't really from you."
🎙 Teacher Script

Read the headline out loud. Then ask: "Have you ever used AI to write a message? Or do you know someone who does?" Wait for reactions. If nobody talks first, try: "What about a work email? Or a message to say sorry?" Most students have done it at least once — this question unlocks the honesty fast.

The Conversation Engine
23 Minutes of Real Talk
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever used AI to write a message, email, or text? What was the situation?
Most students will say yes — work emails are the most common. Let them be specific. Ask: "Did you change anything or just copy it?" That's where it gets interesting.
Q2
Have you ever received a message and thought "this doesn't sound like that person"? What did you think?
Model answer: "Yes! My coworker sent me an email that was super formal and perfect. He never writes like that. I think he used AI. It felt a bit strange."
Q3
Did you ever have trouble writing a message — maybe a difficult one, like saying sorry or asking for help? What did you do?
This opens up vulnerability. Students often admit they rewrite difficult messages many times. Ask: "Would you use AI for that kind of message?" Let opinions divide naturally.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Is it OK to use AI for work emails? What about personal messages to friends or family?
Most students draw the line at personal messages. Push them: "Why? The words are still about what you feel, right?" Let them defend their boundary.
Q5
Do you think AI messages are dishonest — or are they just a tool, like a dictionary or a spell-checker?
The core debate question. Some students say it's just a tool. Others feel the words should come from you. No right answer — let both sides talk.
Q6
Do you think younger people use AI more for messages than older people? Why?
Generational debate — fun and easy to discuss. Model answer: "I think younger people are more comfortable with technology. But older people maybe think it's not right to use a machine for personal things."
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you found out your partner used AI to write a romantic message to you?
This is Sofía's situation reversed. Some students say they wouldn't care. Others say they would feel it wasn't real. Let both sides share without judging.
Q8
What would you do if your boss asked you to always use AI to write client emails — no personal style, just the AI version?
Model answer: "I think I would do it. If the boss says so, it's the job. But I would feel a little strange. Like I'm not really there in my work."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
If the message gets the right result — the person feels happy, the problem is solved — does it matter who or what wrote it?
The philosophical closer for B1.1. Keep it simple: "Is the result more important than the process?" Most students have a strong feeling about this — let them defend it.
Q10
Be honest: do you think you write better messages than AI? Or does AI do it better?
Fun self-reflection closer. Most students laugh but then get honest. Some say AI is more correct but cold. Others say they prefer the human version, mistakes and all. Great ending energy.

One Question. No Easy Answer.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Final Dilemma
Your best friend is very sick in hospital. You don't know what to say. You use AI to write the message — and it comes out beautiful. Your friend says it made them feel much better. Do you tell them AI wrote it — or do you keep the secret?
