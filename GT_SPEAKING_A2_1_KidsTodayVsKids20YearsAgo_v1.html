Kids Today vs.
Kids 20 Years Ago

Are children today different from children when you were young? Better, worse — or just different?

🪝
The Trigger
Open the Conversation
⏱ 4 min
A question for the class
"When you were a child, what did you do after school? Now think about children today. Are they doing the same things — or something completely different?"
🎙 Teacher Script

Ask: "What did you do after school when you were 8 or 9 years old?" Take 2-3 quick answers — play outside, watch TV, help at home. Then ask: "And children today — what do they do?" The contrast activates immediately. Keep it light and nostalgic — this is a fun topic, not an angry one.

The Conversation Engine
23 Minutes of Real Talk
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
What did you do after school when you were a child? What was your favorite game or activity?
Nostalgic and easy — every student has an answer. Model answer: "After school I went outside with my friends. We played football in the street until dinner. My mom called me from the window." Let them describe freely in past simple.
Q2
What did your parents NOT allow you to do when you were a child? Did you think it was fair?
Model answer: "My parents didn't allow me to watch TV after 9pm. I thought it was terrible! Now I understand. But at the time I was very angry." Great past simple practice with an emotional hook.
Q3
Did you get a punishment when you did something wrong as a child? What was it?
Fun and slightly embarrassing stories come out here. Create a safe, light tone: "Don't worry — we all did something bad as children." Most students laugh and share. Past simple naturally flows.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think children today have too much screen time? Is that a problem?
The most common concern about modern childhood. Most students say yes — too much screen time. Push: "Why is it a problem? What can happen?" Simple present reasoning: "Because children don't..." Encourage 2-3 sentence answers.
Q5
What is more important for a child — freedom to make decisions, or clear rules from parents?
A vs. B structure — easy to answer. Model answer: "I think both. Rules are important because children need to learn limits. But some freedom is also important — to grow and learn from mistakes."
Q6
Do you think children today are happier than children 20 years ago — or less happy?
An interesting opinion question with no easy answer. Some say happier (more things, more freedom). Others say less (pressure, social media, less play). Both views are valid — let the class debate in simple present.
Block 3 — Future Plans
Q7
If you have children now or in the future, are you going to limit their phone time? How?
Direct "going to" practice. For students with kids: "Are you going to change anything after today?" For those without: "What are you going to do differently from your own parents?" Both are natural and personal.
Q8
What is one thing from your childhood that you will share with the children in your life?
Warm "will" practice. Model answer: "I will teach my nephew to play football in the street, not on a screen. I think that's important." Creates a positive, hopeful ending to Block 3.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — do you think you were a difficult child? What did you do that made your parents crazy?
Self-reflection with humor. Most students have a story. Create permission to laugh: "Come on — tell me one thing." Model answer: "Yes. I broke my neighbor's window with a ball. I ran away and didn't say it was me. My mom found out one week later."
Q10
You see a child in a restaurant with a phone, watching videos and not talking to anyone. The parents are happy because the child is quiet. What do you think?
Scenario question — specific and visual. Students have strong opinions but may hesitate to judge. Push gently: "Is it OK? Is it a problem? Is it the parents' fault?" Simple present reasoning works perfectly.

The Exit Shot
One Situation. Your Decision.
⏱ 5 min
🎯 Exit Shot
Your nephew or young family member is 10 years old. He spends 6 hours a day on his phone or tablet — games, videos, TikTok. His parents don't say anything. You are going to see him this weekend. Are you going to say something to him or to his parents — or are you going to stay quiet?
🎙️
Voice Note Homework
Record a 60-second voice note in English on WhatsApp. What are you going to do this weekend? Say something or stay quiet? Be specific — and why. Your teacher will listen and give you feedback before the next session.
