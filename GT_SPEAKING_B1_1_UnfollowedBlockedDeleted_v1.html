Unfollowed, Blocked, Deleted
Three buttons. Three ways to end a connection. We've all done it — and we've all felt it. What does it mean when the hardest thing you do is press a button?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One small fact about a very big feeling.
⏱ 4 min
Vox / YouGov Poll · 2022
"64% of social media users say they have been hurt when they found out a friend unfollowed or blocked them. And 71% admit they have checked to see if someone blocked them — more than once."
Being unfollowed, muted, or blocked by someone you know feels very different from losing a follower you've never met. For many people — especially those under 40 — a block from a friend or ex-partner has the emotional weight of being ignored in public. The three-button hierarchy (unfollow, mute, block) has created a new social language that nobody taught us how to use.
Teacher Script Read the stats. Then go straight to experience: "Quick — who has checked to see if someone blocked them? Don't lie." Let the laughter and recognition create the opening. Then: "And who has blocked someone recently? You don't have to say who." Both questions together create an honest, equal room — everyone has done both.

"She blocked me out of nowhere."
Said with real shock — because a block without explanation is a conversation that never happened.
"I'm just going to mute him."
The diplomatic option — you keep the peace but stop seeing their posts. They never know.
"Why is she checking my profile if she doesn't follow me anymore?"
The stalking paradox — you stopped following but you can't stop looking.
"I had to block him for my own peace of mind."
The healthy version of blocking — not revenge, but protection.
"Come on, it's just social media."
Said by someone who has never checked their phone 3 times hoping to see that someone unblocked them.

Andrés, 33
Chef · Monterrey

Accountant · Celaya
"I muted my sister three months ago. She posts 15 times a day and it makes me stressed. She doesn't know. We still talk every week on the phone. I don't see why I need to tell her. Some problems disappear when you just manage them quietly."
🧓
Don Jorge, 55
Retired · Guadalajara
⚡ Provocateur
"In my generation, if you had a problem with someone, you talked to them. Now people end friendships with a button and never say a word. That's not strength — that's fear. Blocking is what you do when you don't have the courage to have a real conversation."
Discussion prompt: "Which of these four people do you agree with the most? Which one says something uncomfortable that you know is at least a little bit true?" Don Jorge is the provocateur — his "blocking is fear" argument will hit some students hard because many have used blocking to avoid exactly the conversation he's describing. The discomfort is the point.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Personal stories only. Everyone has done all of these things.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever been unfollowed or blocked by someone and felt hurt about it? What happened?
Teacher Tip The personal experience opener — present perfect for the question, past simple for the story. Let them tell the story without interrupting. Key follow-up: "Did you ever find out why?" The answer is almost always "no" — and that's the most interesting part.
Q2
Have you ever blocked someone — a friend, an ex, a colleague? What did they do to get blocked?
Teacher Tip The other side — now they're the one who pressed the button. Push: "Did you ever talk to them about it first? Or did you just block?" The Andrés vs. Don Jorge dynamic emerges naturally. No judgment — just curiosity.
Q3
Have you ever checked someone's profile to see if they blocked you — more than once?
Teacher Tip The stat from the trigger said 71% of people do this. Most students will laugh — because they've done it. The present perfect plus "more than once" is both grammatically clear and emotionally honest. Push: "How did it feel when you found out — blocked or not blocked?"
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is it okay to block someone without giving them an explanation? Or do you owe them a reason?
Teacher Tip The core ethical question. Andrés says no explanation needed. Don Jorge says it's avoidance. Most people are somewhere in the middle — "it depends on who it is." Push for specific rules: "For a stranger? For a friend? For an ex? For a colleague?" Different answers, different reasoning.
Q5
Do you think muting someone — without telling them — is dishonest? Or is it just a smart way to manage your life?
Teacher Tip Sofía's quiet solution. "Dishonest" is an interesting word here — because technically you haven't lied. But there's something passive-aggressive about muting someone you talk to every week. Push: "Would you want to know if someone muted you?" The answer to that question often changes their opinion about doing it to others.
Q6
Do you think social media makes it easier to end relationships — or harder to really let go?
Teacher Tip Both sides are true simultaneously. Easier to cut contact — one button. But harder to forget when you can still see their life, their new partner, their posts. The "stalking after blocking" paradox is real. Let them name the contradiction without you resolving it.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if a good friend blocked you with no explanation — and you saw them at work every day?
Teacher Tip Second conditional — and it creates a collision between the online and offline worlds. You can block someone online but you can't block them in the elevator. Push: "Would you say something? Would you pretend it didn't happen? Would it change how you act around them at work?"
Q8
What would you do if you found out your partner was secretly following your ex on social media?
Teacher Tip Second conditional — and very personal. This is a real scenario for many people. Push: "Is it okay to follow an ex? Is it okay if you know about it? What if they were hiding it?" The "secretly" detail is the loaded word — that's where the real conversation is.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Have you ever blocked someone to avoid a conversation you knew you should have had?
Teacher Tip Don Jorge's argument, turned personal. The honest answer for many people is "yes." Don't push for confession — just create the space for honesty. If they admit it, ask: "Do you regret it?" The present perfect makes it personal: "Have you ever blocked someone to avoid..."
Q10 · Self-Reflection
Is there someone you've been following on social media for years who you don't really care about anymore — but you haven't unfollowed because it feels complicated?
Teacher Tip The closing self-reflection — and the most universally relatable one. The "complicated" ex, the old colleague, the cousin you don't really like, the friend from high school. Everyone has at least one. The interesting question is: why is it complicated? What are we afraid of? Give them time to answer honestly.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. Record your answer in English.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · B1.1 Unfollowed, Blocked, Deleted
Your best friend of 8 years unfollowed you on Instagram this morning. No message, no fight, nothing. You see them at lunch today. Do you say something — or do you pretend you didn't notice?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. What do you say — or not say — and why? And be honest: have you already decided before you even think about it?
Retention Hook The exit shot works because it forces a choice between two forms of honesty — saying something (honest about the hurt) or saying nothing (honest about the awkwardness). Both options are uncomfortable. "And have you already decided before you even think about it?" is the real question — most people know their instinct immediately. End: "Send me your voice note. I want to hear what you actually do — not what you think you should do."
