The Bad Boss I Had

Toxic management, humiliation, credit-stealing — and what you actually did about it.

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
Gallup · State of the Global Workplace Report
"The single biggest factor in employee engagement is the direct manager. 70% of the variance in team engagement is determined by the manager alone."
Teacher script: Read the stat aloud. Pause. Then ask: "Think about your best job and your worst job. What was different? Was it the company — or was it the boss?" Let students react freely for 90 seconds before moving to vocabulary.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever had a boss who humiliated you or a colleague in front of others? What happened?
Teacher tip: If students are shy, start with a colleague's story: "Maybe it didn't happen to you directly — have you seen it happen to someone else?" Push for specific detail: who, where, what exactly was said.
Q2
Have you ever felt that your boss took credit for your work? How did you handle it?
Model answer: "Yes, my manager has done this several times. He sent my analysis to the director with his name on it. I felt really angry but I said nothing because I was afraid of losing the job."
Q3
Have you ever quit a job because of a bad boss — or seriously considered it?
Teacher tip: Follow up with: "What kept you there?" or "How long did you stay after you decided to leave?" Common answers: salary, the team, fear of unemployment.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
In your opinion, which is worse — an aggressive boss or an invisible one? Why?
Teacher tip: This is a natural debate. Divide the group: aggressive vs. invisible. Make each side defend their position with at least two reasons.
Q5
Do you think companies should train managers on how to treat people — or is that just common sense?
Model answer: "I think companies should do it, because a lot of people become managers just because they are good at their technical job — not because they know how to lead people."
Q6
What do you think about employees who complain about their boss but never do anything? Is Diego right?
Teacher tip: Reference Diego's opinion from the wall. Some students will agree, some will push back hard. Let the tension develop — don't resolve it early.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your boss humiliated you in front of your team tomorrow?
Model answer: "I would probably stay quiet in the moment, but I would ask for a private meeting the same day and tell them that what they did was not acceptable." Push for concrete steps, not just feelings.
Q8
What would you do if you became a manager and realized you were making the same mistakes as your bad boss?
Teacher tip: This question creates strong self-reflection. Good follow-up: "Do you think you already have any tendencies from a bad boss?"
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Have you ever protected a bad boss — defended them to colleagues or covered up their mistakes? Why?
Teacher tip: This question makes people uncomfortable in the best way. Common motivations: loyalty, fear, hoping the boss will protect them back. Don't judge — explore.
Q10
Be honest: have you ever been a bad boss yourself — even for a short time?
Teacher tip: The key word is "be honest." Give students 15 seconds to think before they answer. This is the most vulnerable question of the session — celebrate honesty when it appears.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
Your current boss has been publicly humiliating a colleague for 3 months. HR knows. Nothing has changed. You are next in line for a promotion — but only if your boss approves it. What do you do?
