Netflix and the End of Real TV
Remember waiting all week for your favorite show? Remember watching commercials with your family? That world is gone — and nobody asked if we wanted to keep it.
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One number that says everything about how TV watching has changed.
⏱ 4 min
Nielsen Media Research · 2023
"For the first time in history, streaming services like Netflix and YouTube now get more viewing time than traditional TV in the United States. In Mexico, the number of cable TV subscribers has dropped 40% in five years."
In 2010, almost every Mexican family had cable TV. Today, many households have cancelled it completely — replacing it with Netflix, Amazon, YouTube, and TikTok. Traditional TV channels are losing viewers every year, and many local shows and news programs are disappearing. The question is whether something important is being lost — or whether this is simply how progress works.
Teacher Script Start with a show of hands: "Who still has cable TV at home? Who cancelled it in the last 3 years? Who never had it — went straight to streaming?" Let the numbers create the conversation. Then: "Think of a TV show you remember watching as a child with your family. What was it?" Let 2–3 students name a show. That nostalgia is your entry point.

Noun

Noun

Verb

Verb

"Just one more episode."
Said at midnight. Never true.
"I don't know what to watch."
Said after 25 minutes of scrolling through 10,000 options on Netflix.
"Don't spoil it for me!"
The new social rule of streaming — everyone watches at a different time, so everyone is always behind.
"I'm still paying for that?"
Said when you check your bank account and realize you forgot to cancel a streaming service 4 months ago.
"We used to watch this together as a family."
The nostalgia that hits when you realize everyone in the house is now watching something different, alone, on their own screen.

Nurse · León

🤨
Rodrigo, 45
Engineer · Querétaro
⚡ Provocateur
"We cancelled cable and thought we saved money. Then we got Netflix. Then Disney. Then HBO. Now we pay more than before — and we never agree on what to watch. The difference is that before, the TV decided for us and we were fine with it. Now we have infinite choice and we're never happy. That says a lot about people."
Discussion prompt: "Which of these four people do you agree with most? Which one says something you've actually thought but maybe never said?" Rodrigo is the provocateur — his "infinite choice makes us unhappy" argument is counterintuitive but backed by real psychology research on the paradox of choice. Most B1.1 students will recognize the feeling even if they haven't named it.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Stories from the sofa. No wrong answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever binge-watched a series until very late at night? What show was it — and did you feel bad the next morning?
Teacher Tip Everyone has done this. The follow-up is key: "What time did you stop? Was it worth it?" Push for the specific show and episode. This is a great storytelling moment — past simple for the events, present perfect for the question. Model: "Have you ever binge-watched...? Yes, I watched Breaking Bad until 3am on a Tuesday."
Q2
Have you ever spent 20 minutes looking for something to watch — and then given up? What did you do instead?
Teacher Tip The "paradox of choice" experience — too many options, no decision. This connects to Rodrigo's point. Most students will say they went to their phone, YouTube, or just went to sleep. The irony: more content, less watching. Let them name their own escape route.
Q3
Do you remember a show you watched with your family when you were young? What was it — and does your family still watch TV together?
Teacher Tip This activates the nostalgia thread from the trigger. Common Mexican answers: Chespirito, telenovelas, El Chavo. The contrast with today — everyone on their own screen — is the emotional core of the conversation. "Used to" is a great grammar structure to listen for and encourage.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is it okay to watch a show on your phone while you are eating with your family? Why or why not?
Teacher Tip A household debate most families have had. Push: "Do you do it? Does your family do it? Does it bother you?" The gap between what people think is okay and what they actually do is always interesting. No judgment.
Q5
Do you think streaming services are good for local Mexican culture — or are they replacing Mexican shows with American ones?
Teacher Tip Culture + media question. Netflix does produce some Mexican content (Club de Cuervos, Luis Miguel, La Casa de las Flores) but most content is American or international. Push: "When was the last time you watched a Mexican show? On what platform?" The answer is usually revealing.
Q6
Is it better to watch one episode per week and wait — or get the whole season at once? What's lost when you don't have to wait?
Teacher Tip The anticipation vs. instant gratification debate. Old TV made you wait — and that waiting was part of the experience. Water cooler conversations, guessing what happens next. Streaming eliminates that shared cultural moment. See if they can name what's lost without you telling them.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if Netflix disappeared tomorrow and you could only watch regular TV with commercials?
Teacher Tip Second conditional — and a fun one. Push for honest reactions: "Would you be okay? Or would you feel like something important was gone?" Most students realize they've become more dependent on streaming than they thought. The commercial detail is key — that's the part that feels most impossible to go back to.
Q8
What would you do if your family said: "No screens during dinner — ever"? Would you be okay with that rule?
Teacher Tip Second conditional + family dynamics. This is a rule some families already have — and many more argue about. Push: "Who has the rule in your house? Who breaks it?" The family tension around screens is relatable and easy to talk about at B1.1 level.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
How many hours did you watch TV or streaming last week? Are you happy about that number?
Teacher Tip Like the screen time question in Lab 1 — specific, personal, slightly uncomfortable. Don't let them say "not that much." Ask: "Estimate. Give me a number." Then: "Is that how you planned to spend that time?" The gap between planned and actual use is the real conversation.
Q10 · Self-Reflection
Think about the last week. Did you watch something that you don't really remember — just background noise while you were on your phone?
Teacher Tip The "background noise" watching phenomenon — streaming on TV while scrolling on phone. Most people do this. The point isn't guilt — it's awareness. Two screens, no attention for either. End with: "Is that how you want to spend your free time?" Don't answer it for them.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. Record your answer in English.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · B1.1 Netflix and the End of Real TV
Your family wants to have dinner together every night this week with no phones and no TV — just talking. For 7 days. Are you in?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. Yes or no — and tell me: what's the hardest part of that challenge for you personally?
Retention Hook The simplicity is the power — this exit shot doesn't need explanation or context. Every student immediately understands what 7 dinners with no screens means for their specific family. The "hardest part" question creates an honest voice note. End: "Send me the voice note before Thursday. And tell me honestly — did you actually try it?"
