Friends Who
Disappeared

You didn't have a fight. You didn't say goodbye. One day they were just… gone. What happened to those friendships?

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocative Headline
⏱ 4 min
The Guardian · "Why Adult Friendships Are So Hard to Keep" · 2022
"Most adults lose half of their close friends every 7 years — not through arguments or dramatic endings, but simply through life getting in the way. Researchers call it 'friendship decay.' Most people don't even notice it's happening until the friendship is already gone."
Teacher Script: Read the quote, then pause. Ask: "Half of your friends every 7 years — does that surprise you? Think about who you were friends with 7 years ago. How many of those people do you still talk to?" Let students count silently. Then: "Did those friendships end — or did they just… fade?" The goal is to open the emotional door quickly without making it heavy.

⏱ 10 min

"I lost my best friend from university and it still hurts. We didn't fight — we just stopped calling. I always think: if I had tried harder, would we still be friends? I should have called first. I keep waiting for him to call me. Neither of us does."

"I was the one who disappeared. I had a baby, I changed jobs, I moved cities. I didn't have time or energy for anyone. It wasn't personal. Now that things are calmer, I try to reconnect — but it's different. You can't just press pause on a friendship."
Provocateur
🧔
Rodrigo · 45
Sales Manager · Querétaro
"Honestly? If a friendship fades that easily, it wasn't that strong. Real friends don't disappear. The ones who left probably weren't your real friends. Stop feeling guilty. You didn't lose a real friendship — you just found out who the real ones are."
Teacher Note: Rodrigo is the provocateur because he removes the guilt — but also removes the nuance. His position is comforting on the surface but dismissive of the real complexity of adult friendship. Push: "Do you agree with Rodrigo — or does his answer feel too easy?" Then: "Have you ever been Valeria — the one who disappeared?"

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever lost a close friend — not because of a fight, but just because you both stopped making effort?
Almost everyone has this experience. Let the student tell the story. Push: "When did you realize the friendship was over?" Target: present perfect to open, then past simple for the story. Key vocab to activate: fade, drift apart, lose touch.
Q2
Have you ever tried to reconnect with an old friend after years of no contact? What happened?
Two types of outcomes: it worked, or it was awkward. Both are interesting. Push: "Was it the same — or did it feel like talking to a different person?" Model answer: "I sent a message after 3 years. We met for coffee and it was nice — but not the same as before. I think we both knew it."
Q3
Have you ever been the one who disappeared from a friendship — maybe without realizing it at the time?
Based on Valeria's bubble. This question flips the perspective — the student was the one who faded. Push gently: "Did you feel guilty when you realized it?" Target: past simple + present perfect for reflection. Key: no judgment, just curiosity.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
In your opinion, whose responsibility is it to keep a friendship alive — or is it always both people?
Good for activating: responsibility, effort, initiative. Push: "What if one person always calls first — and the other never does? Is that still a friendship?" Target: should + both/neither/either. Model: "I think it should always be 50/50 — but in real life, one person almost always puts in more effort."
Q5
Do you think it's possible to go back to a friendship after 5 or 10 years — or does too much change?
Push: "What would need to be the same for it to work?" Target: modals for possibility (can, might, could) + comparative structures. Key vocab: pick up where you left off, common ground, too much water under the bridge.
Q6
Is it OK to have fewer friends as you get older — or should adults try harder to keep friendships?
Connects to Sofía's "quality not quantity" position. Push: "At what age did you start having fewer friends — and was it a choice or did it just happen?" Target: should + comparative (fewer/less/more). Encourage the student to reflect on their own friendship pattern over time.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if an old friend — someone you haven't spoken to in 5 years — suddenly sent you a message asking to meet?
Target: second conditional (would + base verb). Push: "Would your answer change if you were the one who disappeared — vs. them?" Model: "I think I would say yes — but I'd also be a little nervous. I'd probably wonder why they're reaching out now."
Q8
What would you do if you realized your best friend was slowly pulling away — less messages, shorter replies, canceling plans — but hadn't said anything?
Target: second conditional + direct action vocabulary. Push: "Would you say something directly — or wait and see?" Model: "I would probably say something like: 'Hey, are we okay? I feel like we've been a bit distant lately.' It's scary but better than just watching it fade." Role-play this sentence if possible.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Do adults actually need close friends — or do most people get by fine with just family and work colleagues?
Research says: no. People with close friendships outside family live longer and report higher happiness. But many adults genuinely don't have them. Push: "Do you think the people in your life right now are enough — or is something missing?" Target: present simple + modals for need/necessity.
Q10
Is there a person you've been meaning to contact for months — but you keep not doing it? What's really stopping you?
The self-reflection closer. Most students will smile or go quiet — because they know exactly who this is. Don't rush. Push: "What would happen if you sent a message right now — today? What's the worst realistic outcome?" The goal is not to make them feel guilty, but to activate real personal reflection in English.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One Final Dilemma
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
You have a friend you've known for 15 years. In the last 2 years, you've noticed that every time you talk, the conversation is always about them — their problems, their life, their news. They never ask about you. You've started to dread their calls. Do you say something — or slowly let the friendship fade?
Homework Loop: "Record a 60-second WhatsApp voice message. Tell me what you would do — and give me the exact words you would say to that friend. In English. No script."
