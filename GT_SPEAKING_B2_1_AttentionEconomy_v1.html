The Attention Economy
Your attention is the product. Every notification, every scroll, every algorithm is engineered to sell your focus to the highest bidder. And you agreed to all of it.
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One quote about a resource more valuable than oil. Let them sit with it.
⏱ 4 min
The Social Dilemma · Netflix / Tristan Harris, Former Google Design Ethicist · 2020
"There are only two industries that call their customers 'users': illegal drugs and software. That's not a coincidence."
The attention economy describes the model where digital platforms generate revenue by capturing and selling user attention to advertisers. The average person now spends over 6 hours per day on screens, checks their phone 96 times a day, and has seen their average attention span drop from 12 seconds in 2000 to 8 seconds in 2023 — one second less than a goldfish. The platforms are working exactly as designed.
Teacher Script Read the quote. Pause. Then: "First reaction — do you think that comparison is fair, or is it an overstatement?" Then immediately: "Before you answer — pick up your phone. Look at your screen time from yesterday. How many hours?" The screen time number always lands harder than any statistic you can give them. Let the silence after that question do the work.
💉

"I just picked up my phone for no reason."
The moment you realize the habit has become fully automatic and you don't even know why you did it.
"I went down a rabbit hole."
You opened YouTube to watch one video. Ninety minutes later you are still there and cannot explain what happened.
"I need to detox from social media."
Said roughly once a week. Rarely followed through on for more than 48 hours.
"I can't focus like I used to."
Said by people in their 30s and 40s who remember being able to read for two hours without checking their phone.
"If it's free, you're the product."
The most efficient summary of how the attention economy actually works — and most people have heard it but still use the apps.

Phase 3
The Opinion Wall
Four people. Four different relationships with their own attention. Which one are you?
⏱ 10 min
📵
Alejandra, 36
Project Manager · Querétaro
"Two years ago I deleted Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter. Cold turkey. The first two weeks were genuinely uncomfortable — I kept reaching for my phone. But after a month, my ability to concentrate came back in a way I hadn't experienced since my twenties. I'm not saying everyone should do it. I'm saying I had no idea how much it was costing me until it was gone."
🤷
Diego, 29
Social Media Manager · CDMX
"I spend 8 hours a day on social media — for work. And then I go home and scroll for another 3. Does it affect me? Probably. But I'm not interested in the 'phones are bad' conversation. My parents watched TV for 4 hours a night in the 90s and nobody called that an addiction. Every generation has their distraction. This is ours."
🧐
Mónica, 44
Neurologist · Guadalajara
"What I see clinically concerns me. The reward cycles these platforms are built on are functionally identical to the ones that drive substance dependence. We are running a mass neurological experiment on billions of people — especially children — with no long-term data on the effects. The fact that it's legal and profitable doesn't make it safe."
💰
Ernesto, 50
Digital Marketing Director · Monterrey
⚡ Provocateur
"I've spent 20 years building campaigns that capture attention. And I'll tell you something: attention was always for sale. Newspapers, radio, TV — all of it was built on the same model. The difference now is efficiency. We're just better at it. The outrage is selective. People didn't complain about being 'addicted' to Friends. They complain about TikTok because they feel judged for it."
Discussion prompt: "Which of these four people would you most want to have dinner with — and which one would you argue with all night?" Ernesto is the provocateur because his argument is historically accurate and morally uncomfortable at the same time. He's not wrong that TV was also engineered for attention. But he deliberately avoids the question of scale and neurological impact. Let the class catch that gap.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Their phones are probably in their pockets right now. Keep that in the room.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
What's your actual daily screen time? And when you saw that number for the first time — what did you feel?
Teacher Tip Have them check their phones right now if they haven't already. The average in this age group is 4–7 hours. The emotional reaction — denial, guilt, justification — is where the conversation actually starts. Don't skip this.
Q2
Have you ever tried to reduce your screen time or delete an app — and failed? What happened?
Teacher Tip Almost everyone has tried and reinstalled. The failure is the interesting part — what brought them back? Boredom? FOMO? Habit? Push for the specific moment, not just "I went back." Present perfect: "Have you ever tried…"
Q3
Have you noticed changes in your ability to focus on one thing for a long time — reading a book, watching a long film, or sitting without your phone?
Teacher Tip This is the "deep work" question. Most people over 30 can remember being able to read for two hours. Now many can't get through 20 pages. If they say "no change," ask: "When did you last read a book? A full one?" The answer is often years.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is social media addiction real — or is "addiction" too strong a word for something you can technically stop whenever you want?
Teacher Tip The "you can stop whenever you want" argument is exactly what addicts say about their substance of choice. Push them to define addiction, then apply their own definition. Mónica's clinical framing is useful here — the dopamine mechanisms are the same.
Q5
Should platforms be legally required to limit daily usage — like alcohol is limited in advertising — or is that too much government control?
Teacher Tip Regulation vs. individual freedom. This question works well with modals: "should," "could," "might." Push for a specific policy they'd support — not just "yes, regulate it." What would the rule look like? Age limits? Daily caps? Warning labels?
Q6
Ernesto argued that attention was always for sale — that this is nothing new. Is he right, or is there something genuinely different about how apps work today?
Teacher Tip The historical continuity argument vs. the scale/personalization argument. TV was broadcast — the same content for everyone. Algorithms are personalized and adaptive — they learn what keeps you specifically engaged. That's the qualitative difference. See if they find it themselves.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If your phone showed a real-time counter of how much money your attention generated for platforms today — would that change how you use it?
Teacher Tip Second conditional + would. The "if you could see the transaction" framing makes the attention economy tangible. Some students will say yes — seeing it would make them angry. Others will say no — they already know and don't care. Both are interesting.
Q8
Will the next generation — people who grew up on TikTok from age 8 — be able to do deep, focused work in a way that will be competitive in the job market?
Teacher Tip Will + future concern. This is the long-term consequence question. Connect to the AI jobs lab if they've done it: if AI handles repetitive tasks, and humans struggle to do deep work, what role is left? The intersection is genuinely alarming — and worth letting them sit with.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
If someone told you that you spend more time on your phone than with any single person in your life — would that be true? And what do you think about that?
Teacher Tip For most people this is statistically true. The phone is their most-used "relationship." Don't soften it. Let the math land. Then ask: "Does that change anything for you — or is it just a number?"
Q10 · Self-Reflection
What is the most important thing you have not done — a project, a book, a conversation, a relationship — because you were on your phone instead?
Teacher Tip The closing question of the session — and the most personal one. Give them time. The honest answers are uncomfortable: "I haven't called my dad." "I haven't finished my MBA thesis." "I don't know what my kids are actually interested in." Don't follow up with advice. Just let them name it.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. One week to answer with your behavior, not your words.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · B2.1 The Attention Economy
Starting tomorrow, you put your phone in a drawer every day from 7pm to 9pm — no exceptions. No WhatsApp, no Instagram, no news, no email. Just 2 hours. You have a partner, maybe kids, unread messages at work. Can you actually do it for 7 consecutive days?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Don't just tell me yes or no. Tell me: what's the hardest part of this for you specifically? What would you do instead — and does that answer surprise you?
Retention Hook The exit shot is a behavioral challenge, not just a reflection prompt — which makes it memorable. Next session, ask: "Did anyone try it?" The ones who did have a story. The ones who didn't have a more interesting story. Both create the perfect opening for Session 2 on this topic. End: "Send me your voice note before our next class. And I'll know if you actually tried."
