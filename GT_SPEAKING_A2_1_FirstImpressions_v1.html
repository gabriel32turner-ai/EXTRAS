First Impressions:
Right or Wrong?
You look at someone for the first time. You have an opinion in 5 seconds. Is that smart — or dangerous?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
A real fact. A real conversation.
⏱ 4 min
Princeton University · Psychology Study
"Your brain decides if a person is nice, smart, or dangerous in less than one second. And most of the time — you don't change that opinion."
Teacher Script
"Think about the last new person you met. At work, at a party, anywhere. What was your first thought about them? And — was your first impression correct? Today we talk about first impressions. They are quick, they are strong, and sometimes they are completely wrong."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real stories. No wrong answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
Think about a person you met recently. What was your first impression? Was it correct?
The core question of the class. Push for a real person — at work, a new neighbor, someone at a party. Model: "I met a new coworker last month. My first impression was: she is cold. But I was wrong. She was just nervous on the first day."
Q2
Did someone have a bad first impression of YOU — and then change their opinion? What happened?
Flips the perspective to the student as the subject. Very engaging. If they can't think of one, ask: "Did your boss or a colleague think something wrong about you at the beginning? How did you change that?" Good vocabulary: seemed, looked, changed their mind.
Q3
When you started your current job, what was your first impression of your boss or your team?
Workplace context, past simple, very concrete. Model: "My first impression of my boss was: very serious and a little cold. But after two weeks I saw he is actually very fair and he helps the team. I changed my mind completely."
Block 2 — Opinion (Present Simple)
Q4
Do you think clothes are important for a first impression? Do you judge people by their clothes?
Most people say "no, I don't judge clothes" and then give an example that says the opposite. Gently point that out. Don Roberto's point lives here. Simple honest conversation: "Do you dress differently for an important meeting? Why?" The answer reveals the truth.
Q5
Is it good to trust your first impression — or is it better to wait and see?
The core debate question. Model: "I think both. In some situations — like a stranger on the street at night — your first impression can protect you. In work or friendships, it's better to wait." Push for a reason in each case.
Q6
Is a person's face important for a first impression — or do you look at other things first?
Fun sensory question — what do people notice first? Eyes, smile, posture, energy, voice? Everyone has a different answer. No wrong response. Great vocabulary moment: "I look at the eyes first. A person's eyes tell you a lot. When they don't look at you — something is wrong."
Block 3 — Future Plans (Going to / Will)
Q7
Are you going to meet any new people soon — at work, at an event, or with friends? How are you going to make a good first impression?
Future planning with real vocabulary. "I'm going to smile. I'm going to look at their eyes. I'm going to listen more than I talk." Push for specific actions — what ARE they going to do differently after this class? Great retention hook.
Q8
The next time you meet someone new, are you going to trust your first impression — or are you going to wait?
Direct application of the class topic to the future. Model: "I think I'm going to wait. After this class, I see that my first impression is not always correct. I'm going to try to know the person before I have an opinion." Simple, honest, uses going to correctly.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — do YOU judge people by their appearance? Their clothes, their hair, their tattoos?
The honest mirror question. Almost everyone says "no" and almost everyone does it. Don't shame — celebrate honesty. A good A2.1 answer: "Yes. If a person has very dirty clothes and doesn't look clean, I feel uncomfortable. I don't think it's nice but it's true." That's perfect honesty at A2.1.
Q10
You are in a job interview. You look at the interviewer and your first impression is: this person doesn't like me. What do you do?
Simple scenario, real professional stakes. Options: try harder to connect, stay calm and professional, feel nervous and show it. Push the student to role-play 2-3 sentences of what they would actually say or do. Great for professional English at A2.1 level.
