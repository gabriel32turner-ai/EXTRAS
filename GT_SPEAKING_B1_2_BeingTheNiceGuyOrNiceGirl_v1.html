Being the Nice Guy or Nice Girl

Kind, helpful, always available — and always last on everyone's list. Is being too nice actually a problem?

🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
⏱ 4 min
"People who struggle to say 'no' are more likely to experience burnout, resentment, and anxiety. Psychologists say that excessive niceness is often not generosity — it's a fear of conflict and a need for approval that makes you easy to take advantage of."
— Psychology Today / Harvard Business Review
Teacher script: "Honest question — are you a 'yes' person? Someone who finds it really hard to say no? Or do you know someone like that?" [Pause.] "Today we're going to talk about whether being too nice is actually a personality strength — or a trap you fall into."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever done something for someone else — at work or in your personal life — when you really didn't want to, just to avoid saying no?
Universal experience — nearly everyone has done this. Present perfect is natural here. Model: "Yes, I've stayed late at work many times because I felt too guilty to say I had plans."
Q2
Have you ever felt used by a friend or colleague who kept asking for favors but never helped you back?
The "one-way friendship" pattern. Students often have a very specific person in mind. Let them tell the story. Don't rush — this is the most engaging part of the topic.
Q3
Have you ever said yes to something — and then regretted it immediately?
The "yes regret" moment. Very relatable. Model: "I've agreed to take on extra work and then sat in my car wishing I had said no." Light tone, good for humor — don't let it become too heavy too fast.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
In your opinion, is it possible to be too nice — or is kindness always a positive quality?
Core question. Push them to distinguish between kindness (healthy) and people-pleasing (fear-based). Model: "I think you can be too nice if it means you never put yourself first."
Q5
What do you think — do people respect you more when you say no, or when you always help?
Counter-intuitive insight: "no" often earns more respect than "yes." Good for real examples. "I think that in my experience, when you always say yes, people start to assume it and stop appreciating it."
Q6
Do you think it is harder for women to say no than for men — because of how society sees them?
Gender and social expectations angle. In Mexican professional culture this is very real. Let women in the class lead this one if possible. No wrong answer — both perspectives are valid.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if a coworker kept asking you to cover their tasks because they know you never say no?
Second conditional. Very concrete professional scenario. "If a coworker kept doing this... I would..." Push: "How many times would you do it before you said something? Once? Five times? Never?"
Q8
What would you do if your boss gave you extra work every Friday — and you wanted to say no but were scared of the consequences?
Power dynamics + niceness. The workplace version of the problem. "If my boss did that... I would probably say yes but feel terrible." Honest answer is often "I would say yes anyway" — and that is a great conversation starter.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
If someone is always nice and never complains, is it their fault when people take advantage of them?
This question sounds harsh — and it is meant to. Some students will say yes, some no. The truth is uncomfortable. Push: "At what point does being kind become enabling bad behavior in others?"
Q10
Are you a people-pleaser? And if you are — do you want to change it, or do you think it is part of who you are?
The self-reflection bomb. This is where students recognize themselves in the topic. Create a safe space — "there is no shame in it, but let us be honest." The best sessions end here with real personal revelation.

The Exit Shot
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
