🪝 Phase 1 — The Trigger
The Headline
Read it aloud. Then ask the first question.
4 min
01
BBC Health · 2023
"Ultra-processed foods now make up more than 50% of what the average person eats every day — and most people say they know it's bad but can't stop."
— Knowing something is bad and actually stopping are two completely different things.
The Guardian · Food
"Junk food is engineered to be addictive. Food scientists are paid specifically to find the exact amount of salt, sugar, and fat that makes you want more. You were never really in control."
— Which is either a relief or a little terrifying. Maybe both.
Launch line: "Quick question — what's the one junk food you could never, ever give up? Not even for your health. The one thing that, if someone told you to stop eating it forever, you'd seriously think about it." [Let them answer.] "That one. Today we talk about that one." This opener always gets an immediate, enthusiastic response.

Provocateur rationale: Fernando dismisses addiction language around food and places full responsibility on the individual. Push them: "Is Fernando 100% wrong — or does he have even a small point?"
🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Personal and honest. Show tips only after the student answers.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
What is the one junk food you absolutely cannot live without? Describe it — what it is, when you eat it, and how it makes you feel.
The central confession question. Model first: "Mine is chips — specifically the spicy ones. I eat them at night, in front of the TV, and I always finish the whole bag even though I tell myself I won't." Let the student go long. Activate: craving, comfort food, snack, guilt.
Q2
Have you ever tried to quit a junk food and failed? How many times?
Present perfect — everyone has this story. Model: "I've tried to stop eating fast food maybe five or six times. The longest I lasted was three weeks. Then someone in the office ordered McDonald's and I was done." Activate: give up, cheat, start over, resist.
Q3
Have you ever hidden junk food from your family, your partner, or your kids? Tell me about it.
Funny and very personal. Most students have done this. Common: chocolate in a drawer, chips in the car, secret candy stash. The fact that they hide it reveals they know it's wrong — and they do it anyway. Activate: hide, guilt, temptation.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think junk food is actually addictive — like a real addiction? Or is that just an excuse?
The Trigger + Fernando's argument. Most B1.1 students will say "a bit of both." Push: "Have you ever felt like you physically needed to eat something — not just wanted it?" The science (from the Trigger) supports real addiction pathways. Activate: craving, temptation, habit.
Q5
Is it OK to eat junk food every day if you also exercise? Does exercise cancel it out?
Diego's argument. The honest answer: not really, no — but many people use this logic. Model: "I think it helps, but it doesn't cancel it out completely. Your body still has to process all that sugar and salt. But mentally, exercising makes me feel better about it." Good for modals: "You should exercise anyway, but..."
Q6
Do you think food companies are responsible for making junk food addictive — or is it 100% the person's fault?
References the second Trigger quote. Most students will split the blame. Good for "I think... but I also think..." structures at B1.1. Activate: temptation, label, resist. The most thought-provoking opinion question of the block.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your doctor told you to stop eating your favorite junk food completely — forever?
Second conditional with real medical stakes. Most say they would stop — push: "Would you really? Or would you maybe eat it less but not stop completely?" Honest answers are more interesting. Model: "If my doctor said that, I would probably stop for 6 months — and then slowly start again." Activate: give up, resist, craving, guilt.
Q8
What would your life look like if you ate perfectly healthy for one full year — no junk food at all? Would you be happier?
Imaginative second conditional. Most students describe feeling great physically but missing the pleasure. The follow-up: "Do you think you would miss the food — or just the feeling it gave you?" Activate: comfort food, craving, deserve, habit.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — when do you eat the most junk food? When you're happy, bored, stressed, or sad?
This question connects food to emotion — the real root of most junk food habits. Most students say "stressed" or "bored." The follow-up: "So is the junk food about the taste — or about the feeling?" Activate: comfort food, craving, temptation, stress.
Q10
If you could send a message to your 20-year-old self about food, what would you say?
Nostalgic closing. Light but personal — students often surprise themselves with what they say. Common answers: "Eat more vegetables," "Stop eating so much fast food," or the unexpected "Enjoy it more while your metabolism still works." All three are great B1.1 answers. Activate: habit, diet, start over.
B1.1 grammar check: Q2–Q3 = present perfect. Q7–Q8 = second conditional ("What would you do if...? / I would..."). Keep it simple. Q10 works naturally with "I would tell myself to..." which is a great conditional structure.

Energy note: This is a very high-energy topic — everyone participates. Q9 is where the session gets deeper. Q10 is a warm, nostalgic close. Let both breathe.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. One honest answer.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
Your doctor gives you two options: Option A — you can eat whatever you want for the rest of your life, but you will probably have health problems at 60. Option B — you stop eating junk food completely starting tomorrow, and you will most likely live 10 years longer. Which do you choose — and why?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Tell me about your worst junk food habit. When do you eat it? Have you ever tried to stop? What happened?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: The choice is stark, concrete, and impossible to answer vaguely — it forces the student to reveal how much they actually value food pleasure vs. long-term health. Most say Option B... but many hesitate. That hesitation is the conversation.

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about stress — the real reason most of us eat badly, sleep badly, and feel tired all the time. Very connected to today."
