The Opinion Wall
Four real takes. Pick one. Defend it.

Sofía, 34
Financial Analyst · Monterrey
"I've been studying markets for ten years. Bitcoin is not an investment — it's a speculation instrument with no underlying cash flow, no dividend, no real use case beyond trading. "I put 15% of my savings into Bitcoin three years ago and it's up 200%. Meanwhile my savings account in pesos lost value every single year because of inflation. Tell me again why traditional finance is safer. At least with crypto I'm betting on something that can't be printed by a government."

Economics Professor · Guadalajara

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real talk. No right answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever bought crypto, considered it, or actively decided not to? What was your reasoning?
Push for specifics: when, how much, what happened. If they've never considered it: "Why not?" — is it risk aversion, lack of knowledge, or genuine skepticism?
Q2
Do you know anyone who's made a lot of money in crypto? What was their story — and what do you think about it?
Watch for envy vs. admiration vs. skepticism. Great vocabulary moment: "They got lucky" vs. "They took a calculated risk." Ask which one they believe.
Q3
Has inflation in Mexico ever pushed you to think differently about how you save or invest your money?
This bridges to Bitcoin's use case for emerging markets. Let them connect the dots. Follow up: "So does that make Andrés's argument more convincing?"
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think crypto is closer to a legitimate financial revolution or just sophisticated gambling? Why?
Model answer: "I think it's somewhere in between. The technology behind it — blockchain — is genuinely useful. But most of what people are trading is pure speculation. It's gambling with better branding."
Q5
Should companies and governments hold Bitcoin as a reserve asset, the way they hold gold? What would be the consequences?
Push for consequences on both sides: stability, legitimacy, volatility risk. El Salvador made it legal tender — how did that go? Let them research or guess.
Q6
Is it fair that people who got in early made millions while people who invested late lost everything? Who's responsible for that?
Great ethical debate. Is it the early investors' fault? The influencers who promoted it? The buyers who didn't do their research? Roberto's argument lives here.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
If Bitcoin reaches $500,000 in the next five years, would you regret not having invested? What would you do differently now?
Force a commitment. "So would you put in $1,000 today?" If they say yes: what stops them? If no: what would change their mind? Great use of second conditional.
Q8
If your employer offered to pay part of your salary in Bitcoin, would you accept? Under what conditions?
Model answer: "If it were 10% of my salary and the rest was guaranteed in pesos, I'd probably try it for a year. But if it were 50%? Absolutely not — I have rent and groceries."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Roberto says the real scam is the traditional financial system. Do you agree with any part of that? What's your honest take?
The hottest question. Don't let them dodge it. A comfortable answer: "Well, both systems have problems." Push: "But which one has hurt you more personally?"
Q10
Be honest: is your position on Bitcoin based on logic and research — or on your emotions, your social circle, and what you've seen happen to others?
The self-reflection closer. Most people are influenced by their social environment more than they admit. The goal isn't to shame — it's to build real critical thinking. Celebrate honesty.
