Cheap Hotels vs.
Expensive Hotels

Do you really need to pay a lot to sleep well — or is a clean, simple room enough?

🪝
The Trigger
Open the Conversation
⏱ 4 min
A question for the class
"Think about the last hotel you stayed in. Was it cheap or expensive? Was it good or terrible? Did the price tell you the truth — or did it lie to you?"
🎙 Teacher Script

Read the three questions slowly. Ask for a quick show of hands: "Who here prefers to save money on hotels?" Then: "Who likes to pay for a good hotel?" Count the hands — usually it divides the room in half. Then say: "OK — let's talk about hotels." Simple, fast, no pressure.

The Conversation Engine
23 Minutes of Real Talk
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Did you ever stay in a hotel that was terrible? What was the problem?
Adjective practice: dirty, small, noisy, dark, ugly, smelly, far from everything. Let them describe. Help with: "The room was... / The bathroom was... / The bed was..." Most students have a story and love to tell it.
Q2
Did you ever stay in a very nice hotel? What did you like about it?
Model answer: "Yes, one time my company paid for a very nice hotel in Cancún. I didn't want to leave! The bed was amazing, the breakfast was huge, and the view was beautiful." Keep the description concrete and fun.
Q3
When was the last time you traveled and stayed in a hotel? What was it like?
Simple past simple recall. Help students sequence: "First I arrived... then I checked in... the room was... I liked / didn't like..." Two to three sentences is a complete and successful answer at A2.2.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
What is more important in a hotel: the price, the location, or the cleanliness?
Three options make it easy to answer. Most students choose cleanliness — it's a universal answer. Ask: "What happens if the hotel is cheap and clean but very far from everything?" That creates a real decision moment.
Q5
Do you think expensive hotels are always better — or is a cheap hotel sometimes the right choice?
The core debate question. Gabriela's answer is often the most honest: "No, not always." Ask: "When is a cheap hotel the right choice? When is it a bad idea?" Context matters — business trip vs. backpacking are very different.
Q6
Do you read hotel reviews online before you book? Do you trust them?
Universal topic — everyone uses TripAdvisor, Google, or Booking.com now. Ask: "How many stars is enough for you? Only 5? Or 4 is OK?" Students have strong opinions about review scores. Fun and personal.
Block 3 — Future Plans
Q7
Are you going to travel this year? If yes, what kind of hotel are you going to choose?
Direct "going to" practice. Even "I don't have plans to travel this year" is a valid and useful answer — help them say it in English: "I'm not going to travel this year because..." Budget, family, work — all real reasons.
Q8
If you win a vacation trip — the hotel is free — will you choose a 5-star hotel or a simple, clean hostel near everything?
Simple "will" practice with a fun imaginary scenario. Most students choose 5 stars without hesitation — but then ask: "Why? What do you want to do there?" Seeing what students value in luxury is always revealing.
Block 4 — Used To & Reflection
Q9
Did you use to travel and stay in cheap hotels when you were younger? Is that different now?
Good "used to" practice with a clear before-and-after structure. Model answer: "I used to sleep anywhere — a friend's sofa, a very cheap room, no problem. Now I need a real bed and a quiet room. I got old!" Relatable and fun.
Q10
Did you use to think that expensive hotels were always amazing? What do you think now?
Nice closing reflection — connects "used to think" with current reality. Model answer: "I used to think a 5-star hotel was perfect. Now I think: it depends. Sometimes a small, clean hotel is better. Price is not everything." Great session closer.
💣
The Exit Shot
One Question. Simple but Real.
⏱ 5 min
🎯 Final Question
Your company is going to pay for your hotel on a business trip. You can choose: a very nice 5-star hotel, or a simple but clean hotel — and you keep the difference in money. What do you choose — and why?

Voice Note Homework
Record a 60-second voice note in English on WhatsApp. Answer this question: what do you choose — the 5-star hotel or the simple hotel and the money? Be specific. Why did you choose that? Your teacher will listen and give you feedback before the next session.
