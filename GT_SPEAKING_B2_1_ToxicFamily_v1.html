Cutting Toxic Family Members Off
In Latin culture, family loyalty is almost sacred. So what happens when the person causing you the most damage is your own blood?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One insight that lands harder than a statistic.
⏱ 4 min
Psychology Today · Dr. Kristina Coop Gordon · 2022
"The fact that someone is family does not make them safe. And safety — emotional, psychological, and sometimes physical — is not a privilege. It's a requirement for human wellbeing."
Research on family estrangement — the deliberate decision to cut off or limit contact with a family member — shows it is far more common than most people admit. A 2020 University of Cambridge study found that 27% of Americans are estranged from a family member. Yet the topic remains heavily stigmatized, especially in cultures where family loyalty is treated as an absolute moral value.
Teacher Script Read the quote slowly. Then pause. Then say: "Before we talk about whether you agree or disagree — does this feel like a new idea to you, or have you heard something like this before?" In Latin cultures, this quote will land differently than in an American context. Let the cultural tension breathe. Don't rush to the discussion. The discomfort is the lesson.

"Blood is thicker than water."
The most common argument against cutting family off — and the one people use to make you feel guilty for protecting yourself.
"You only get one family."
Said to someone who is exhausted, hurt, and seriously considering cutting contact. Rarely helpful.
"That's just how they are."
The phrase that normalizes bad behavior from family members and teaches you to accept it.
"I'm done making excuses for them."
The sentence someone says right before they finally decide to stop tolerating the behavior.
"You can love someone and still walk away."
The hardest truth about family estrangement — and the one that gives people permission to protect themselves.

Phase 3
The Opinion Wall
Four positions on one of the most taboo decisions a person can make. Choose carefully.
⏱ 10 min
💪
Marcela, 38
Entrepreneur · Guadalajara
"I cut my mother off three years ago. It was the hardest thing I've ever done. And it was also the best decision I've ever made. She was emotionally abusive my entire life — and everyone around me kept telling me 'but she's your mom.' The day I stopped taking her calls, I started sleeping without anxiety for the first time in 15 years. I don't need anyone's approval for that."
🙏
Jorge, 55
Retired Teacher · Celaya
"I understand that families can be difficult. But completely cutting someone off — especially a parent — is something you can never take back. What happens when they get sick? What happens when they die? The guilt of not being there will follow you forever. I believe you can protect yourself and still maintain contact. Distance is not the same as abandonment."
🧠
Daniela, 33
Psychologist · Querétaro
"In my practice, I've worked with people on both sides of this. What I've found is that estrangement is rarely dramatic or sudden. It's usually a last resort, after years of trying everything else. The people who make this decision don't do it lightly. What's actually harmful is the cultural pressure to forgive and maintain contact regardless of the cost to your own mental health."
🔪
Tomás, 44
Business Consultant · CDMX
⚡ Provocateur
"I'm going to say what nobody wants to say: the 'toxic family' narrative is partly a product of therapy culture and social media. Every difficult relationship is now called abuse. Every imperfect parent is a narcissist. People are using these labels to justify running away from relationships that require real work. Not every uncomfortable family dynamic is a reason to burn everything down."
Discussion prompt: "Which of these four people would you be most afraid to disagree with in real life? Which one do you think is the most wrong?" Tomás is the provocateur — his argument is culturally provocative because it mirrors what many Mexican families actually say. Some students will agree with him more than they'd like to admit. That discomfort is exactly right.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Handle with care. This is real territory.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever had a family relationship that felt consistently draining, painful, or unfair — not just occasionally, but as a pattern?
Teacher Tip This opener is careful — "have you ever" gives them distance. They don't have to name anyone. Listen for the vocabulary: toxic dynamic, enable, guilt. Don't push for names or details. The experience is what matters, not the identity of the person.
Q2
Think of someone in your life who you've had to limit contact with — for any reason, family or not. What made you finally take that step?
Teacher Tip "Any reason" broadens it — they can talk about a friend, colleague, or acquaintance, which keeps it safe. But often students will go straight to family. Listen for the moment of decision — what was the final event? That's the heart of the story.
Q3
Have you ever felt judged — by family, friends, or society — for protecting yourself from someone? What was that like?
Teacher Tip This surfaces the cultural layer: in Mexico and Latin America broadly, protecting yourself from family often comes with enormous social cost. Listen for the internalized guilt — "I know I shouldn't feel bad but I do." That's the core tension of this topic.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is there a meaningful difference between "toxic" and "difficult"? Where's the line between a relationship worth fixing and one worth leaving?
Teacher Tip This directly challenges Tomás's argument from the Opinion Wall. Push them to give criteria, not just feelings. What specific behaviors cross the line? Consistent gaslighting? Emotional abuse? Or is it purely subjective? Best students will say: the difference is whether the harmful person is willing and able to change.
Q5
Do you think the obligation to maintain family relationships is genuinely moral — or is it a cultural expectation that we've confused with ethics?
Teacher Tip This is the philosophical core of the session. In many cultures, family loyalty is treated as an intrinsic moral value — but is it? Or is it a social norm that benefits those in power within families? Push for a real position, not a diplomatic non-answer.
Q6
Tomás argued that "toxic family" is partly a product of therapy culture — that we're labeling too much as abuse. Is he completely wrong?
Teacher Tip The spiciest question — because "completely wrong" invites nuance. Some students will reluctantly agree that there's overcorrection in therapy culture. Others will push back hard. Both are fine. The goal is a position + a reason + an acknowledgment of the counterargument.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If a close friend told you they were thinking about cutting off a parent who had consistently hurt them, what would you say — and what would you avoid saying?
Teacher Tip Second conditional + empathy language. The "what would you avoid" part is key — it forces them to confront the platitudes: "but she's your mom," "blood is thicker than water," "you'll regret it." Great for the colloquial phrases from Phase 2.
Q8
If you knew for certain you would feel better in 3 years but worse for the next 12 months — would you still cut someone toxic off today?
Teacher Tip Second conditional + will. This question tests how much they actually believe in long-term thinking versus short-term pain avoidance. Most students know intellectually that they should. The real answer is often more complicated. Don't judge it — just explore it.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Are there people in your family right now whose behavior you are tolerating — not because it's okay, but because the alternative feels too complicated?
Teacher Tip This question often produces silence first. That silence is the answer. Don't rush to fill it. If they do answer, listen for the "enable" pattern — staying in the dynamic because leaving seems harder than staying. That's the real thing to explore.
Q10 · Self-Reflection
Have you ever been the difficult family member in someone else's story — the one they might have needed to distance themselves from?
Teacher Tip The hardest question of the session — because it inverts the narrative. All conversation so far has positioned the student as the one who might need to walk away. This asks them to consider the other role. Give them space. Don't push if they're not ready. But for students who engage with it, this produces the most authentic and memorable conversation of the lab.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. No comfortable answer. Record your response.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · B2.1 Cutting Toxic Family Off
Your sibling has been emotionally manipulative your entire life — 35 years of it. You've tried everything: conversations, therapy, distance. Nothing changes. They just had a baby. The baby has nothing to do with any of this. Do you cut your sibling off — knowing that decision also means missing that child growing up?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. What do you do — and why? And if you're not sure, tell me that too. Uncertainty is a real answer.
Retention Hook The baby detail is what makes this exit shot exceptional — it adds an innocent third party that has nothing to do with the conflict, which is exactly how these situations play out in real life. The student can't say "protect yourself" without also saying "miss the baby." There's no clean answer. End with: "Send me your voice note. I'll listen before our next session — and I want to hear your actual opinion, not the one you think sounds right."
