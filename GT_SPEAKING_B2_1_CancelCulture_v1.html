Cancel Culture:
Justice or Witch Hunt?
One tweet. One mistake. One lifetime of consequences. Is that accountability — or mob rule?
🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocation & Opening
⏱ 4 min
Pew Research Center · 2023
"44% of Americans believe cancel culture has gone too far — while 38% say it doesn't go far enough. The remaining 18% aren't sure. Neither are most of us."
Teacher script: Read it. Then: "Before we talk about celebrities — think about your own world. Have you ever seen someone lose their job, their reputation, or their social circle because of something they said? Was it fair?" Pause ten seconds. Then: "Today we're not talking about Hollywood. We're talking about how accountability works — and when it stops being justice."

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever changed your opinion about a public figure because of something they said online? Did you stop following them — or did you give them a second chance?
Teacher Tip Push for a specific person and a specific moment. "Who was it? What did they say? Did you feel pressure from others to react the same way — or did you form your own opinion?"
Q2
Have you ever stayed silent about your real opinion on a sensitive topic at work or online — because you were afraid of how people would react?
Model Answer "Yes — more than once. There are things I genuinely believe that I keep to myself because the cost of saying them out loud feels too high. I don't know if that's wisdom or self-censorship. Probably both."
Q3
Do you know someone whose reputation was seriously damaged by something they said — online or in person? Looking back, was the reaction proportional?
Teacher Tip Keep focus on proportion, not guilt or innocence. "Would you say the punishment matched the offense? Would your answer change if it happened to you?"
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is there a meaningful difference between holding someone accountable and trying to destroy them? Where exactly is the line?
Teacher Tip Push them past agreeing the line exists. "Name the line. What specific behavior crosses from accountability into destruction? Give me a concrete example."
Q5
Should things people said 10 or 15 years ago be used to judge who they are today? Does the age they were at the time matter?
Model Answer "Context matters enormously. A 19-year-old who said something offensive in 2009 and a 45-year-old who says the same thing today are in completely different situations. The internet doesn't age the way people do — and that's a serious design flaw."
Q6
Has cancel culture made people more careful about what they actually think — or just more careful about what they say publicly?
Teacher Tip The distinction between genuine change and performance is where the conversation gets real. "Do people actually think differently now — or just talk differently? And which is more valuable?"
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If a colleague at work was publicly called out online for something offensive they said years ago — and your company was deciding whether to fire them — what would you recommend?
Model Answer "I'd want the full story before recommending anything. What did they say? Have they addressed it? Do they still hold those views? Firing someone based on a screenshot without any conversation feels like exactly the mob mentality we should be moving past."
Q8
If you were cancelled tomorrow for something you said five years ago — taken out of context — what would you do? Who do you think would actually stand by you?
Teacher Tip The second part — who would stand by you — reveals more than the first. "How many people? And how many would stay quiet to protect themselves? What does that tell you about the culture you work in?"
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Have you ever joined a pile-on — liked, shared, or commented to publicly criticize someone — and later felt uncomfortable about it? What did that feel like?
Teacher Tip The vulnerability question. Most people have done this. Give space. "What does it feel like to be part of a crowd judging someone? Did the crowd feel righteous? Did you feel righteous — and what happened to that feeling later?"
Q10
If cancel culture disappeared tomorrow — no public callouts, no viral consequences — do you think powerful people would behave better or worse? And what does your answer say about why it exists?
Model Answer "Honestly, worse. A lot of people behave better because consequences exist. That doesn't mean every cancellation is fair — but zero social accountability wasn't working either. The problem isn't accountability. It's proportionality. And we haven't figured out how to calibrate that yet."

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · Final Dilemma · No right answer
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot
A tweet from 2012 resurfaces. It was written by your best friend — now a respected professional with a family. It's offensive by today's standards, but they were 24 and it was a different time. You know they've changed completely. A journalist contacts you for a quote. Your friend's employer is already aware and considering termination. Do you defend them publicly — knowing it could damage your own reputation — or do you stay silent and protect yourself?
📱 WhatsApp Homework Record 60 seconds in English: What do you do — and why? Is loyalty to a person the same as loyalty to what they once said?
