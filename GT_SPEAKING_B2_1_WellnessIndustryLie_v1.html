B2.1 Health & Wellness 🌶 Spicy 50 min

The Wellness Industry Lie

Is the $5 trillion wellness industry actually making us healthier — or just making us anxious, broke, and obsessed?

🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
⏱ 4 min
"The global wellness market is worth $5.6 trillion — and growing. But research shows that most wellness products have little to no scientific backing, and the industry profits from making people feel perpetually unwell."
— Harvard Business Review / Global Wellness Institute, 2023
Teacher script: "Before we start — how much did you spend on wellness last month? Supplements, gym, apps, anything. Don't answer yet. Just think about it." [Pause 5 seconds.] "Now — how much healthier are you because of it? That's what we're talking about today."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever bought a wellness product or service that didn't actually work? What happened?
Push for specifics: Was it a supplement? App? Retreat? Did they feel embarrassed? This often triggers honest, funny stories that open up real conversation.
Q2
Do you have a wellness routine? What's in it — and what made you start?
Great for present simple + frequency adverbs. Listen for "I do it every day" — follow up with "What happens if you miss a day? Do you feel guilty?"
Q3
Has your relationship with wellness changed in the last 5 years? What's different now compared to before?
Natural present perfect trigger. Model answer: "It has changed a lot. I used to ignore everything, but now I've become more aware — maybe too aware."
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think wellness influencers do more harm than good? Why?
Expect polarized answers. If student agrees, ask: "But some people genuinely changed because of them, right?" If they disagree, ask: "What about the ones promoting unproven supplements to sick people?"
Q5
Is the wellness industry exploiting people's insecurities — or responding to a real need?
This is the core tension of the topic. Strong students can say "both." Model: "I think it does both — there's a real need for stress management, but the industry monetizes that need aggressively."
Q6
Should companies be legally required to prove their wellness claims before selling? Or is that too much regulation?
Great for modals of obligation: "They should / shouldn't have to..." Connects to professional world — many students work in regulated industries.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
If you found out your favorite wellness product was basically a placebo, would you stop using it — even if you felt better with it?
Second conditional. The "placebo paradox" — if it works, does it matter why? Encourage: "If I found out... I would probably..." This is philosophically rich and genuinely divides people.
Q8
If your boss offered you free therapy sessions as a company benefit, would you use them — or would it feel too personal?
Bridges health and work. Reveals attitudes toward vulnerability in professional settings. Many Mexican professionals feel stigma around mental health — this surfaces it naturally.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Is wellness culture a luxury for people who can afford it — and just another way to make poor people feel like they're failing at life?
Spicy socioeconomic angle. "You can't practice self-care if you work two jobs." Great for strong opinions. If student hesitates, push: "Do you think wellness is truly accessible to everyone in Mexico?"
Q10
Honestly — when you take care of yourself, is it for your health, or is part of it about how it looks to other people?
Self-reflection bomb. The honest answer for most people is "both." This question creates real intimacy in the classroom. Don't rush through it. Silence after this is productive.
💣

The Exit Shot
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
You have a chronic health issue. Your doctor recommends a treatment that costs $200/month. An influencer promotes a "natural alternative" for $60/month with thousands of five-star reviews and no clinical evidence. Your insurance covers neither. What do you do?
