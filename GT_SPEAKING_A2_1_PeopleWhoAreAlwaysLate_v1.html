People Who Are Always Late

They always have an excuse. You always wait. Why is it so hard for some people to be on time?

🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
⏱ 4 min
"Studies show that chronically late people waste an average of 30 minutes per day of other people's time. That is more than 180 hours a year. People who wait say the number one feeling is: disrespect."
— Harvard Business Review / Time Management Study
Teacher script: "Do you know someone who is ALWAYS late? Always with a different excuse?" [Pause.] "Today we talk about late people — why it makes us angry, and what we can do about it. And maybe — be honest — are YOU sometimes late too?"

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
When was the last time someone was very late and you waited for them? What happened?
Past simple narrative. "Last week, my friend was 40 minutes late. I waited at the restaurant. I ate the bread. I was angry." Short, connected sentences. Let the story develop — ask: "And what did you say when they arrived?"
Q2
Did you ever miss something important because someone else was late?
Past simple. "Yes, one time my friend was late and we missed the movie / the bus / the first part of the event." If they say no — ask: "Did you ever arrive late and cause a problem for someone else?" Keeps the energy going either way.
Q3
What was the worst excuse a late person gave you? Did you believe it?
Pure storytelling fun. "One time a person said..." Common funny excuses: "My dog was sick," "I forgot my keys three times," "My neighbor wanted to talk." Model: "My friend said a chicken crossed the road and stopped traffic. I did not believe it."
Block 2 — Opinion (Simple Present)
Q4
Do you think being late is disrespectful — or is it just a personality type?
Core opinion. "I think it is disrespectful because..." / "I think some people are just not good with time..." Fernando vs Mateo tension. No complex grammar — just "I think" + simple sentence. Strong opinions come naturally here.
Q5
Is it okay to start a meeting or dinner without the late person — or do you always wait?
Practical opinion. "I think it is okay to start because..." / "I always wait because..." Can + should modal if they are ready: "You should start without them." Good quick debate — most people have a strong preference here.
Q6
What do you think is a good solution for a friend who is always late?
Solution-focused. "I think a good solution is..." Andrea's "fake time" trick is useful here. Push for creative answers: leave without them, tell them a fake time, stop inviting them, talk to them directly. Good for will: "I will tell them..."
Block 3 — Future Plans (Going To / Will)
Q7
The next time a friend is very late — what are you going to do?
Going to for plans. "Next time, I'm going to..." Leave? Say something? Order food without them? Start without them? Students often decide in this moment what they will really do. Very concrete future planning in English.
Q8
Are you going to try to be more on time in the future — or is that too difficult for you?
Going to + self-honesty. "Yes, I'm going to try because..." / "No, it's too difficult because I..." The honest answer often gets a laugh from the class. Mateo's "I have a lot to do" defense is their escape route — let them use it.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — are YOU sometimes the late person? What is your usual excuse?
Self-reflection. The mirror moment. Most people say "sometimes" — nobody says always late. But everyone has a go-to excuse. Model: "Yes, sometimes I am late. My excuse is always traffic. But sometimes it is true!" Said with a smile — creates honesty and laughter.
Q10
Your best friend is always late. Today they are 1 hour late for YOUR birthday dinner. They arrive with a big smile and say "Sorry! Traffic!" What do you say to them?
Scenario with will. "I will say..." Role-play moment. Some students say "I will smile and say nothing." Others say "I will be honest this time." The birthday detail raises the emotional stakes just enough without being too heavy. Very A2.1 appropriate.
