B1.1 Daily Life & Lifestyle 🌶 Spicy 50 min

Living With Roommates From Hell

Dirty dishes, loud music, missing money, no privacy. Everyone has a roommate story. What is yours?

🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
⏱ 4 min
"In a survey of 2,000 adults, 72% said they had at least one roommate they would describe as 'difficult' or 'impossible to live with.' The most common complaints: dirty spaces, loud noise at night, and not paying rent on time."
— YouGov Survey / The Guardian, 2022
Teacher script: "Okay — hands up if you have ever lived with a roommate." [Pause.] "Now keep your hand up if that roommate was a little bit... crazy." [Laugh.] "Today we are going to share our worst roommate stories. Don't be shy — the worse, the better."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever lived with a roommate? What was the best or worst part?
Great opener — everyone has a strong opinion. If they never had a roommate, ask: "Would you? Why or why not?" Present perfect + past simple will come naturally. Let the story develop.
Q2
Have you ever had a big argument with someone you lived with? What happened?
Past simple narrative — "We argued because..." "One day he/she..." This is where great stories come out. Don't rush. Encourage details: "And then what happened?"
Q3
Did you ever put up with a bad situation at home for too long before doing something about it?
The "why do we wait?" angle. Most students have done this. Model: "Yes, I put up with a really messy roommate for four months before I finally said something." Good for past simple + adverbs of time.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
What do you think is the most important rule when you live with someone else?
Good for modals: "You should always... / You must never..." Expect answers like: keep it clean, be quiet at night, pay rent on time. Ask: "What happens if someone breaks that rule?"
Q5
Is it better to live with a close friend or with a stranger? Why?
Classic debate. Many students have learned the hard way that friends can become enemies when you live together. "It is better to... because..." Good for comparative structures and "in my experience."
Q6
Do you think it is okay to eat your roommate's food without asking? What about using their things?
The "small crimes" of shared living. Very relatable. Generates funny stories. Modal: "You should always ask first." "It is never okay to take..." Expect strong opinions.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your roommate stopped paying rent and had no money?
Second conditional. Very concrete situation — loyalty vs money. "If my roommate stopped paying rent... I would..." Push: "How long would you give them? One month? Two? What if they were your best friend?"
Q8
What would you do if you came home and found your roommate had a party in your house without asking you?
Fun and relatable scenario. "If I came home and found a party... I would..." Expect range from "I would join the party" to "I would ask them to leave immediately." Both authentic — great for debate.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Are you a good roommate? Be honest — what do you do that could bother other people?
Self-reflection. The twist: we always blame the other person. This asks students to consider their own behavior. Model: "I think I am mostly good, but I am quite loud in the morning and I forget to clean the bathroom sometimes."
Q10
Have you ever been the "difficult" roommate — and only realized it later?
The Ricardo moment. Like the chef in the Opinion Wall — sometimes we are the problem. This can generate surprising honesty and humor. Allow silence. The best answer is often the most honest one.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
Your best friend needs a place to live. They ask to stay with you for "just one month." It has now been four months. They are messy, loud, and they eat your food. But they are your best friend and they have nowhere to go. What do you do?
