Is Long-Distance
Love Real?

You love each other. You live in different cities — or different countries. Can that actually work? Or is distance just a slow goodbye?

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · True Story Starter
⏱ 4 min
Statistic Brain Research Institute · Long-Distance Relationships · 2023
"About 14 million couples in the US say they are in a long-distance relationship. 40% of them break up. But the 60% who make it work say the relationship was actually stronger because of the distance — they learned to really talk."
Teacher Script: Read the stat. Then ask: "40% break up — does that number surprise you? Or did you think it would be higher?" Then: "Have you ever had a long-distance relationship — or do you know someone who did? What happened?" The goal is immediate personal connection. If the student has no personal story, ask: "Do you think you could do it — or is it impossible for you?"

Provocateur
🧔
Fernando · 40
Sales Manager · Querétaro
"Long-distance is not a relationship. It is a promise. You are not in love with a person — you are in love with an idea of a person. When you finally live together, reality is different. Many couples break up when the distance ends — not when it starts."
Teacher Note: Fernando is the provocateur because his position is counterintuitive — he says the danger isn't the distance itself but what happens when it ends. Push: "Do you agree — is it possible to fall in love with an idea of a person?" Use Andrea to challenge Fernando: "But Andrea is still with her husband after 15 years. Is Fernando wrong?"

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever been in a long-distance relationship — or know someone who has? What happened in the end?
Open the story. Let the student tell it — even if it's a friend's story. Push: "How long was the distance? How did they communicate? Did it work?" Target: past simple for the story + present perfect to open. Key vocab: visit, call, miss, wait, end.
Q2
Have you ever missed someone so much it was physically painful? What did you do?
More emotional, personal question. Can be a partner, friend, or family member who moved away. Push: "What did you do with that feeling — call them? Distract yourself? Work more?" Target: present perfect + past simple for coping strategies. Key vocab: miss, call, cry, distract, wait.
Q3
Did you ever have a relationship — or friendship — that changed when one person moved to a different city?
Broadens the question beyond romance to include friendships — more accessible for students who haven't had LDRs. Push: "Was the change positive or negative?" Target: past simple + comparative (better/worse/different). Key: validate that change is not always bad.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Do you believe long-distance relationships can really work — or are they always temporary?
Push: "What conditions would make it work — or not work?" Target: can/can't + it depends on... Model: "I think it can work if both people have a plan — like 'we will live in the same city in 1 year.' Without a plan, it's just waiting." Key vocab: plan, temporary, commitment, trust.
Q5
Is jealousy worse in a long-distance relationship — or is trust more important?
Good for activating: jealous, trust, worry, imagine. Push: "What would make you feel jealous in a long-distance relationship?" Target: comparative (more/less jealous) + would for hypothesis. Model: "I think jealousy is worse because you can't see what the person is doing. You imagine things. Imagination is the problem."
Q6
Is it OK to start a relationship knowing from the beginning that it will be long-distance?
Good for: it's OK / it's not OK + because. Push: "Or is it better to not start and avoid the pain?" Target: it's OK to + infinitive. Model: "I think it's OK if you both know what you're getting into. But starting a relationship that you know will hurt — I don't know."
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your partner got a great job offer in another country — and asked you to wait 2 years?
Target: second conditional (would + base verb). Push: "Would it depend on how long you had been together?" Model: "I think I would try — but only if we had a clear end date. '2 years and then we live together.' Without a plan, 2 years becomes 4 and then it's over." Get the student to commit to a specific answer.
Q8
What would you do if you were in a long-distance relationship and you started to have feelings for someone in your city?
The most honest hypothetical. Target: second conditional. Push: "Would you tell your long-distance partner? Or would you try to make the feelings go away first?" This is where Fernando's "you're in love with an idea" argument becomes very real. No judgment — just curiosity about what the student would actually do.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Fernando says long-distance is not a relationship — it's a promise. Do you agree with that?
Direct engagement with the provocateur. Push: "What's the difference between a relationship and a promise?" Target: present simple for opinion + contrast (but, however, on the other hand). Good for 3-4 exchanges. Use Andrea's story to challenge: "But Andrea has been married 15 years — was that just a promise?"
Q10
Have you ever stayed in a relationship — or a friendship — longer than you should have, because the distance made it easier to ignore problems?
The self-reflection closer. Distance can hide problems beautifully. Push: "What problems are easier to ignore when you don't see someone every day?" Let the student sit with this. Target: present perfect + past simple for honest reflection. No judgment — this is about genuine personal insight in English.
💣
The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One Final Dilemma
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
You have been with your partner for 3 years. Everything is good. Then they get a job offer in another country — better salary, better future. The plan is 2 years apart. But you have a feeling — not a certainty, just a feeling — that 2 years will become permanent. Do you ask them to stay?
Homework Loop: "Record a 60-second WhatsApp voice message. Tell me your answer — and tell me what you would actually say to your partner. In English. No script."
