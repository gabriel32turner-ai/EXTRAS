B2.1 Social Issues & Society 🌶 Edge: Spicy ⏱ 50 min
Generational Warfare:
Boomers vs. Millennials vs. Gen Z
Every generation thinks the next one is too soft. Every generation thinks the previous one ruined everything. Who's actually right?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
A real headline. A real debate.
⏱ 4 min
Gallup Workplace Report · 2024
"Gen Z employees are the most disengaged generation in workplace history — and the most vocal about why. Meanwhile, Boomer managers say they've never worked with a generation more entitled and less resilient. Both sides are convinced the other one doesn't get it."
Teacher Script
"Quick question: when you see a 22-year-old at work set boundaries on day one and leave at exactly 5 PM — what's your first thought? And when you see a 58-year-old expect 60-hour weeks and no complaints — what's your first thought about that? Keep both of those reactions in mind. We're going to explore both of them honestly today."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real talk. No right answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever had a conflict at work that was partly about generational differences — different expectations, different ways of communicating, different ideas about effort? What happened?
Almost everyone has a story here. Watch for: was the conflict named as generational at the time, or only in retrospect? That difference reveals whether they were analyzing or just surviving. Push for specifics: what did each side actually want?
Q2
Which generation do you belong to — and do you feel like your generation is understood at work, or constantly misread?
Millennials (1981–1996) and Gen Z (1997–2012) are the most likely in your classroom. But don't assume. Some students are older. The personal stake changes the conversation dramatically — let each person situate themselves first.
Q3
Did your parents' generation have a better or worse deal than yours economically — housing, job security, salary growth? Be specific.
Ground the abstract in real numbers. "My dad bought a house at 30 on one salary." "My mom retired with a pension." These concrete comparisons make the privilege conversation land without becoming political — it becomes personal.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Is Gen Z actually more entitled — or are they just the first generation willing to say out loud what everyone else was thinking?
Model answer: "I think they say things we were trained not to say. 'This workload is unreasonable.' 'This meeting could have been an email.' 'I need time to recover.' We thought those things — we just swallowed them. They don't. Whether that's entitlement or honesty depends on whether you think the system deserved the silence."
Q5
Should young employees have to "pay their dues" — take bad projects, long hours, lower pay — before they earn respect and flexibility? Or is that just hazing with a professional name?
The spiciest opinion question. Push for specifics: what does "paying dues" actually teach? Is it skills, or is it submission? Ask: "Would you put your own kid through it?" That personal test usually changes the answer.
Q6
Is Dra. Isabel right that the generational war is a distraction — designed to keep workers fighting each other instead of questioning the system?
The structural question. This one divides people between the individual-responsibility camp and the systemic-critique camp. Both are valid. The goal is to see that the two aren't mutually exclusive — personal agency AND systemic unfairness can both be real at the same time.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
If you were a manager tomorrow and had to lead a team of 22-year-olds who expect full flexibility, constant feedback, and psychological safety from day one — what would you do differently from the way you were managed?
Great role reversal. Forces them to design the management they wish they'd had. Model: "I'd be explicit about expectations from day one — not assume they know the unwritten rules. I'd give feedback weekly, not annually. And I'd actually listen when they pushed back instead of filing it under 'attitude problem.'"
Q8
If a 58-year-old colleague dismisses your idea in a meeting because "that's not how we do things here" — what do you actually do? And what do you wish you could do?
The gap between "what I do" and "what I wish I could do" is gold. Most professionals swallow it. The ones who've found ways to navigate it have real stories. Ask for the technique — how do you get an idea through without creating an enemy?
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest: have you ever dismissed a younger colleague's concern because you thought "I survived that, so can you"? What does that say about you?
The self-implication question. Most people over 35 will recognize this moment. It doesn't make them bad — it makes them human. The goal is recognition, not shame. "I survived it" is also how cycles of harm get passed down with a clean conscience.
Q10
If your 22-year-old self showed up at your office tomorrow — would you manage them with patience and respect, or would you expect them to just figure it out the way you did?
The closer. Forces them to confront the gap between what they needed and what they'd give. The honest answer usually sounds like: "I'd probably be harder on them than I should be — because that's what I was taught competence looks like." That's the conversation worth having.
