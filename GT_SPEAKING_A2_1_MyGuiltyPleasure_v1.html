My Guilty Pleasure

Something you love — but maybe you shouldn't. Everyone has one. What's yours?

🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
⏱ 4 min
"72% of adults say they eat junk food in secret. 60% watch TV shows they are embarrassed to talk about. And 45% spend money on things they don't want other people to know about."
— YouGov Lifestyle Survey
Teacher script: "Everybody has a guilty pleasure — something you love but maybe you feel a little embarrassed about. For me it's..." [Tell your own example — real or funny.] "Now — what is YOUR guilty pleasure? Don't say 'nothing.' Everybody has one."
💉

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
What did you do last weekend that was a little bit "bad" for you — but felt great?
Past simple. "Last weekend I..." Keep it light. Common answers: slept until noon, ate a lot of junk food, watched 5 episodes of a show, spent too much money. Model: "Last Saturday I ate pizza and watched TV for four hours. It was amazing."
Q2
Did you ever hide food from your family or partner? What was it?
Past simple + fun confession. Almost everyone has done this. Model: "Yes! I hid chocolate in my desk. My wife eats everything fast so I put it there. She never found it." Laughter is guaranteed. Keep the energy high.
Q3
When was the last time you spent money on something you didn't need? What did you buy?
Past simple with time reference. "Last week I bought..." Connect to Sofía's online shopping story. If they say "never" — ask: "Really? Not even coffee every day? Or a snack you didn't need?" Usually opens them up.
Block 2 — Opinion (Simple Present)
Q4
Do you think guilty pleasures are bad — or do we all need them?
Core opinion. "I think guilty pleasures are okay because..." / "I think they are bad because..." Simple present + "because." Rodrigo's provocateur opinion is useful here as a counterpoint. Most students will defend their pleasures.
Q5
Is it better to enjoy your guilty pleasure alone — or with other people?
Comparison opinion. "I think it is better alone because..." / "I like it with people because..." Carolina's pizza-alone story is the anchor. Generates personal preferences naturally. No complex grammar needed.
Q6
What do you think is the most common guilty pleasure in Mexico?
Fun cultural question. "I think the most common is..." Food-related answers dominate in Mexican groups: tacos late at night, chicharrón, eating in the car. Light, funny, and culturally relevant. No pressure on grammar — just talk.
Block 3 — Future Plans (Going To / Will)
Q7
Are you going to stop your guilty pleasure this year — or not?
Going to for plans. "I'm going to try to stop because..." / "No, I'm not going to stop. I like it." Honest answers are always "no" or "I'll try but I know I won't." That honesty is perfect — it creates laughter and real conversation.
Q8
What guilty pleasure are you going to enjoy this weekend?
Going to for near future plans. Very concrete and fun. "This weekend I'm going to..." Students can plan their guilty pleasure out loud in English — low stakes, high engagement. Great closing question for this block.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — do you feel guilty when you enjoy your guilty pleasure, or do you actually feel great?
Self-reflection. Most people say "in the moment — great! After — a little bad." That's the perfect A2.1 honest answer: two simple contrasting sentences. Model: "In the moment I feel great. After I feel a little bad. But the next week I do it again."
Q10
Your partner, friend, or family member says: "You eat too much junk food. You need to stop." What do you say?
Scenario question with will. "I will say..." / "I'm going to say..." Role-play element. Common responses: "Okay, I'll try" (but won't), "It's my life!", "I know, but I like it." Very relatable and generates animated responses.
💣
Phase 5
The Exit Shot
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
Your doctor says: "You need to stop your guilty pleasure. It is bad for your health." But your guilty pleasure is your favorite thing in the week. Are you going to stop — or are you going to continue?
