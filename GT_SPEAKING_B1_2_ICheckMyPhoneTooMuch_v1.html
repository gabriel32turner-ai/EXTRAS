I Check My Phone
Too Much

The average person picks up their phone 96 times a day. Most of us already know it's a problem. So why can't we stop?

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocative Headline
⏱ 4 min
BBC Science Focus · Screen Time Study · 2023
"The average person checks their phone 96 times a day — once every 10 minutes. But when researchers asked people to estimate how often they check, most said 'around 20 times.' We are significantly worse at controlling our phone use than we think we are."
Teacher Script: Read the stat, then ask: "How many times do you think you check your phone per day? Be honest — guess a number." Let them answer. Then: "Now go to your phone settings and check your actual screen time. What does it say?" If they have it available, this creates an instant "wow" moment. The gap between estimate and reality is the emotional hook of the session.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever checked your phone screen time and been surprised — or embarrassed — by the number?
Most students have done this and felt a small shock. Push: "What app takes up most of your time? What do you think about that?" Target: present perfect to open, past simple for details. Key vocab: screen time, average, per day.
Q2
Have you ever been in a conversation with someone — a friend, partner, or family member — and realized they were on their phone the whole time? How did it feel?
Very personal and common. Push: "Did you say something — or did you just accept it?" Then flip: "And have you ever been the person on the phone?" Target: present perfect + past simple. Key vocab: distracted, rude, ignored, present.
Q3
Have you ever tried to use your phone less — even for a few days? What happened?
Push: "What did you miss? What did you not miss at all?" Model: "I tried to stop using Instagram for a week. The first 2 days were hard — I kept picking up my phone automatically. After day 3 it got easier. But I went back after the week." Target: past simple + sequence adverbs (first, then, after that).
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Do you think phone addiction is a real addiction — like alcohol or cigarettes — or is it something different?
Neuroscience says yes — phones trigger the same dopamine response as other addictions. But many people resist the label. Push: "What would have to be true for you to call your phone use an addiction?" Target: present simple for opinion + conditional for the hypothetical test.
Q5
Is it the user's responsibility to control their phone use — or should companies design apps that are less addictive?
Good for activating: responsibility, choice, design, algorithm. Push: "Is it fair to design an app to be as addictive as possible — and then blame users for getting addicted?" Target: should + passive voice (apps are designed to...). Model: "I think it's both — but companies have more power and should use it responsibly."
Q6
Do you think people who use their phones a lot are less present — or is that just an old-fashioned idea?
Connects Lucía's argument to real life. Push: "Can you be on your phone a lot AND be fully present in your relationships?" Target: present simple + modals. Watch for students who defend Lucía's position — push them: "OK, but what about Fernando's daughter asking if the phone was more important?"
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your doctor told you that your phone use was damaging your sleep and concentration, and recommended a maximum of 1 hour per day?
Target: second conditional. Push: "Would you actually do it — or would you nod and then change nothing?" Model: "I'd probably try for a week, fail, feel guilty, and then find a way to justify why my use is different." Get the student to be honest about the gap between intention and behavior.
Q8
What would you do if your partner asked you to put your phone away completely every evening from 7pm to 10pm?
Target: second conditional + relationship vocabulary. Push: "Is that a reasonable request — or controlling?" Model: "I think I would agree — but I'd also be annoyed at first. And I'd probably check it once and feel guilty." Get students to role-play the conversation: "What would you actually say to your partner?"
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Do you think the generation growing up with phones now — kids who are 8, 10, 12 — will have serious problems as adults because of it?
Research is increasingly clear that early smartphone use correlates with higher anxiety and depression, especially in girls. Push: "If you have or want kids — what rules would you set about phones?" Target: will/going to for predictions + modals for obligation. Good for activating parenting vocabulary.
Q10
Be honest: what is the first thing you do in the morning and the last thing you do at night? And how do you feel about that answer?
Most students will say: check their phone. The question isn't the behavior — it's "how do you feel about that?" Push: "Is that who you want to be?" Let the silence work. Target: present simple + honest emotional vocabulary (ashamed, fine with it, worried, trying to change). This is the self-reflection closer that makes the class personal.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One Final Dilemma
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
Your phone shows 4 hours and 20 minutes of screen time today. You have a 9-year-old who asked you twice to play with them and you said "in a minute" — but the minute never came. Tonight, before bed, they show you a drawing they made. In the drawing, your family is at the dinner table. Everyone has a face — except you. You have a phone where your face should be. What do you do tomorrow?
Homework Loop: "Record a 60-second WhatsApp voice message. Tell me what you do tomorrow — and what you say to your child tonight. In English. No script."
