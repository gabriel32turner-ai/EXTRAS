B1.2 Social Issues & Society 🌶 Spicy 50 min
Street Harassment: Normal or Unacceptable?
In Mexico, it has a name: acoso callejero. Many women experience it every day. Many men have never thought about it. This conversation changes that.
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One number. Let it create a reaction before the discussion starts.
⏱ 4 min
UN Women · Safe Cities Global Initiative · 2022
"In Mexico, 9 out of 10 women say they have experienced some form of street harassment. Most of them say they change their route, their clothes, or the time they go out because of it."
Street harassment includes comments about appearance, whistling, following, touching, and blocking someone's path in public. In Mexico, the term "acoso callejero" has been used in national campaigns since 2019, and several states have passed laws making it a punishable offense. Despite this, most incidents still go unreported — and many people still debate whether it is a serious problem or a cultural tradition.
Teacher Script Read the statistic. Then say: "9 out of 10. That means almost every woman in this room — or every woman in someone's life — has experienced this. Before we start: does that number surprise you?" If the class is mixed gender, let both sides react. If it's mostly women, ask: "Has this happened to you or someone you know? You don't have to give details — just yes or no." Don't explain or debate the stat. Just let it land.

"I just ignore it and keep walking."
The most common strategy women use — not because it feels right, but because it feels safer.
"It's just a compliment."
The most common defense — and the one that makes the conversation most difficult.
"I didn't feel safe walking there alone."
Said after choosing a different route — or staying home instead.
"Why does nobody say anything?"
The question bystanders ask themselves — and rarely answer out loud.
"That's just how men are."
Said to explain — and accidentally excuse — behavior that causes real harm.

Accountant · León
"I saw a man follow a woman for two blocks last month. She was clearly scared. I didn't do anything. I still feel bad about it. I didn't know what to say. I think the problem isn't just the men who do it — it's the rest of us who look the other way."
😠
Valeria, 45
Nurse · Querétaro
⚡ Provocateur
"I've experienced harassment my whole life. But I'm also tired of teaching women to protect themselves. We tell women: don't go out at night, don't dress that way, don't walk alone. Why aren't we telling men to stop? The problem is men's behavior — not women's routes."
Discussion prompt: "Which of these four people do you understand the most? Which one says something you've never thought about before?" Valeria is the provocateur — she flips the "safety advice" narrative by pointing out that we spend more time teaching women to protect themselves than teaching men not to harass. This often makes both men and women stop and reconsider.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Personal answers only — not what you think you should say.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever felt unsafe walking alone in a public place? What happened — or what were you worried might happen?
Teacher Tip This question works for both men and women — though the experiences are often very different. Let everyone answer. Women often have many stories. Men sometimes say "no" at first, then remember a moment. Both are interesting. Use "Have you ever…" as a model for their answer.
Q2
Have you ever seen someone being harassed or made uncomfortable in public — on the street, on the bus, at the market? What did you do?
Teacher Tip This connects to Mateo's story. Most people have seen something and done nothing — that guilt is worth exploring. Push: "Why didn't you say something? What stopped you?" No judgment. The answer reveals a lot about how normalized the behavior is.
Q3
Have you ever changed your plans — your route, your clothes, the time you went out — because you were worried about your safety?
Teacher Tip The statistic in the trigger says most women do this. If men in the class say "no," that gap is the conversation. Ask: "Does it surprise you that many women do this every day?" Let the difference in experience become a real talking point, not a lecture.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
In your opinion, is there a difference between a compliment and harassment? Where does one end and the other begin?
Teacher Tip Fernando raised this point in the Opinion Wall. The best answer is usually: the difference is whether the person wants it. A compliment given once to someone who smiles is different from a repeated comment to someone who is clearly uncomfortable. Push them to name the line.
Q5
What do you think about the phrase "that's just how men are"? Is it an explanation — or an excuse?
Teacher Tip This is about the normalization of behavior. Explanation vs. excuse is a useful distinction — push them to define both. "An explanation says why something happens. An excuse says it's okay. Which one is 'that's just how men are'?"
Q6
Do you think bystanders — people who see it happen but don't stop it — are part of the problem? Why or why not?
Teacher Tip Mateo said it best: the problem is also "the rest of us who look the other way." Most students will say yes, bystanders share responsibility — but push: "What would you actually do? Name one specific thing." Abstract agreement is not fluency. Concrete action language is.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you saw a man following a woman on the street and she looked scared? Would you say something?
Teacher Tip Second conditional: "What would you do if you saw…" Push for specifics — not "I would help her" but what they would actually say or do. Research shows the most effective bystander strategy is talking to the person being harassed, not confronting the harasser. Share this if helpful.
Q8
What would you say to a friend — a male friend — if you found out he often makes comments to women on the street?
Teacher Tip Second conditional + reported speech. This is the personal accountability question. Many people find it easier to condemn abstract bad actors than confront someone they know. Push: "Would you actually say something to him? Or would you stay quiet?" The honest answer is often "I don't know."
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Do you think street harassment is more of a problem in Mexico than in other countries — or is it the same everywhere but less talked about?
Teacher Tip This is about cultural honesty. Mexico does have a significant street harassment problem — and also has growing public awareness and legislation about it. The comparison to other countries prevents the "it's just our culture" deflection while keeping it honest. Push them to use evidence from their own experience.
Q10 · Self-Reflection
Have you ever said or done something — even something small — that made another person feel uncomfortable in public? Be honest.
Teacher Tip The hardest question — and the most important one. Most people have stared too long, made a comment they didn't think through, or laughed at something they now realize wasn't funny. The goal isn't guilt — it's honest reflection. Give them space. Don't push if they're not ready.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. Record your answer in English.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · B1.2 Street Harassment
Your 16-year-old daughter — or sister, or niece — tells you that a man follows her every morning on her walk to school. She asks you: "What should I do?" What do you tell her?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. What do you tell her — and does your answer focus on what she should do, or on what he should stop doing?
Retention Hook The question works because it's concrete and personal — a family member, not a statistic. The second part ("does your answer focus on her behavior or his?") echoes Valeria's provocateur point and lands harder when the student hears themselves giving advice. End: "Send me the voice note. I want to hear what you tell her."
