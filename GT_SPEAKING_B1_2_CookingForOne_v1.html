Cooking for One:
Boring or Peaceful?
One plate. No sharing. No negotiations. But also — no one to talk to.

The Headline
Read it aloud. Then ask the first question.

01
The New York Times · Food
"For the first time in history, more adults in the US live alone than in any other household type. The cooking industry has completely failed to adapt."
— On how recipes, restaurants, and supermarket packaging are still designed for families of four — despite the reality of millions of solo kitchens
YouGov Survey · 2023
"52% of people who live alone say they often just eat cereal, toast, or delivery instead of cooking — because cooking for one feels pointless."
— "Pointless" is the word. Not hard. Not expensive. Just pointless.
Launch line: "Have you ever cooked a full meal just for yourself — and actually enjoyed the process?" [Pause.] "Or is cooking only fun when there are other people?" That single question splits the room immediately and sets up the whole conversation.

Colloquial

The great freedom of solo cooking: nobody judges your food choices

After vocab, ask: "Which phrase describes you most — 'I can't be bothered' or 'I eat whatever I want'?" Those two capture the two main attitudes toward solo cooking. Use the student's answer as their starting position in the Opinion Wall discussion.

03
👩‍🍳
Lucía, 32
Nurse · Guadalajara
"I live alone and I love cooking for myself. I put on music, take my time, try new recipes. It's the best hour of my day. I'm not cooking for anyone — just for me. That means I can make exactly what I want, when I want it. It's not lonely. It's freedom."
😔
Carlos, 38
Sales Rep · Monterrey
"I tried cooking for myself for a year after my divorce. It was depressing. I would make a nice meal and then sit at the table in silence. Food is about sharing — when there's no one to share it with, it loses its point. I order delivery most nights now. It's not healthy, but it feels less sad."
🥗
Sofía, 27
Teacher · CDMX
"I do meal prep on Sundays. I cook big portions and eat the same thing for three days. Is it exciting? No. But it saves time, money, and I eat healthy. Cooking for one is a skill — you just have to learn how to scale down and stop wasting food. It gets easier with practice."
Provocateur
🍕
Diego, 45
Accountant · Celaya
"If you feel sad eating alone, the problem is not the food — it's that you haven't learned to enjoy your own company. People who need someone at the table to enjoy a meal are people who are uncomfortable being alone. Cooking for one is actually a test. Either you're okay with yourself, or you're not."
Provocateur rationale: Diego turns a lifestyle preference into a psychological test — implying that people who hate eating alone have a problem with solitude. This is uncomfortable because it's partly true and partly unfair. Students who relate to Carlos will feel judged. Students who relate to Lucía may secretly agree with Diego.

Facilitation move: "Which opinion makes you feel most uncomfortable — and why?" The answer to that question usually drives the best conversation of the session.
🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Build from personal to provocative. Show tips only after the student answers.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever lived alone — even for a short time? What was your relationship with cooking and eating during that period?
Present perfect opener. Even students who live with others often have a period alone — a trip, a work relocation, a partner away. Model: "Yes — I lived alone for two years after university. The first month I cooked properly. By month three I was eating the same three things on rotation." Activate: leftovers, order in, bother.
Q2
Have you ever eaten at a restaurant completely alone — without your phone, without a book, just you and your food? How did that feel?
The phone detail makes this question harder — most people eat alone with their phone, which is different. This tests real comfort with solitude. Model: "I've done it on business trips. The first 5 minutes feel awkward, then you just start observing people around you and it becomes interesting." Activate: lonely, enjoy, uncomfortable.
Q3
What do you actually eat when no one is watching — the meals you would never tell anyone about? Be honest.
Light and funny — the secret solo meal question. Almost everyone has a shameful answer (cereal for dinner, chips and salsa, a full bag of something they'd never eat in front of others). This is the highest-energy question in Block 1. Activate: comfort food, eat standing up, skip a meal.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think cooking and eating are more enjoyable when you do them with other people — or is that just what we've been told to believe?
Central question of the lab. Push for nuance: "Is it always more enjoyable? What about when you're tired? What about when the other person is annoying?" Model: "I think shared meals are more memorable — but solo meals are more relaxing." Activate: loneliness, enjoy, comfort food.
Q5
Is it lazy to order delivery instead of cooking? Or is it just a smart use of your time and money?
Common guilt for solo eaters. Most students will say "it depends" — push for a real opinion. Model: "I think it's fine 3 times a week. After that it starts to feel like you've given up." Activate: bother, order in, waste, routine. Good for modals: "You should probably cook more, but you don't have to."
Q6
Some people say that cooking is an act of love — you do it for others, not for yourself. Do you agree — or can cooking just for yourself also be an act of self-care?
This connects Lucía's position to something broader. Model: "I think cooking for someone you love is an act of love. Cooking for yourself is an act of respect — treating yourself like a person worth feeding well." B1.2 students often find this distinction meaningful. Activate: enjoy, cook from scratch, comfort food.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you had to live completely alone for one year — no partner, no roommates, no family nearby? Would you cook properly, or would you just survive?
Second conditional. Most students have a realistic answer here — most say they'd start motivated and then decline. Model: "If that happened, I'd probably cook properly for the first month — and then slowly start eating cereal at 9pm." Push for specifics: "What would your go-to solo meals be?" Activate: meal prep, leftovers, order in.
Q8
What would you cook for yourself if money was no issue — you could buy anything, use any ingredient, take as long as you wanted? Describe the meal.
Creative and personal — great for B1.2 descriptive language. Students love this question. It reveals what they actually love to eat when no one else's preferences matter. Let it run. It often produces the most natural, expressive English of the session.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Do you think people who say they hate eating alone are actually afraid of being alone with their thoughts — and food is just the context?
Diego's argument from the Opinion Wall. Some students will strongly agree — others will find it too harsh. Model: "I think there's some truth to it. But I also think humans are social animals and wanting company at a meal is completely normal — it doesn't mean you can't be alone." Don't push one answer.
Q10
Honestly — do you take care of yourself the same way when you're alone as when others are watching? With food, with your home, with your sleep?
The self-reflection close. The honest answer for most people is no — they eat worse, sleep worse, clean less when they're alone. That gap between public and private self-care is the insight. Let the student sit with it. It's the most personal moment of the session.
B1.2 grammar check: Q1–Q3 = present perfect ("Have you ever..."). Q7–Q8 = second conditional ("What would you do if..."). Q8 is a great place for "going to" practice too: "I would make... and then I'd probably... and I'd have..."

Energy note: This topic has a deceptively rich emotional layer for some students — particularly those who have been through separation, relocation, or a period of isolation. Q9 and Q10 can land deeply. Give them space.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. Personal. No wrong answer.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
You have a free Saturday with nothing planned — no family, no friends, no obligations. You could order delivery and watch TV, or you could spend 2 hours cooking a proper meal just for yourself: something you actually love, with music, no rush. Which do you choose — and does your answer say something about how you feel about being alone?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Describe the last time you cooked a real meal. What did you make, who was it for, and how did it feel? If you can't remember — tell me why." Send before our next session.
Why it works: The Exit Shot doesn't ask about solo cooking in general — it asks about a specific Saturday. The choice between delivery and cooking becomes a mirror for the student's relationship with solitude. The closing question ("does your answer say something about how you feel about being alone?") is the one that stays with them.

Homework note: The recording prompt is warm and personal — very easy to record. Even students who say "I can't remember the last time I cooked" are giving a rich, honest answer. That's also a valid response.

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about how we eat — habits, food choices, and what our relationship with food says about our stress levels and our daily life. A lot of it connects to what we discussed today."
