§ 01 · The Trigger
🪝 The Number That Explains Everything
Read aloud or project · First reaction only · No debate yet · 4 min max
01
4 min
💸
Bankrate Annual Emergency Savings Report + Forbes Financial Survey 2023
78%
Of American workers live paycheck to paycheck — including people earning over $100,000 per year
$400
The amount 40% of Americans say they could NOT cover in an unexpected emergency without borrowing
1 in 3
Adults have no retirement savings at all — not even a small emergency fund
67%
Of people who earn more money say they spend more money — the paycheck-to-paycheck cycle often grows with income
Sources: Bankrate Emergency Savings Report (2023) · Forbes Financial Health Survey · Federal Reserve "Report on the Economic Well-Being of US Households"
🪝 Opening Trigger — Read this, then point at someone
"A person earns $120,000 a year. After rent, car payments, school fees, subscriptions, and lifestyle costs — they have $200 left at the end of the month. Are they rich — or poor?"
Teacher Mechanics — 4 min Read the scenario slowly. Let the number ($120,000) land. Then point: "First reaction — rich or poor?" Don't answer. Don't explain. Wait for the tension. After 2–3 responses, say: "We'll come back to this in §4. Hold your answer."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The $120K scenario works because it dismantles the assumption that a high salary = financial security. Many students will say "that person is rich" — then realize the trap. That cognitive dissonance is the activation.
  • The 67% stat (more income = more spending) is the most surprising. Mark who reacts to it — they'll lead Q5 and Q6.
  • If the group is silent: ask directly — "Do you know anyone who earns a good salary but is always broke? Don't say the name. Just: does this person exist in your life?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Money is one of the most avoided topics in Mexican professional settings. The fact that this class discusses it openly is itself a differentiator. Say: "In most classes, this topic doesn't come up. Here, it does. In English."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Retention hook: "Next session we go into money and friendship — who you lend to, who you borrow from. Same depth."

Challenge Mode Available

💡 10 Nouns — The Money Cycle
🔵 10 Verbs — What People Do With (and Without) Money
⚡ Production Challenge — No preparation
Each person picks 3 words. One sentence each about your real financial life right now. Be honest. No writing. Just talk.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • "Broke" vs "in debt" — clarify if it comes up: broke = no money right now; in debt = you owe money to someone. You can be broke AND in debt, or just one.
  • The production challenge for this topic is often surprisingly honest. Let the honesty happen. Don't react — just validate the language: "Great sentence. That's the grammar. That's the vocab."
  • Best trigger: "Which 3 words describe the week before your next paycheck?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "These are the words you need to talk to your bank, your partner, or your kids about money — in English."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "These words will come up in your next performance review when you talk about your raise — and when you explain why you need one."

🔁 Processing Question — Pairs → Group
"Who do you agree with most — and who makes you feel something uncomfortable? Why?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Read all 4 before opening discussion. The contrast between Diana (shame/silence) and Ramón (system blame) is where the real debate lives.
  • The provocateur is Lic. Aguilar. His opinion will cause the most friction — especially with younger students who feel the system is the problem. Let that friction fuel the session.
  • Push: "Lic. Aguilar says it's always a personal choice. Do you agree — even if someone earns minimum wage?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • If the group identifies strongly with Diana (shame) — use it: "Why do we feel ashamed about money? Is it like that in the US too? Or is it something more Mexican?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Testimonial trigger: "One sentence — which of these four people describes someone you know personally? Without saying the name."
§ 04 · The Conversation Engine
🗣 10 Questions. 4 Blocks. 23 Minutes.
Personal → Opinion → Hypothetical → Provocative · No round-robin · Point and push
04
23 min
⚡
Rule: Minimum 2 sentences per answer. Position first. Reason or example second. No yes/no answers accepted.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Block 1 uses present perfect. Monitor: "Have you ever been broke" vs "Were you broke." Gently recycle the PP form when you hear simple past without stopping the flow.
  • Q7 and Q8 (second conditional) — watch for "If I would have" — note and recycle at end only.
  • Q9 is the most honest question of the session. Let it breathe — 5 full seconds of silence before redirecting if needed.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Celebrate vocab activation: when a student uses "broke," "savings," or "in debt" naturally in their answer — acknowledge it immediately: "That's a real English sentence. Not a translation. Good."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • After Q10: "You just talked about money in English — honestly — for 23 minutes. Most people can't do that in Spanish with their family. You did it here."
§ 05 · The Exit Shot
💣 One Question. No Easy Answer.
5 min · No chips · Just position + reason · Then the loop
05
5 min
EXIT
💣 Exit Shot
It's the 25th of the month. You have 3 days until your next paycheck. You have 800 pesos left. Your best friend texts you: "Can you lend me $500? I'll pay you back next week." What do you do?
Answer in 3 sentences minimum. Be specific. What do you actually say — and what don't you say?
📌 B1.2 Model Answer — Reveal AFTER student speaks
"I would say yes — because she's my best friend and I've been in that situation before. But I would feel anxious for those 3 days. I wouldn't tell her how much I actually have left, because I don't want her to feel bad. I've done this before. It always works out — but it's not comfortable."
🔁 Retention Loop — The WhatsApp Homework "Before our next session — record a 60-second voice note in English. Your answer to today's Exit Shot. Don't write it first. Don't rehearse it. Just talk. Send it to [teacher number]."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The amounts are in pesos — concrete and relatable for Celaya students. The scenario bridges directly into the next lab (Money and Friendship). Use it as a teaser: "We're going to talk more about that exact situation next time."
  • After the student answers: follow-up only — "What would you do if she didn't pay you back?" Second conditional activated naturally.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The model answer is honest and imperfect — "it's not comfortable." That imperfection is the point. Give students permission to give imperfect, honest answers in English.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Closing line: "Next Speaking Lab: Money and Friendship. What happens when the two mix. It gets more complicated than today." This is your direct tease to Lab 3 of this lote.
