My Worst Day
at Work

Bad days happen to everyone. Let's talk about yours.

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
A very simple fact
"The average person has about 2,000 bad days at work in their lifetime. That's almost 6 years of bad days."
Teacher script: Read the stat. Smile. Then ask: "OK. Think about your worst day at work. Just one day. What happened?" Point to one student. Let them say 2–3 sentences. Then move to vocab. Keep it fast and fun — don't let it become a long story yet. That comes in Phase 4.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Past Experience
Q1
When was your last really bad day at work? What happened?
Teacher tip: Help with sentence starters: "It was on [day]. I was at work and..." or "My boss said..." or "The problem was..." Keep it simple — 3 or 4 sentences is great at this level.
Q2
Did you make a mistake at work one time? What did you do?
Model answer: "Yes. I sent an email to the wrong person. It was a mistake. My boss was angry. I said sorry and I sent the correct email. It was very embarrassing."
Q3
What did you do after your worst day at work? Did you go home and rest, or did you do something fun?
Teacher tip: Great for practicing past simple. Sentence starters: "I went home and..." / "I called my friend and..." / "I ate a lot of..." Expect funny answers.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think it's OK to cry at work when things go very wrong?
Teacher tip: Simple yes/no with a reason. Push: "Why yes?" / "Why no?" Model: "I think it's OK. We are human. But I never cry at work. I wait until I'm at home."
Q5
Do you think a bad day at work is worse than a bad day at home? Why?
Model answer: "I think work is worse. At home, I can relax. At work, I have to continue. I can't stop. And everyone can see me. So a bad day at work is more difficult for me."
Q6
Is it good to talk to your boss when you have a really bad day — or is it better to say nothing?
Teacher tip: Very simple debate. Two clear positions. Let students defend their choice with one or two reasons. Vocabulary to use: talk, problem, boss, angry, better, worse.
Block 3 — Future Plans
Q7
Are you going to do something different next time you have a bad day at work?
Teacher tip: Practice "going to." Help with: "Next time, I'm going to..." Common answers: talk to my boss, go home early, take a walk, eat something good. Simple plans are perfect.
Q8
Will you stay in your job if every day is a bad day?
Model answer: "No, I will not stay. If every day is bad, I will look for a new job. Life is too short for a terrible job. But I will save some money first."
Block 4 — Past Habits & Reflection
Q9
Did you use to have more bad days at work before, or is your job better now than in the past?
Teacher tip: Practice "used to." Help with: "Before, I used to have..." / "My old job used to be..." Great for comparing past and present work situations simply.
Q10
Look at your colleagues. Do you think they have bad days too — or do they always look happy?
Teacher tip: A fun observation question. Students often laugh because they realize they don't know. Push: "How do you know when a colleague is having a bad day? What do they do?"
💣
The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
Think about your worst day at work. What did you learn from that day? Tell your partner in 3 sentences.
