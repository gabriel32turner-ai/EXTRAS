People Pleasers
The psychology behind saying yes when you mean no — and the real cost it has on your career and relationships.
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One headline. One question. Let them react before they think too much.
⏱ 4 min
Harvard Business Review · 2023
"People pleasers are the highest-rated employees and the most likely to burn out. They're also the least likely to get promoted."
HBR research on people-pleasing in the workplace found that employees who consistently prioritize others' approval over their own boundaries tend to be rated higher by managers — but advance slower, earn less, and report significantly higher rates of emotional exhaustion than their more assertive peers.
Teacher Script Put the headline up. Give them 10 seconds. Then say: "First reaction — is this fair or totally wrong? Don't think about it. Just tell me." Let 2–3 students react, then ask: "Quick show of hands — how many of you have said yes to something at work this week that you really wanted to say no to?" That's your hook. Don't explain the topic yet. Let it unfold.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real answers only. No rehearsed corporate speak.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever said yes to something at work or in your personal life that you immediately regretted? What happened?
Teacher Tip Almost everyone has. The key is getting them past the surface answer ("yes, I took on extra work"). Push for: what did you actually feel in that moment? What stopped you from saying no? What was the real cost?
Q2
Think of someone in your life — at work or personally — who you'd describe as a people pleaser. What does that actually look like in their behavior?
Teacher Tip This question creates useful distance — they describe someone else before turning the mirror on themselves. Listen for specific behaviors: always volunteering, never disagreeing, apologizing constantly, saying "no problem" when it clearly is a problem.
Q3
Have you ever felt resentment toward someone you were helping — even though you were the one who said yes in the first place?
Teacher Tip This is the core people-pleaser experience. If they've felt it, they'll recognize themselves in it. Use present perfect here: "Have you ever felt…" — encourage them to use it in their answer too.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is people-pleasing a personality trait, a learned behavior, or a sign of low self-esteem? Can you separate them?
Teacher Tip Push them to take a position, not just list options. "You gave me three options — which one do you actually believe?" Good vocabulary activation: self-worth, validation, conflict avoidance.
Q5
Do you think there's a difference between being a people pleaser and just being a kind, supportive person? Where's the line?
Teacher Tip This connects to Camila's opinion from the wall. The line is usually: do you feel resentful? Are you doing it because you want to, or because you're afraid of the consequences of not doing it?
Q6
Andrés from the Opinion Wall said people pleasers are actually manipulative. Do you agree — even a little? Why or why not?
Teacher Tip The spicy one. Many will resist it. But push them to steelman it: "Even if you disagree, explain the argument as well as you can before you reject it." This is the highest-level language task in the session.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If you could completely stop caring about what other people think of you at work, how would your behavior change on Monday morning?
Teacher Tip Second conditional: "If I could… I would…" Push for specific, concrete answers. Not "I'd be more assertive" — what would they actually do differently? Who would they push back on? What meeting would they not volunteer for?
Q8
Will people-pleasing become more or less common as younger generations enter the workforce? What's driving that?
Teacher Tip Will + prediction. Encourage them to connect to real trends: Gen Z, social media, hustle culture, therapy culture, etc. No right answer — push them to defend their position with a reason.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Have the people in your life — your boss, your partner, your family — actually benefited from your people-pleasing? Be honest.
Teacher Tip This is uncomfortable because the honest answer is often yes. And if the people around you benefit from it, there's a dynamic being maintained. Let them sit with that. Don't rush to resolution.
Q10 · Self-Reflection
On a scale of 1 to 10, how much of a people pleaser are you? And — more importantly — are you okay with that number?
Teacher Tip The closer question of the session. The number is less important than the second part. Some students will say "I'm a 7 and I'm fine with it." Others will say "I'm a 4 and it's still too much." Both are valid — the goal is honesty, not performance of self-awareness.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. No right answer. Record your response.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · B2.1 People Pleasers
Your manager asks you to work extra hours on a project every week for the next 3 months. No extra pay. No promotion on the table. But they say: "I really need someone I can count on — and I thought that was you." Do you say yes?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. Tell me: what would you actually say to your manager — word for word? And be honest: is your answer today different from what you would have done 5 years ago?
Retention Hook The guilt trip in the exit shot ("I thought that was you") is deliberate — it's the exact mechanism people pleasers can't resist. Most students will feel the pull even as they read it. End with: "Send me your voice note before our next session. I want to hear the word 'no' — or a very good reason why not."
