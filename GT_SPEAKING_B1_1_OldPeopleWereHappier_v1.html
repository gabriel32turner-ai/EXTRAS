Old People
Were Happier

No internet, no options, no anxiety — or just no way to complain about it. Were things actually better before?

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
BBC · Health · 2023
"Studies show that people born before 1965 report higher levels of daily happiness than younger generations — despite having fewer material possessions and less access to technology."
Teacher script: Read the stat. Then ask: "Think about your parents or grandparents at your age. Do you think they were happier than you? Why?" Let two students react quickly before moving to vocab. This topic always triggers immediate personal memories.

The Opinion Wall
Phase 3 · Who do you agree with?
10 min
👴
Don Roberto, 72 — Retired farmer, Guanajuato
"We didn't have much, but we were together. My wife and I ate together every day. My kids played outside all afternoon. Nobody was looking at a phone. Now I see my grandchildren at dinner, all of them with a screen, and nobody talks. I feel sad for them."
👩
Andrea, 29 — Nurse, CDMX
"My grandmother had seven children and no choice about it. She couldn't work, couldn't travel, couldn't leave if she was unhappy. She wasn't happy — she was trapped. We remember the past as simpler because we forget how hard it was for women especially."
👨
Ricardo, 44 — Accountant, Monterrey
provocateur
"We are not less happy. We are more aware of our problems. Before, people suffered in silence. Now we have words for anxiety, depression, stress. Naming a problem is not the same as having more problems. We are not sadder — we are just more honest."
👩
Valeria, 36 — Teacher, Puebla
"I look at photos of my parents in the 80s and they look happy — really happy. Not 'I posted a perfect photo' happy. Just happy. I think we have too many options now. Too many choices make people more anxious, not more free. Sometimes simple is better."
💬 Choose one opinion. Who do you agree with most? Does Ricardo's idea surprise you — or does it make sense? Tell your partner.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever had a conversation with a grandparent or older person about how life was "before"? What did they say?
Teacher tip: This generates beautiful personal stories. Push: "What specifically did they miss? What did they say was better? Did you believe them — or did you think they were just being nostalgic?"
Q2
Have you ever had a moment — a weekend, a trip, a day — where you felt really simple and happy, with no phone and no worries?
Model answer: "Yes. Last year we went to a rancho for the weekend and there was no signal. The first hour I felt anxious. Then I relaxed completely. We cooked, we talked, we slept. On Sunday I didn't want to go back."
Q3
Have you ever felt more stressed or anxious after spending a lot of time on your phone or social media?
Teacher tip: Almost everyone says yes. Push: "What specifically made you feel bad — news? Other people's photos? Notifications? What did you do after?"
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think people were actually happier before — or do they just remember the good parts and forget the bad?
Teacher tip: Reference Ricardo's idea about "naming problems." This is the intellectual heart of the session. Ask: "Is it possible that people were miserable before but didn't have words for it?"
Q5
Do you think too many options make people unhappy? Is it better to have few choices?
Model answer: "I think yes. When I had only 3 channels on TV, I watched whatever was on and I was happy. Now I have Netflix with 10,000 things and I spend 30 minutes choosing and then I'm tired. More options, more frustration."
Q6
Is it OK to be nostalgic — to think the past was better — or is that just a way of avoiding the present?
Teacher tip: Push students to be honest: "When you feel nostalgic, are you actually missing the past — or are you unhappy right now and looking for an escape?" Strong self-reflection question.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you had to live one full week without your phone — no calls, no messages, no apps?
Teacher tip: Keep it concrete. Ask: "Would you be scared? Relieved? What would you actually do with the time? Who would you miss the most?" The last question usually reveals something real.
Q8
What would you keep from today's world — and what would you give back — if you could choose?
Model answer: "I would keep modern medicine, travel, and music. I would give back social media, 24-hour news, and the feeling that I'm always behind. Those three things make me tired more than anything else."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Do you think you are happy right now — actually happy, not just busy and distracted?
Teacher tip: The most important question of the session. Give long silence — 20 seconds — before asking for answers. Don't jump to rescue students who pause. Let them think. Some of the most honest moments in a speaking class come from this kind of question.
Q10
Be honest: what is one thing from the past — even from your own childhood — that you genuinely miss?
Teacher tip: Light but real. Great answers come from concrete memories: a specific food, a smell, playing outside, a person. Concrete nostalgia produces better language than abstract nostalgia. Push: "Describe it. What did it look like, smell like, feel like?"
💣
The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
A time machine gives you two options: go back to 1985 and live your whole life over — no internet, no phone, simpler but less free. Or stay exactly where you are right now. Which do you choose — and what does your answer say about how happy you actually are today?
