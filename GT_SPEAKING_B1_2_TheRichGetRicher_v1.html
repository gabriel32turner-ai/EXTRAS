The Rich Get Richer
You work hard. They have a yacht. Is the system broken — or is that just how life works? Let's have the honest conversation.
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
A real headline. A real conversation.
⏱ 4 min
Oxfam Report · 2024
"The five richest men in the world doubled their wealth since 2020. In the same period, five billion people became poorer. This is not a coincidence. It is the result of economic systems designed to move money upward."
Teacher Script
"Quick question — and be honest: do you think you will be richer than your parents were? Do you think your children will be richer than you? And if the answer is no — why not? Today we're going to talk about money, success, and fairness. This one might get uncomfortable. That's okay."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real talk. No right answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever felt that someone had a big advantage over you — at school, at work, or in life — just because their family had more money? What happened?
Get a real story. "A colleague got promoted because his uncle was a director" is more useful than "yes, in general." Specific personal memories open the most authentic vocabulary. Follow up: "How did that make you feel — angry, motivated, or resigned?"
Q2
Have you ever looked at someone very rich and felt jealous? What were you jealous of exactly — their money, their freedom, or something else?
Jealousy is honest and universal. The interesting part is what specifically they're jealous of: security? time? options? not having to worry? That distinction leads to a much richer conversation than "I want to be rich."
Q3
Have you or your family ever struggled financially — not having enough money for something important? How did that experience shape you?
Sensitive but important. Many B1.2 students in corporate Mexico have complex family histories with money. Respect silence if it comes. The option is there to share — not an obligation. If they share: validate before analyzing.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
In your opinion, is it possible for a poor person to become rich in Mexico today — just through hard work and good decisions? Or do you need luck and connections too?
The classic debate. Push for specifics: "Can you name someone who did it? What did they have that others didn't?" The goal is to test the "hard work is enough" theory against real examples they know personally — not famous exceptions.
Q5
Do you think rich people deserve their money — or do you think a lot of wealth comes from luck, family, and connections more than personal effort?
The "deserve" question is spicy. Push them to think of a specific person: a Mexican billionaire, a wealthy colleague, a boss. "Does that specific person deserve what they have?" is harder to answer than the abstract version.
Q6
Gabriela says the real problem is personal decisions, not the system. Is she wrong — or does she have a point that people don't want to hear?
The spiciest opinion question. Both are true simultaneously, which makes the answer genuinely hard. Personal decisions DO matter. AND the starting conditions are not equal. The challenge is holding both truths without using one to dismiss the other.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you suddenly received 5 million pesos? Would you spend it, save it, invest it — or share it? Be specific.
Great vocabulary practice: pay off debt, buy a house, start a business, help my family, invest. Push for a real plan: "How much for each thing?" The decisions reveal values. Watch especially for who they would help — and who they wouldn't.
Q8
What would you do if you found out your company's CEO earns 400 times more than you — and just approved a freeze on salaries because "the company needs to cut costs"? Would you say something?
Hypothetical with real emotional charge. Push: "Would you say something to your manager? To HR? Or would you just update your CV?" The range from silence to action reveals their real feelings about power and fairness at work. No wrong answer.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
If you became very rich one day — really rich — do you think you would still believe the system is unfair? Or do you think your opinion would change?
The honest question about how wealth changes perspective. Research shows that people who become rich tend to attribute it more to personal merit over time — and less to luck or circumstances. Ask: "Is that a problem? Is it human? Is it dangerous?" Let them decide.
Q10
Be honest: do you think your children will have a better economic life than you — or a harder one? And what does your answer say about the world we are building?
The closer. This connects the abstract to the deeply personal. Most people in Mexico are not optimistic about this for their children. That feeling — named out loud in English — is one of the most powerful moments a language class can produce. Don't rush the silence after this one.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One final question. No easy answer.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot
Your company offers you a promotion. The new salary is 35% more than what you earn now. But you find out that a colleague with less experience and fewer results also got the same promotion — because his father is a personal friend of the director. You have two choices: accept the promotion and say nothing, or turn it down in protest and look for a new job. What do you do — and what does that choice cost you?
