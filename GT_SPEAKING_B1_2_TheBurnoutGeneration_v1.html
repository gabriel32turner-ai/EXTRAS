§ 01 · The Trigger
🪝 When Hard Work Stops Paying Off
Read aloud or project · First reaction only · No debate yet · 4 min max
01
4 min
🔥
Gallup State of the Global Workplace + WHO Burnout Classification 2023
77%
Of workers have experienced burnout at their current job — 28% say they feel burned out "very often" or "always"
$322B
Annual global cost of employee burnout in lost productivity, healthcare, and turnover — equivalent to 5% of global GDP
2× more
Burned-out employees are twice as likely to quit their job within the next year — and 63% more likely to take a sick day
2019
The year the WHO officially classified burnout as an occupational phenomenon — not a personal weakness or character flaw
Sources: Gallup State of the Global Workplace 2023 · WHO International Classification of Diseases (ICD-11) · Deloitte Workplace Burnout Survey · McKinsey Health Institute
🪝 Opening Trigger — Read this, then point at someone
"In 2019, the World Health Organization officially recognized burnout as a real medical condition — not laziness, not weakness, not a personal problem. It took them until 2019. Why do you think it took so long?"
Teacher Mechanics — 4 min Read the WHO statement once, slowly. The question "why did it take so long?" is the hook — it opens the cultural conversation immediately. Point at someone: "First reaction." No judgment. 2–3 reactions max. "Hold everything. We come back in §4."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The WHO 2019 classification is the most powerful fact in this session — it reframes burnout from a personal moral failure to a recognized medical condition. Most students will not know this. Let the surprise work.
  • The $322B cost stat is useful for students who work in corporate environments — it makes burnout a business problem, not just a personal one. That framing resonates in manufacturing and corporate settings.
  • If the group is quiet: "How many of you have felt burned out at work at some point in the last two years? Raise your hand." Then: "How many of you told your boss?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • This topic lands immediately in most corporate groups — especially at Apex Tool Group where production pressure is real. The emotional activation requires almost no push.
  • Frame it professionally: "This is not a therapy session — it's a conversation about a workplace reality that affects performance, retention, and your career."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Retention: "Being able to discuss burnout, workload, and boundaries in English is increasingly expected in international corporate roles. HR teams in the US and Europe talk about this in every leadership meeting."

Challenge Mode Available

💡 10 Nouns — Work, Exhaustion & Recovery
🔵 10 Verbs — What Burned-Out People Do (and Don't Do)
⚡ Production Challenge — No preparation
Each person picks 3 words. One sentence each about your real work life right now — honest. No writing. Just talk.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • "Burned out" vs "tired" — the distinction matters: tired is temporary; burnout is chronic and affects motivation, identity, and purpose. Clarify once and move on.
  • "Detach" — often confused with "disconnect." Clarify: detach means emotionally separating from work (not caring anymore); disconnect means physically turning off devices. Both appear in burnout conversations.
  • Best production challenge trigger: "Which 3 words describe how you feel on Sunday evening when you think about the next work week?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "These words appear in every performance review, HR survey, and leadership training at international companies. If someone asks you how you are doing professionally, these are the words you need."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Burnout vocabulary is essential for any professional working in a bilingual or international environment. HR managers at US headquarters will ask about workload, boundaries, and recovery in English. You now have the words."
§ 03 · Opinion Wall

03
10 min
Processing Question — Same for all 4 voices
"Who do you agree with most — and who sounds most like your boss?"
Don't vary the question
🔁 Processing Question — Pairs → Group
"Who do you agree with most — and who sounds most like your boss?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The processing question ("who sounds most like your boss?") is deliberately different from the usual "who do you agree with" — it creates distance, allows honesty, and often generates a laugh that opens the group.
  • The provocateur is Ing. Castellanos. His position — that burnout is a label for people who lack resilience — will create friction, especially with students who have experienced real burnout. Push: "He's a plant director. Would you say this to his face?"
  • Renata's position (she quit a high-paying job and recovered) is the most aspirational and will resonate emotionally. Don't let students stay in that emotional space — push back: "But not everyone can afford to quit. What about them?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Best activation: "Quick question — when was the last time you felt genuinely rested after a weekend? Not just less tired. Actually rested. Take a moment." The pause itself is the activation.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Testimonial trigger: "One sentence — which of these four people best describes where you are right now professionally? Be honest."
§ 04 · The Conversation Engine
🗣 10 Questions. 4 Blocks. 23 Minutes.
Personal → Opinion → Hypothetical → Provocative · No round-robin · Point and push
04
23 min
⚡
Rule: Minimum 2 sentences per answer. Position first. Real example second. No generic answers — connect it to your actual work life.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Block 1 present perfect — watch for "I have work too much" (missing -ed) or "I have felt burn out." Correct once: "So you have felt burned out — tell me what that was like."
  • Q9 is the most personal and most likely to generate honest answers. Silence after asking is productive — wait a full 6 seconds before redirecting.
  • Block 3: watch for "If I would have more time" — note and recycle at end only. Don't interrupt the flow.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • When "overwhelmed," "detach," or "boundaries" appear naturally — celebrate immediately: "That word — burnout vocabulary in a real sentence. That's what today is building."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • After Q10: "You just had a conversation about work, identity, and limits — in English. These are the conversations that happen in international HR meetings. You're ready for them."
  • Upsell: "The next Speaking Lab in this health series looks at toxic positivity — when 'stay positive' becomes the problem. Same honesty, different angle."
§ 05 · The Exit Shot
💣 One Question. No Easy Answer.
5 min · No chips · Just position + reason · Then the loop
05
5 min
EXIT
💣 Exit Shot
You have been in your job for 4 years. You are good at it. But for the last 8 months you wake up tired, you have no motivation, and you have been doing only the minimum. Your performance reviews are still fine — nobody has noticed. You know you are burned out. Do you tell your boss — or do you keep going and hope it passes?
Answer in 3 sentences. Be specific — what do you actually do, and what do you think the real risk is in each choice?
📌 B1.2 Model Answer — Reveal AFTER student speaks
"I would not tell my boss — not yet. In my company, showing vulnerability has never ended well for the person who showed it. What I would do first is take a real vacation — not a long weekend, a full week offline — and see if I can recover enough to think clearly. If it still hasn't changed after that, then I'd consider the conversation. Burnout doesn't fix itself, but timing matters."
🔁 Retention Loop — The WhatsApp Homework "Before our next session — record a 60-second voice note in English. Your answer to today's Exit Shot. Don't write it first. Don't rehearse. Just talk. Send it to [teacher number]."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The "nobody has noticed" detail is critical — it removes external pressure and forces a purely internal decision. The question is about self-honesty, not performance management.
  • Follow-up only: "What would you do if your boss noticed before you said anything — and asked if everything was okay?" Second conditional naturally activated, plus present perfect if they set the scene.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The model answer is strategically honest — "showing vulnerability has never ended well." That realism gives students permission to be politically astute rather than idealistic. Point it out: "This model answer doesn't say 'just be honest with your boss.' It says: timing matters. That's a B1.2 nuance."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Closing: "You just described a real professional situation — burnout, risk, decision-making — in English. Most people can't do that clearly in their first language. You just did."
  • Referral trigger: "If a colleague of yours is in that exact situation right now — tired, doing the minimum, nobody noticing — they need this conversation. This is what English for professionals looks like."
