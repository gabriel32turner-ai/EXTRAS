🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Personal stories first. Opinions second. Show tips only after the student answers.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever had a gym partner, a running friend, or someone who motivated you to exercise? What happened to that relationship?
Present perfect opener — most students have had this. Model: "Yes — I had a coworker who went to the gym with me every Tuesday for about a year. Then he changed shifts and we stopped going. I didn't go for four months after that." Activate: gym buddy, motivation, quit, routine.
Q2
Have you ever felt judged or compared at the gym — by a friend, a trainer, or even a stranger? How did it affect you?
Carolina's experience. Many students — especially women — have this story. Keep the space safe. Model: "A trainer once told me my technique was wrong in front of everyone. I was so embarrassed I didn't go back to that gym for two weeks." Activate: judge, body image, compliment, comparison.
Q3
Have you ever stopped exercising because someone in your life — friend, partner, or family — made you feel bad about your body or your progress?
A sensitive but very common story. Let the student decide how much to share. If they say no — great: "What helped you stay consistent?" If yes, acknowledge it and keep moving. Activate: body image, quit, motivation, judge.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you prefer to exercise alone or with someone? Why?
Simple and personal — great for B1.1. Ricardo vs. Mateo argument. Both answers are valid. Push: "Has your answer always been the same? Or has it changed based on experience?" Activate: motivation, routine, goal, stick to.
Q5
Is it OK to comment on a friend's body — even if you think you're being helpful or motivating?
The central body image question. Most students will say no — but push: "What about a compliment? What about 'You look great, you've lost weight'? Is that also a problem?" The answer: it can be, depending on the person. Activate: compliment, body image, judge, support.
Q6
Do you think fitness influencers on social media help people get healthy — or do they mostly make people feel bad about themselves?
References the Forbes Trigger. Most students have strong opinions here. Good for modals: "They should be more honest about..." "They might actually help some people but..." Activate: body image, compare, motivation, goal.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your gym buddy kept canceling, and you realized you only go when they go?
Second conditional — directly relevant for many students. Model: "If that happened, I would try to go alone for one week and see if I could keep going. If I couldn't — I'd find a new gym buddy or try a different type of exercise." Activate: motivation, give up, quit, stick to.
Q8
What would you say to a friend who keeps making negative comments about your body at the gym — even if they think they're being helpful?
Second conditional focused on direct communication. Good for practicing difficult conversations at B1.1 level. Model: "I would say: 'I know you want to help, but those comments make me feel worse, not better. Can you stop?'" Activate: judge, support, compare, body image.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — have you ever felt jealous of a friend's body or fitness progress? Did it affect your friendship?
Difficult to admit but very common. Most students will say yes — or deflect. Model: "Yes — I had a friend who lost a lot of weight after a diet and I felt happy for her but also a little jealous. I never said anything. I'm not proud of it." Activate: jealousy, compare, competition.
Q10
Do you exercise for your health — or partly because you care about how you look to other people? Be honest.
The self-reflection close. Almost everyone exercises for both reasons — but admitting the appearance motivation is harder. Model: "Honestly — both. I want to be healthy. But I also want to look good. I think that's human. The problem is when the appearance part starts to hurt you." Don't judge either answer.
B1.1 grammar check: Q1–Q3 = present perfect. Q7–Q8 = second conditional. Q8 is especially good for practicing conversational indirect speech: "I would tell them that..." / "I would say..."

Sensitivity note: Body image can be a painful topic for some students. Keep the tone warm and non-judgmental throughout. If a student shares something difficult about their body, acknowledge it and move on gently. The point is self-reflection, not therapy.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. One honest answer.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
Your best friend asks you to be their gym partner. You know they will be consistent and motivating — but you also know they tend to make comments about other people's bodies, including yours sometimes. Do you say yes? And what would you do the first time they made a comment you didn't like?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Tell me about a time someone's comment — at the gym, about your body, or about your health — affected how you felt. What did they say? How did you react?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: The Exit Shot puts the student in a real decision with competing values — friendship, motivation, and self-protection. The second question ("what would you do the first time they made a comment?") forces them to plan a difficult conversation, which is exactly the kind of real-life English skill B1.1 students need.

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about sleeping pills and natural remedies — another health topic where people have very strong personal opinions. Very different energy from today, but just as honest."
