Office Politics
and Favoritism

Who gets promoted, who gets ignored — and how much of it is about talent.

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
Harvard Business Review · 2022
"Research shows that employees who have a personal relationship with their manager are 2.5 times more likely to be promoted — regardless of performance scores."
Teacher script: Read the stat. Pause. Then ask: "Does this surprise you? Or does it just confirm something you already knew?" Let students react for 90 seconds. Most will have a personal story — let them surface it early.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever seen someone get promoted who was less qualified than their colleagues? What happened?
Teacher tip: Almost everyone has a story. Push for specifics: "How long had the other person been waiting? What was the reaction of the team?"
Q2
Have you ever played office politics — done something specifically to be liked by your boss or a powerful colleague?
Model answer: "Yes. I've laughed at my boss's jokes even when they weren't funny. I've volunteered for projects I didn't want because I knew he was watching. I'm not proud of it — but it worked."
Q3
Have you ever felt jealous of a coworker who was clearly the favorite? How did you manage that feeling?
Teacher tip: Reference Paola's guilt about her jealousy. Normalize the emotion: jealousy at work is one of the most common and least talked-about feelings. Let it surface.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
In your opinion, is building political relationships at work smart strategy — or is it a form of dishonesty?
Teacher tip: This is the core tension of the session. Some students will agree with Diego completely. Others will find it morally wrong. Make both sides argue their position clearly before moving on.
Q5
Do you think companies where favoritism is common lose their best people? Why?
Model answer: "Yes, absolutely. The best people have options. If they see that effort isn't being rewarded, they leave. The ones who stay are either the favorites or the people who can't leave. That's a terrible culture."
Q6
What do you think is worse: a boss who openly favors someone, or a boss who claims to be fair but secretly plays favorites?
Teacher tip: The hypocrisy angle generates strong reactions. Most students will say the secret favoritism is worse. Ask: "Why does the dishonesty make it worse than the unfairness itself?"
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you found out that your promotion was partly due to favoritism — not just your results?
Model answer: "Honestly, I think I would feel uncomfortable — but I probably wouldn't give it back. I'd try to prove I deserved it after the fact. But I would feel the pressure to perform better than anyone else."
Q8
What would you do if you were a director and you realized you had been playing favorites without noticing?
Teacher tip: This question produces genuine self-reflection. Good follow-up: "Do you think you already do this in some areas of your life — at home, with friends?"
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Have you ever been the favorite — and used that position to benefit yourself or protect someone else?
Teacher tip: Flip the script from victim to actor. Students who admitted to jealousy in Q3 now have to reflect on the other side. Some will realize they have also benefited from favorable treatment. Let the discomfort work.
Q10
Be honest: if you became a director tomorrow, would you naturally give more opportunities to people you personally like? Why?
Teacher tip: The most honest question of the session. Most people, if they're truthful, will admit they would. That's human. The conversation is about whether they would notice it and correct it — or just rationalize it.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
You have been working hard for 2 years. Your colleague — who is close friends with the director — just got the promotion you deserved. HR confirms the decision is final. You have three options: confront the director, complain to HR's manager, or accept it and quietly start looking for another job. Which do you choose — and what does that choice say about you?
🎙️
Homework — WhatsApp Voice Note
Record a 60-second voice message in English. Choose one of the three options from the Exit Shot and explain your decision. Be specific and honest — not the answer you think sounds best, but what you would actually do.
