Working From Home:
Dream or Trap?

Flexibility, isolation, midnight emails — what remote work really costs you.

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
BBC Worklife · 2023
"Remote workers report higher productivity — but also higher rates of loneliness, anxiety, and difficulty disconnecting from work after hours."
Teacher script: Read the headline slowly. Then ask: "So — is remote work a promotion or a punishment? What was your experience when you first started working from home?" Let two or three students react before moving on.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever worked from home for a long period? Was it mostly positive or mostly negative?
Teacher tip: Don't let students say just "positive" or "negative." Push: "Give me one concrete thing that got better and one concrete thing that got worse."
Q2
Have you ever received a work message after 9 PM? Did you answer it? How did you feel?
Model answer: "Yes, my manager has sent messages on Sunday mornings. I've answered them because I'm afraid of looking lazy — but I've been feeling resentful about it for months."
Q3
Have you ever felt lonely or disconnected from your team while working remotely?
Teacher tip: This is a vulnerable question. Normalize it: "A lot of people felt this — it doesn't mean you're weak. It means you're human."
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think remote workers are less likely to get promoted than people who go to the office? Why?
Teacher tip: Reference Fernando's opinion. Ask: "Is it fair or unfair that visibility affects promotions?" Both answers generate strong conversation.
Q5
In your opinion, should companies have the right to call you back to the office after you've been working remotely for years?
Model answer: "I think it depends on the contract. If they promised remote work, they shouldn't be able to change it. But if the contract says 'the company decides,' then legally they can — even if it's not fair."
Q6
What do you think is the real reason companies want people back in the office — productivity or control?
Teacher tip: This will generate strong opinions. Let it run. Common views: it's about control and real estate costs. Push for evidence: "What makes you think that?"
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your company asked you to return to the office full-time starting next Monday?
Model answer: "Honestly, I would probably comply — I need the job. But I would also start looking for another position that offers hybrid work, because I've already adjusted my life to remote."
Q8
What would your ideal work arrangement look like — full remote, hybrid, or office? Why exactly?
Teacher tip: Push for specifics: how many days? Which days? What time? Why those? The more concrete the answer, the better the language production.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest: have you ever pretended to be working while at home when you were actually doing something else?
Teacher tip: The best question in the session. Most people laugh immediately — that's the answer. Don't judge. This opens a real conversation about trust, autonomy, and results-vs-presence culture.
Q10
Have you ever judged a colleague for being too comfortable working from home — and later realized you were wrong?
Teacher tip: Self-awareness question. Give 15 seconds of silence before asking for answers. This produces the most honest language of the session.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
Your company is offering two options: keep your remote position at your current salary, or accept a 20% raise and return to the office 5 days a week. You have 48 hours to decide. What do you choose — and what does that tell you about yourself?
