Mexican Stereotypes
Abroad

What people assume about you before you open your mouth — and what you do about it.

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
BBC · Latin America Correspondent · 2023
"Mexico is one of the most visited countries in the world and one of the most misrepresented. Most foreigners' image of Mexico comes from news coverage and Netflix — not from the 130 million people who actually live there."
Teacher script: Read the quote. Then ask directly: "Has a foreigner ever said something to you about Mexico that was completely wrong — or that made you feel reduced to a cliché? What did they say?" Let the first personal story open the session.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever been abroad and felt that someone had a wrong or limited idea about Mexico or Mexicans? What happened?
Teacher tip: This is the most personal question of the session. Push for the specific moment: "What exactly did they say? Where were you? What did you do?" Don't rush past it — this is where the real energy of the session lives.
Q2
Have you ever felt proud to be Mexican in a situation abroad — a moment when your nationality was actually an advantage?
Model answer: "Yes. Once in a business dinner in Canada, the moment I mentioned I was from Mexico, everyone at the table wanted to talk about food, travel, and culture. For 20 minutes I wasn't the engineer — I was the most interesting person there."
Q3
Have you ever changed how you presented yourself professionally because of how you thought foreigners might perceive you?
Teacher tip: Sensitive question — let it land. Some students will admit they dressed differently, changed their accent, avoided mentioning their city. That's a real conversation about identity and code-switching.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
In your opinion, is it your responsibility to correct stereotypes about Mexico when you meet foreigners — or is that exhausting and not your job?
Teacher tip: This divides the room. Some feel strong responsibility. Others feel the emotional labour is unfair. Both positions are valid and both produce strong language. Let both speak fully.
Q5
What do you think is the most damaging stereotype about Mexico that affects Mexican professionals abroad?
Teacher tip: Common answers: the safety/violence narrative, the assumption of lower education levels, the "mañana culture" stereotype about punctuality and reliability. Let students name it directly — they usually know exactly which one hurts most professionally.
Q6
Do you think Mariana is right — that Mexicans also promote stereotypes of themselves? Where do you see that happening?
Teacher tip: The most provocative opinion of the wall. Expect some resistance, some agreement. Push: "Can you give a specific example — something you've seen on social media, in advertising, or in how Mexicans present themselves internationally?"
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if a foreign colleague told you in a meeting that they had heard Mexico was "not really a business-serious country"?
Model answer: "I would stay calm and ask them where they heard that. Then I would give them two or three concrete facts — Mexico is the 12th largest economy in the world, it exports more than the rest of Latin America combined. I wouldn't be angry — I would be very specific."
Q8
What would you do if you were offered a job abroad but you knew the company had a reputation for treating Latin American employees differently?
Teacher tip: This is a real dilemma many of your students may face or have already faced. Push for concrete thinking: "Would you investigate first? Would you ask in the interview? Would you take the risk anyway?"
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Have you ever had a stereotype about another country or culture that turned out to be completely wrong when you actually met someone from there?
Teacher tip: This flips the conversation from victim to actor. Students who are frustrated by stereotypes about Mexico now have to reflect on their own assumptions about other cultures. Creates the most honest self-reflection of the session.
Q10
Be honest: is there anything about Mexico that you think foreigners get right — even if it's uncomfortable to admit?
Teacher tip: The best closing question of this lab. Requires real courage to answer honestly. Give 15 seconds of silence. Students who answer truthfully here produce the most memorable moments of the session. Do not rush them.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
You are in a job interview abroad. The interviewer says: "I have to be honest with you — we've had some challenges with candidates from Latin America in terms of communication style and punctuality. Can you tell me why you would be different?" You need this job. What do you say — and what do you feel while you say it?
