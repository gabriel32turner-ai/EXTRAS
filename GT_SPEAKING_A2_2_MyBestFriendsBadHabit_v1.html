My Best Friend's Bad Habit

We all love our friends — but sometimes they do something that drives us crazy. What is your friend's worst habit?

🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
⏱ 4 min
"The most common complaint people have about their friends: always late, always on the phone, always borrowing money and not paying back. Sound familiar?"
— YouGov Friendship Survey
Teacher script: "Think about your best friend. Do you love them? Of course! But — do they do something that makes you a little crazy?" [Smile and pause.] "Today we talk about our friends' bad habits. And maybe — our own."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
What is your best friend's worst habit? Tell me about it.
Simple present for description — "My friend always..." Let them tell a story in the present or past. The most common answers: always late, always on the phone, always complains, borrows money. All generate great conversation.
Q2
Did you ever talk to a friend about a habit that bothered you? What did you say?
Past simple. "Yes, I talked to them and I said..." / "No, I never said anything because..." Both answers are equally interesting. Ask follow-up: "And did they change?" — spoiler: usually not, which is funny.
Q3
Did a friend's habit ever create a real problem between you? What happened?
Past simple narrative. The "that was the last straw" moment. Model: "Yes, my friend cancelled our plans three times. I was very angry. We did not talk for two weeks." Simple past + sequence of events.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Is it good to tell a friend when they have a bad habit — or is it better to say nothing?
Simple opinion: "I think it is good to say something because..." / "I think it is better to say nothing because..." No complex grammar needed. The answers reveal a lot about personality: avoiders vs direct communicators.
Q5
What do you think is worse: a friend who is always late, or a friend who is always on their phone?
Comparison question — good for "I think... is worse than... because..." Very easy to answer, generates quick debate. Ask: "Which one is YOUR friend?" This connects opinion to personal experience naturally.
Q6
Do you think people can really change a bad habit? Why or why not?
Simple present opinion. "I think people can change because..." / "I think it is very difficult to change because..." Good for can/can't modal. Real experience often shows the honest answer: usually no. That is okay to say.
Block 3 — Future Plans (Going To / Will)
Q7
Are you going to say something to your friend about their habit — or are you going to stay quiet?
Going to for plans. "I'm going to talk to them next week because..." / "I'm not going to say anything because..." Connects to a real person and a real situation. Many students choose "stay quiet" — ask "are you sure that is a good idea?"
Q8
Will you try to change one of your own bad habits this year? Which one?
Will for future decision. "Yes, I will try to... because..." / "No, I don't think I will because..." Fernando's honest self-reflection moment. Most students have something they know they should change. Keep it light and funny.
Block 4 — Past Habits & Reflection (Used To)
Q9
Did you use to have a bad habit that you changed? What was it?
Used to + positive change. "I used to... but now I..." Great structure for A2.2. Common answers: "I used to be very late," "I used to eat too much junk food," "I used to look at my phone all the time." All very accessible.
Q10
Did you use to accept all your friends' habits — or were you always honest with them?
Used to + self-reflection on past behavior. "I used to accept everything. Now I say something." / "I was always honest. Sometimes too honest." Connects the whole topic to personal growth. Ends the engine on a warm, personal note.

⏱ 5 min
Final Question
