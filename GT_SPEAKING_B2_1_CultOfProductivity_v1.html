The Cult of
Productivity
We optimized our calendars, tracked our habits, and built morning routines. Did we become more productive — or just better at performing productivity?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
A real headline. A real debate.
⏱ 4 min
Harvard Business Review · 2023
"The productivity self-help industry is worth $15 billion a year. Most of its customers report feeling less productive than ever. We've never had more tools for getting things done — and never felt more overwhelmed."
Teacher Script
"I want you to think about the last time you genuinely did nothing for a full day — no phone, no podcast, no list, no 'productive rest.' How long ago was that? And did you feel guilty about it? Today we're going to question something most of us take for granted: the idea that being productive is always good."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real talk. No right answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Describe your morning routine. Now tell me — is it actually something you enjoy, or is it a performance you put on for yourself?
The discomfort is the point. Many people have routines they don't enjoy but maintain for identity reasons. Push: "If no one would ever know — would you still do it?" That question breaks the performance layer.
Q2
Have you ever used productivity tools — apps, planners, time-blocking, habit trackers? Did they actually work, or did they just add more stress?
Great vocabulary activation moment. Most people have tried Notion, Todoist, Pomodoro, etc. Ask: "What happened when you fell off the system?" — because that failure moment tells you everything about whether the tool was serving them or controlling them.
Q3
When was the last time you did something completely unproductive — and didn't feel guilty about it? How long did it take?
Most adults can't remember. The guilt is the tell. If they say "I watch Netflix" — push: "But do you feel like you should be doing something else while you watch it?" That anxious multi-tasking impulse is hustle culture in the nervous system.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Is "I'm so busy" a complaint — or a brag? Why do people wear exhaustion as a status symbol?
Model answer: "I think for most professionals it's both at the same time — they genuinely feel overwhelmed, but they also need others to see that. Busyness signals importance. The moment you say 'actually, I have a lot of free time,' people assume you're not good at your job."
Q5
Sofía worked 70-hour weeks and says it was worth it. Is that admirable, or is it a rationalization for a life she can never get back?
The spiciest question in the block. "Worth it" is always determined in retrospect — and the person saying it is always the one who won. Ask: "What about the people who worked just as hard and didn't get the title or the salary?" Were their 70-hour weeks worth it too?
Q6
Should companies measure people by hours worked, tasks completed, or results delivered? Which system is fairest — and which one do you actually work under?
Great gap between ideal and reality. Most people say "results" but work under "presence." Ask: "Does your manager notice when you're physically there — even if you're not doing much? What does that tell you about how your company actually measures value?"
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
If you could get the exact same salary and career results working 4 hours a day — would you? Or would something feel wrong about it?
Most people say yes immediately — then hesitate. Push: "What would you do with those extra 4 hours?" If they can't answer easily, they've lost the ability to want things outside of work. That's the real cost of hustle culture.
Q8
If your child told you at age 25 that they were working 80-hour weeks because "that's what it takes" — what would you say to them?
The classic flip: advice we give others vs. what we do ourselves. Model answer: "I'd tell them to check why — if it's a temporary sprint for a specific goal, fine. If it's just the culture of the company, I'd tell them to get out. No job is worth your health at 25."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Is it possible that you're addicted to productivity — and that you use "being busy" to avoid thinking about things that actually matter?
The most uncomfortable question in the lab. Busyness as avoidance — of relationships, of existential questions, of unhappiness. Some of the most productive people in the world are running from something. Don't comfort. Let the question breathe. The silence after is gold.
Q10
If you removed every productivity system, every tracker, every routine from your life for 30 days — who would you be? Does that answer scare you?
The identity closer. When productivity becomes identity, losing the system means losing the self. This is where the real conversation about values begins. What's left when you take the calendar away? If they say "I don't know" — that's the most honest and important answer in the room.
