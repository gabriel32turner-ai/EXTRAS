People Who Talk Too Much
vs. People Who Talk Too Little

Some people never stop. Some people never start. Which one is more annoying — and which one are YOU?

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Let's Talk Starter
⏱ 4 min
A simple question to start
"In every office, every family, and every group of friends — there is one person who talks too much, and one person who is always quiet. Which one is more difficult to be with? And be honest: which one are you?"
Teacher Script: Ask directly: "OK — which one are you? Too much or too little?" Then: "Think of someone in your life who talks too much. Don't say the name — but describe them. What do they talk about?" This gets the student describing real people immediately. Keep it fun and light.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience — Past Simple
Q1
When was the last time someone talked too much and it drove you crazy? What happened?
Target: past simple narrative. Push: "Where were you? What did you do — did you say something or just listen?" Model: "Last week. A meeting at work. One person talked for 30 minutes. Same idea, 10 times. I looked at my watch 6 times. Everyone was tired. Nobody said anything." Short and real.
Q2
Did you ever feel uncomfortable because a person was too quiet — and you didn't know what they were thinking?
Target: past simple + emotion. Push: "Who was it? A boss, a date, a new person?" Model: "Yes. A new coworker. He never talked. He just listened. I always thought: is he angry? Does he like me? Does he have a problem with me? After 2 months I knew — he's just quiet. But the first weeks were difficult." Connects to Carolina's bubble.
Q3
Did someone interrupt you in a conversation last week? What did you do?
Target: past simple. Push: "Did you say something — or did you just stop?" Model: "Yes. My sister. She always interrupts me. I was talking and she started a new topic. I stopped. I said 'I wasn't finished.' She said 'sorry' and I continued. But it was annoying." Good for activating the verb "interrupt."
Block 2 · Opinion — Simple Present
Q4
Which is more annoying — a person who talks too much or a person who is always quiet?
The central binary. Push: "Why? Give me a specific example." Target: I think + comparative (more annoying than). Model: "For me, talking too much is worse. I need silence. When a person talks and talks I can't think. But a quiet person is fine — I don't need to talk every minute." Gets strong opinions.
Q5
Do you think quiet people are shy — or just smart?
Connects to Ernesto's observation. Push: "What is the difference between shy and quiet?" Target: I think / maybe + present simple. Model: "I think it's different. Shy people want to talk but they're afraid. Quiet people choose to listen. One is a problem. The other is a personality. I prefer quiet people — they listen better."
Q6
In your job, is it better to talk a lot — or to be quiet and work?
Very context-specific. Push: "What about your current job — what is more important?" Target: it's better to + infinitive + because. Model: "In my job, you need both. Quiet when you work. Talk when there's a problem or meeting. People who only talk and don't work are the problem — not the quiet ones."
Block 3 · Future Plans — Going to / Will
Q7
Is there a person at work or in your family who talks too much? Are you going to say something to them — or not?
Target: going to + will for plans and intentions. Push: "What are you going to say? Give me the words." Model: "My coworker Carmen. She talks every morning for 30 minutes. I'm going to say 'Carmen, I need to work now, sorry.' Nice but direct. I'm going to do it next Monday. Maybe." The "maybe" is natural and valid.
Q8
Next time a person interrupts you in a conversation, what are you going to do?
Target: going to + direct action. Push: "Say the exact words." Model: "I'm going to say 'excuse me, I wasn't finished' and continue talking. I don't want to be rude. But I also don't want to stop and lose my idea." Gets students producing real, usable English phrases for real situations.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Be honest — do YOU talk too much sometimes? When?
Self-reflection — most students will laugh and admit something. Push: "When do you talk the most — when you're nervous, happy, angry?" Target: present simple + specific situations. Model: "Yes. When I'm nervous, I talk a lot. Too much. I say the same thing 3 times. I know. My friends say 'yes, yes, we heard you.'" Honest and fun.
Q10
A new person in your office is very quiet. Nobody knows them. People say "they are rude." Do you agree — or do you think that's not fair?
Simple scenario with a mild cultural tension. Push: "Is quiet the same as rude?" Target: present simple + I think that's fair / I think that's not fair. Model: "No, that's not fair. Quiet is not rude. Quiet is just quiet. Maybe they are nervous, maybe they are shy, maybe they just need more time. People jump to conclusions too fast." Good closer with a clear position.
💣
The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One Final Scenario
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
Tomorrow you are going to have lunch with a person who talks too much. They always talk about the same things. You have a problem at work and you want to talk about it. But they are going to talk first — and they are not going to stop. What are you going to do?
Homework Loop: "Record a 60-second WhatsApp voice message. Tell me: what are you going to do — and what are you going to say to this person? In English. Simple words are perfect."
