Bad Drivers in My City
You see them every day — the driver who runs the red light, parks on the sidewalk, or uses their phone. Why is driving in Mexico so stressful?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One simple question. Everyone has an answer.
⏱ 4 min
INEGI · Mexico Road Safety Report · 2022
"Mexico has one of the highest rates of road accidents in Latin America. Every day, about 105 people die in traffic accidents in the country."
Bad driving is one of the most talked-about problems in Mexican daily life. Many drivers run red lights, park anywhere, talk on their phone, or drive very close to other cars. But almost everyone thinks the other driver is the problem — not them.
Teacher Script Read the stat. Then ask directly: "Think about this morning — did you see a bad driver on the way here? What did they do?" Let 2–3 students describe quickly in simple sentences. That's your opening. Keep it light and funny — this is a topic everyone loves to complain about.

Discussion prompt: "Which person do you agree with? Which one is most honest?" Jorge is the soft provocateur — his honesty about his own bad driving is simple but effective at A2.2 level. He admits what others won't say. Students often laugh and then quietly agree.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Short answers are perfect. Use past simple and going to.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
What did the last bad driver you saw do? Describe it.
Teacher Tip Past simple storytelling. Help them with: "He/She drove very fast." "He/She didn't stop." "The driver cut in front of me." Keep it simple and encourage full sentences. Almost everyone has a story from today or yesterday.
Q2
Did you get angry at a driver this week? What happened?
Teacher Tip Past simple — "I got angry because..." or "Yes, a driver cut in front of me and I honked." If they say no, ask: "Really? Not even a little?" Usually they remember something. Keep the mood light — this is a fun topic.
Q3
When was the last time you were late because of traffic? How long did you wait?
Teacher Tip Past simple + time expressions: "I was late last Monday." "I waited 45 minutes." "The traffic was terrible on the highway." Numbers and time words are great at A2.2. Encourage specific details.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
What is the most dangerous thing drivers do in your city?
Teacher Tip Simple present opinion. Target answers: using the phone, running red lights, driving fast, not respecting pedestrians. Push for "I think..." at the start of their answer. Accept short answers — "I think using the phone is very dangerous."
Q5
Is it okay to use your phone when you drive? Why or why not?
Teacher Tip Simple opinion question with "It's okay" / "It's not okay" as scaffolding. Push: "Do you do it?" Most people will say no — but they do. Light humor works here. Model answer: "No, it's not okay. It's very dangerous. But many people do it."
Q6
Do you think the police do enough to stop bad drivers in your city?
Teacher Tip Opinion with "I think the police..." Model: "I think the police don't do enough. I never see police on the road." Or: "Sometimes they stop drivers, but not many." Keep it descriptive, not political.
Block 3 · Future Plans (Going to / Will)
Q7
Are you going to drive somewhere this weekend? Where?
Teacher Tip Going to for concrete plans. Model: "Yes, I'm going to drive to my mom's house on Sunday." "No, I'm not going to drive — I'm going to stay home." Simple and personal. Ask follow-up: "Is the traffic bad there?"
Q8
Will driving in Mexico get better in the future — or will it stay the same?
Teacher Tip Will for predictions. Model: "I think it will get better — maybe." "I don't think it will change. People are the same." Keep it simple. The goal is using "will" correctly, not a complex analysis.
Block 4 · Past Habits (Used to)
Q9
Did you use to take public transport before you had a car? Was it better or worse than driving?
Teacher Tip Used to for past habits. Model: "I used to take the bus every day. It was slow but cheaper." If they still use public transport, ask: "What was it like when you were younger?" Keep it descriptive and personal.
Q10 · Self-Reflection
Honestly — did you ever do something bad when you were driving? What did you do?
Teacher Tip The Jorge moment — inviting honest self-reflection with a smile. This is not serious or judgmental. Model: "Yes, I ran a red light last week." "I used my phone once." The humor and honesty create a great closing to Block 4. Laugh with them.
💣
Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. Record your answer in English.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · A2.2 Bad Drivers
Tomorrow you are going to drive to work. A driver cuts in front of you and almost hits your car. What are you going to do — and what do you want to say to that driver?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. Tell me what you are going to do AND what you want to say. Be honest — you can say it in English now!
Retention Hook The "what do you want to say" part creates funny, honest answers — students love to express road rage in English for the first time. The voice note becomes a fun homework because they can say things they normally only say in Spanish. End: "Send me the voice note. I want to hear your English road rage."
