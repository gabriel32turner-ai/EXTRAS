Bosses Who
Play Favorites

The colleague who gets everything. The boss who sees everything — except you. And how angry it really makes you.

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
Gallup · Workplace Report · 2022
"56% of employees say their boss has a clear favorite on the team. Of those employees, 71% say it has made them want to quit."
Teacher script: Read the numbers slowly. Then ask: "Raise your hand if you have ever worked with a boss who had a clear favorite." Wait. Then: "Keep your hand up if it made you angry." Almost everyone will keep it up. That's your entry point.

The Opinion Wall
Phase 3 · Who do you agree with?
10 min
👩
Lucía, 33 — Nurse, Monterrey
"My boss has a favorite and everyone knows it. She gets the best shifts, the easy patients, and nobody says anything to her when she's late. I've worked there for 5 years and I've never seen anything change. I'm thinking about leaving — not because of the job, but because of this."
👨
Rodrigo, 42 — Salesperson, Guadalajara
"I was the favorite for two years. My boss gave me the best clients, the best territory. Did I complain? No. When the new boss arrived, I was suddenly nobody. That's when I understood — being the favorite doesn't last. You have to build real skills, not just a relationship."
👨
Mateo, 47 — Manager, CDMX
provocateur
"I'm a manager and yes, I have people I trust more than others. That's not favoritism — that's reality. If someone does excellent work and builds a good relationship with me, of course I give them more. Why would I give the same opportunities to someone who does the minimum?"
👩
Sofía, 29 — Teacher, Querétaro
"In my school, the director has a favorite teacher — and it's not about the work. It's because they are friends outside of school. The rest of us work harder and get nothing. I think that's wrong, and I think everyone knows it's wrong, including the director."
💬 Choose one opinion. Who do you agree with? Does Mateo make you angry — or does he have a point? Tell your partner.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever worked with a boss who clearly had a favorite? Who was it and how did you know?
Teacher tip: Let students be specific. Push: "What exactly did the boss do for this person? Extra time off? Better projects? More money? Not getting in trouble for things others got in trouble for?" The specifics reveal the real anger.
Q2
Have you ever been jealous of a colleague because of how the boss treated them? Did you say anything?
Model answer: "Yes. My colleague got a promotion after only one year, and I had been there for four. I was very jealous. I didn't say anything to my boss — I just complained to my friends at work. I'm not proud of that, but it's the truth."
Q3
Have you ever been the favorite — and did it make you feel good or uncomfortable?
Teacher tip: Reference Rodrigo's story. Many students will admit this quietly. The honest ones say it felt good at first and then awkward. Follow up: "Did your colleagues know? How did that change things?"
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Is Mateo right? Is giving more to your best workers just smart management — or is it unfair?
Teacher tip: This is the core debate. Some will agree with Mateo. Most will resist. Push: "What if the 'favorite' really IS the best worker? Is that still favoritism?" The distinction between merit and friendship is where the real conversation lives.
Q5
Is it OK to be friends with your boss outside of work? Does it always cause problems?
Model answer: "I think it's OK to have a good relationship, but real friendship — going to parties, texting on weekends — I think that creates problems. The other team members can always see it, and they feel left out."
Q6
Do you believe most bosses know when they are playing favorites — or do they really not see it?
Teacher tip: Reference Sofía's opinion — she says the director knows. This question is about self-awareness and honesty. Great follow-up: "If you were a boss, do you think you would notice yourself doing it?"
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your colleague got promoted over you and you knew it was because of their friendship with the boss — not their work?
Model answer: "Honestly, I would be very angry. I think I would ask my boss for a meeting and say: 'I want to understand what I need to do to get a promotion. Can you tell me?' I wouldn't say 'it was unfair' directly — but I would make them explain it to my face."
Q8
What would you do if you were a manager and you realized you had been treating one employee better than the others without noticing?
Teacher tip: Keep it simple and concrete: "Would you say something to the team? Would you change your behavior quietly? Would you give the other employees something extra to compensate?"
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Have you ever tried to become the favorite — done something specifically to get closer to your boss?
Teacher tip: Most people will laugh or look down. That's a yes. Common examples: laughing at the boss's jokes, volunteering for things they hate, bringing coffee. Ask: "Did it work? Are you ashamed of it?"
Q10
Be honest: if you were a boss tomorrow, would you have a favorite? Who would it be and why?
Teacher tip: The best question of the session. Give 15 seconds of quiet before asking for answers. The students who answer honestly produce the most memorable moments in the room. Don't rush — wait for it.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
You and a colleague both want the same promotion. You work harder. But your colleague is the boss's friend. The decision is tomorrow. You have one chance to talk to your boss today. What do you say?
