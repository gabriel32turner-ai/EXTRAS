🪝 Phase 1 — The Trigger
The Headline
Read it aloud. Pause. Then ask the first question.
4 min
01
Gallup World Poll · 2023
"Mexico is one of the most stressed countries in the world — 42% of Mexicans report experiencing stress most of the day. The global average is 35%."
— That means almost half the people you see every day are carrying something heavy.
BBC Health
"Chronic stress doesn't just feel bad — it physically changes your body. It affects your sleep, your immune system, your heart, and even your memory."
— The body keeps the score. It always does.
Launch line: "Scale of 1 to 10 — how stressed are you right now? Not today specifically. In general, in your life." [Let them answer.] "What's the main thing causing it?" [One-word answer is fine.] "Today we talk about stress. Not in theory — your actual experience with it. The real moments when it got too much."

5 Colloquial Phrases — Real Stress Talk
Things people actually say · Click to hear
Colloquial
"I'm running on empty."
When you have nothing left — no energy, no patience, no reserves
"I was at my breaking point."
The moment just before you cry, quit, or do something you regret
"I just needed to breathe."
Wanting to escape — even for five minutes — from a stressful situation
"I snapped."
Lost control of your emotions — said something, cried, or reacted badly
"I couldn't take it anymore."
The sentence that comes just before a major decision — quit, leave, change something
After phrases, ask: "Which phrase describes a moment you've had?" Be careful — this topic can surface real pain for some students. Keep the energy present but not heavy. If a student shares something difficult, acknowledge it briefly and continue. Don't turn the class into a therapy session, but don't dismiss real moments either.

Provocateur
🙄
Carlos, 50
Engineer · Guadalajara
"Everyone is 'stressed' now. It's become fashionable. My father worked 14 hours a day and never complained about stress. I think we've become too sensitive. Real problems are hunger, poverty, illness. Feeling overwhelmed by emails is not a crisis. Toughen up."
Provocateur rationale: Carlos minimizes modern stress by comparing it to past generations and real hardship. This is a very common attitude in Mexican workplaces — especially from older managers. Students who have felt dismissed for being stressed will react strongly. Push: "Have you ever met a Carlos at work? How did that feel?" Use the anger productively.
🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Personal and honest. Show tips only after the student answers.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
What was the most stressful period of your life? What caused it — work, family, money, health, or something else?
Central storytelling question. Let the student narrate freely — past simple tense practice is natural here. Model briefly: "The most stressful time for me was when I changed jobs and had money problems at the same time. I didn't sleep properly for three months." Activate: stress, pressure, sleep, limit.
Q2
Have you ever felt stress physically — headaches, no sleep, stomach problems, or something else in your body?
Present perfect — almost everyone has a physical stress story. Model: "Yes — I used to get terrible headaches every Sunday night before the work week. My body knew before my mind did." Activate: headache, body, sleep, anxiety. Reference the BBC Trigger quote here.
Q3
Have you ever snapped at someone — a friend, family member, or coworker — because of stress, and later regretted it?
Present perfect with a story. Everyone has this one. Model: "Yes — I once said something very harsh to my partner during a difficult week at work. It wasn't about them at all. I felt terrible about it afterward." Activate: snap at someone, pressure, recover, support.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think stress is just part of modern life — or is there something wrong with the way we work and live?
Opens the societal dimension. Most B1.1 students say "both." Push: "What specifically do you think is wrong?" Good for "I think... because..." structures. Activate: pressure, limit, deadline, sleep. References Rodrigo's manageable view vs. the structural critique.
Q5
Is it OK to quit a job because of stress — even if it means earning less money?
Lucía's decision. Very relevant for Mexican professionals. Most say "it depends." Push: "If you had a family depending on your salary — would that change your answer?" Good for modals: "You should be able to... but you might have to..." Activate: break, limit, support.
Q6
Do you think younger generations are more stressed than your parents' generation — or do they just talk about it more?
Carlos's argument reframed as a fair question. Most students will say "both" again. The interesting part: "Is talking about stress helpful — or does it make it worse?" Activate: anxiety, cope, pressure. Good generational comparison discussion.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you woke up tomorrow and felt completely calm — no stress at all? What would change in your day?
Light second conditional — imaginative and revealing. Students often describe very simple changes: eating breakfast, not rushing, being kinder. The realization that stress is coloring their whole day is the point. Activate: break, slow down, cope, body.
Q8
What would you do if a close friend told you they were completely breaking down from stress — crying every day, not sleeping, not eating?
Second conditional focused on support. Good for "I would..." structures: "I would listen... I would take them to..." Also a mirror — what would they want someone to do for them? Activate: support, ask for help, break down, recover.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Do you ever feel proud of being busy and stressed — like it proves you work hard? Be honest.
The identity question — stress as a badge of honor. Many Mexican professionals wear busyness proudly. Most students will pause before answering honestly. Model: "I think sometimes yes — if I say 'I've been really busy,' it sounds like I'm important and needed. Which is a problem." Don't rush through this one.
Q10
Right now — is there something in your life causing you stress that you haven't talked to anyone about?
The most personal closing question in the B1.1 lineup. Handle with care. Give the student space to answer — or not. If they don't want to share, that's fine: "You don't have to tell me what it is. But is there someone in your life you could tell?" The point is the invitation to speak, not the disclosure.
B1.1 grammar check: Q2–Q3 = present perfect. Q7–Q8 = second conditional. Q10 works naturally with present continuous: "Is there something that is causing you stress right now?" which is exactly B1.1 level.

Emotional note: This lab can go deep. Some students use it to share something real. That is a success — it means they feel safe enough to speak. If the conversation becomes heavy, acknowledge it, let them finish, and redirect gently toward the Exit Shot.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. Honest answer. No judgment.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
Your doctor tells you: "Your stress levels are dangerous. If you don't make one real change in your life in the next 30 days, you will have serious health problems." What is the one thing you would change — and why haven't you changed it already?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Tell me about the most stressful week of your life. What happened? How did you feel? And what did you do about it?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: The doctor authority forces a real answer. The second question — "why haven't you changed it already?" — is the one that lands. Most students know exactly what they need to change. They just haven't. That gap between knowing and doing is the most honest conversation in health.

Sensitivity note: If a student has shared something genuinely difficult today, close with warmth. "You spoke honestly today — that takes courage. That is also, in a way, taking care of yourself."

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about how people try to sleep better — pills, remedies, routines. Very connected to stress and very personal."
