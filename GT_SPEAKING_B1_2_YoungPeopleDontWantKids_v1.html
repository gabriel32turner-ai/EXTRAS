B1.2 Social Issues & Society 🌶 Spicy 50 min
Why Young People Don't Want Kids
It's not laziness. It's not selfishness. It's a generation doing the math on money, time, climate, and a world that makes having children harder than ever.
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One number that many parents and grandparents don't want to believe.
⏱ 4 min
INEGI · Mexico National Survey · 2023
"In Mexico, the birth rate has dropped by 35% in the last 20 years. For the first time in history, more young Mexicans between 18 and 35 say they do not want children than say they do."
The trend is not unique to Mexico. South Korea, Japan, Italy, Spain, and the United States are all experiencing record-low birth rates. Researchers point to rising housing costs, job insecurity, career pressure, and climate anxiety as key factors — alongside a generation that simply sees parenthood as a choice, not an obligation.
Teacher Script Put the number up. Then ask: "Does this number surprise you — or does it make total sense?" Then: "Quick — how many of you want children, or already have them? And how many of you understand why someone wouldn't want them?" Don't ask people to justify — just get a quick read of the room before the conversation opens up.

Phase 3
The Opinion Wall
Four honest positions. Which one do you understand — even if you disagree?
⏱ 10 min
🙅
Sofía, 29
Marketing Coordinator · CDMX
"I don't want kids. I've known this since I was 22. I'm not broken, I'm not selfish, and I'm not going to change my mind. I have a full life. I love my nephews. I sleep eight hours. I travel. And I don't owe anyone an explanation for my uterus."
😊
Ricardo, 36
Father of 2 · Accountant · Celaya
"Having kids was the best decision of my life. Yes, it's expensive. Yes, I'm tired. But nothing prepared me for how much I would love them. I understand people who don't want children. I just think some of them are afraid — and fear isn't a reason to miss this."
😟
Gabriela, 33
Nurse · Guadalajara
"I wanted children. I can't have them. So when I hear people say 'I don't want kids' like it's so easy to decide — I feel a lot of things. I respect their choice. I really do. But I wish it was that simple for everyone."
👴
Don Ernesto, 68
Retired · Father of 4 · Querétaro
⚡ Provocateur
"My generation didn't have money either. We didn't have job security. We had children anyway — and it made us stronger. This generation talks about anxiety and lifestyle. We worked. I think young people today are looking for the perfect moment. It never comes. You just decide."
Discussion prompt: "Which of these four people makes you feel something strong — agreement, frustration, or sadness? Which one says something nobody usually says out loud?" Don Ernesto is the provocateur — his "we had children anyway" argument is what many grandparents genuinely think, and many young people silently fight against. Gabriela is the emotional wildcard — her presence reframes the debate as one that not everyone has equal access to.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real opinions only — not what your family wants to hear.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever felt pressure from your family to have children — or to have more children? What did that feel like?
Teacher Tip In Mexican families, this pressure is often intense and starts early. Most students 30+ have a story. Push for the specific moment — not "yes, my mom asks" but the actual conversation. "What did she say? What did you say back?" That's where the real language lives.
Q2
Have you ever heard someone judged for not wanting children — or for having too many? What was the reaction around them?
Teacher Tip Both directions are interesting — judgment against people who don't want kids, and judgment against people who have many. Mexico has both conversations happening at the same time. Let the contrast surface naturally.
Q3
Have you ever worried about money when thinking about your future — your house, your retirement, your health? How does that affect big decisions?
Teacher Tip This connects the "kids" conversation to the broader economic reality for this generation. Use vocabulary: rent, debt, savings, afford. The point is that the decision to not have children is often economic — not just personal preference. Let them make that connection themselves.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
In your opinion, is choosing not to have children selfish — or is it just a personal decision that doesn't affect anyone else?
Teacher Tip The "selfish" accusation is very common. Push them to define selfish: "Can a decision be selfish if it doesn't hurt anyone?" The interesting follow-up: "Is having children selfish too? Are you doing it for them — or for yourself?" That reversal often surprises students.
Q5
What do you think about Don Ernesto's idea that "your generation looks for the perfect moment"? Is he right — or does he not understand the situation young people are in today?
Teacher Tip The inter-generational tension question. Don Ernesto's argument is common — but it ignores real differences: housing costs, job insecurity, longer working hours. Push students to name specific things that are different today versus 30 years ago. Concrete comparisons are better than general "things are harder now."
Q6
Do you think society should do more to help people who want children — better salaries, childcare, parental leave? Or is it a personal decision and the government shouldn't be involved?
Teacher Tip Policy vs. personal choice. Mexico has limited parental leave and expensive childcare. Most students will say "yes, the government should help" — push for specifics: what would help the most? Salary, childcare, housing? Makes the vocabulary concrete: savings, afford, debt, sacrifice.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your partner wanted children but you didn't — or the other way around? Is that a deal-breaker for you?
Teacher Tip Second conditional — and very personal. This question works for all relationship statuses. Push: "Is there a compromise? Or is this one of those things where there's no middle ground?" Most students realize there isn't — you can't have half a child.
Q8
What would change in your life — in your daily routine, your finances, your plans — if you had a child right now?
Teacher Tip Second conditional — and very concrete. Push for specific numbers and details: "How much do you spend on rent? Could you afford childcare on top of that? What plans would you give up?" The specificity is the point — abstract "my life would change" is not as useful as naming the actual changes.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Do you think people who have children they can't afford are making a responsible decision — or are they being irresponsible with a child's life?
Teacher Tip Spicy — and deliberate. This question inverts the "selfish for not having children" argument. The point isn't to judge parents who struggle financially — it's to examine the double standard: why is "no kids" selfish but "kids you can't support" not irresponsible? Let the tension sit.
Q10 · Self-Reflection
If you have children — or want them — are you completely sure it's what you want? Or is part of it what you think you should want?
Teacher Tip The closing self-reflection — and the most honest question of the session. Many people who have children have never fully separated "I want this" from "I'm supposed to want this." Give students time. The silence before they answer is valuable. Don't rush.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. Record your answer in English.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · B1.2 Why Young People Don't Want Kids
Your 27-year-old nephew — or younger sibling — tells the family at Sunday dinner that he and his partner have decided not to have children. Ever. Your grandmother starts to cry. Your parents get angry. What do you say to him — and what do you say to your family?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. Do you defend him — or do you stay quiet to keep the peace? What does your answer say about what you actually believe?
Retention Hook The Sunday dinner setting is universally recognizable in Mexican culture — it's where these conversations actually happen. The two audiences (nephew vs. family) force the student to take two positions at the same time, which is linguistically rich and personally revealing. End: "Send me your voice note. I want to hear both conversations — not just one."
