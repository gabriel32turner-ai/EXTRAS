Rude People:
What Do You Do?

Someone is rude to you — in a line, at work, on the street. Do you say something? Or do you stay quiet?

🪝
The Trigger
Open the Conversation
⏱ 4 min
A question for the class
"Think about the last time someone was rude to you. What did you do? Did you say something — or did you stay quiet? How did you feel after?"
🎙 Teacher Script

Ask: "Last week — did someone do something rude to you? In a line, at the supermarket, on the road, at work?" Take 2-3 quick stories. Ask just: "What happened?" and "What did you do?" Don't analyze — just collect. The energy builds fast with this topic because it's personal and recent.

The Conversation Engine
23 Minutes of Real Talk
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
When was the last time someone was rude to you? What did they do?
Every student has a story. Let them tell it freely in past simple. Ask: "Where were you? What did they do? What did you do?" Sequence the story naturally — don't rush past the detail. The story IS the speaking practice.
Q2
Did you say something to a rude person — or did you stay quiet? What happened after?
Model answer: "I stayed quiet. I was angry but I didn't say anything. But I felt bad after — like I let them win. I wanted to go back and say something." The regret of staying quiet is very relatable.
Q3
Did you ever see someone be rude to another person? Did you do anything?
The bystander question. Most people do nothing — and know it. Model answer: "I saw a man yell at an old woman on the bus. I wanted to say something but I didn't. Nobody said anything. I still think about it." That honesty is valuable.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Is it better to say something to a rude person — or ignore them? What do you usually do?
Adriana vs. Héctor — the core debate. No right answer. Let students defend their choice. Push: "OK but if you always ignore, do rude people learn anything?" Gentle challenge in simple present.
Q5
Do you think people are more rude today than before — or is it the same?
Generational question — always generates opinions. Common answers: phones, stress, less community. Simple present: "I think people are more rude because..." Older students often say yes; younger sometimes say no. Let both sides talk.
Q6
Is rude behavior at work different from rude behavior on the street? Which is worse?
Patricia's distinction in question form. Work rudeness is different because you see that person every day and can't just walk away. Students usually feel more strongly about workplace rudeness. Explore that gap.
Block 3 — Future Plans
Q7
The next time someone is rude to you in a line or at the supermarket, what are you going to do?
Direct "going to" practice. Encourage specific and honest answers: "I'm going to..." Options: say something politely, ignore, speak to a manager. Compare reactions around the room — fun and natural contrast.
Q8
Are you going to be more patient with people this week — or do you think nothing is going to change?
Honest self-reflection with "going to." The second option is funny and real — most students laugh and say "nothing is going to change." That's fine! Model answer: "I'm going to try. But if someone cuts in front of me in traffic... I can't promise anything."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — do YOU sometimes do things that are a little rude? What do you do?
Ernesto's question. Normalize it — everyone has done something small. Model answer: "OK... sometimes I look at my phone when someone is talking to me. I know it's rude. I do it without thinking." The honest admission creates trust and good conversation energy.
Q10
A person cuts in front of you in a long line. You waited 20 minutes. The person looks at you and says nothing. What do you do right now?
The vivid scenario closer — very specific, 20 minutes of waiting makes it personal. Let students be dramatic and honest. Ask: "And if they say 'sorry, I was here before' — what do you do then?" The follow-up is always the most interesting part.

The Exit Shot
One Situation. Your Decision.
⏱ 5 min
🎯 Exit Shot
A coworker is rude to you in a meeting — in front of your boss and five other people. He interrupted you and said your idea was bad. Your boss said nothing. After the meeting, you are alone in the hall with your coworker. What are you going to say to him?

Voice Note Homework
