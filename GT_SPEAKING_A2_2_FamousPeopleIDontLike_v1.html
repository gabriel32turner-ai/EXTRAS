Famous People I Don't Like
Every famous person has fans — and people who can't stand them. Today we talk about the second group. Who do you not like, and why?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One simple question to start. No wrong answers.
⏱ 4 min
YouGov · Celebrity Popularity Study · 2023
"Almost every famous person has a significant number of people who actively dislike them — even celebrities with millions of fans. The most talked-about people are often also the most disliked."
Fame means that many people know your name — but it also means that many people have an opinion about you. Some famous people are very popular with some groups and very unpopular with others. This is normal — and it makes for a very interesting conversation.
Teacher Script Ask directly and with energy: "Quick — think of one famous person you don't like. Just one. You don't have to say why yet. Who is it?" Go around the room fast. Don't comment on choices — just collect names. Then pick one name someone said and ask: "Why don't you like them? Tell me in English." That's your opening.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Short, fun, honest answers. Use past simple and simple present.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
Did you use to like a famous person — and then stop? What happened?
Teacher Tip "I used to like _____ but then _____ happened." A simple used-to structure with a clear narrative. Many students have a story about a singer, actor, or athlete who did something that changed their opinion. Keep it light — this doesn't need to be a big event.
Q2
Did you ever argue with someone because they liked a famous person you don't like?
Teacher Tip Past simple — "Yes, I argued with my sister about ______." Or: "No, but my friend loves ______ and I don't understand why." This is a fun conversation about different tastes. Encourage a specific name if they're comfortable.
Q3
Did you ever watch a movie or listen to music and think: "Why is this person famous?"
Teacher Tip Past simple observation. The goal is simple descriptions: "I watched the movie and I didn't like it." "I don't think she has a good voice." Keep it personal and not mean — this is about taste, not insults.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is it okay to say publicly that you don't like a famous person? Or is it better to say nothing?
Teacher Tip Simple opinion: "I think it is okay because..." or "I think it is better to say nothing because..." No complex reasoning needed. Target: freedom of opinion vs. being kind. Accept short answers.
Q5
What makes you stop liking a famous person? Is it their actions, their personality, or something else?
Teacher Tip Descriptive opinion. Target words: actions, behavior, attitude, scandal. "For me, it's the attitude." "I don't like when celebrities lie." Keep it simple and personal. No need for long explanations.
Q6
Do you think Roberto is right — is it strange to have strong feelings about someone you never met?
Teacher Tip Simple agree/disagree with Roberto's opinion wall comment. "I agree because..." or "I disagree because..." This is the soft provocateur question — it makes them reflect briefly without being heavy. Short answers are perfect here.
Block 3 · Future Plans (Going to / Will)
Q7
Is there a movie, show, or concert you are not going to see because of the famous person in it?
Teacher Tip Going to for decisions. "I'm not going to watch _______ because _______." This connects personal taste to future behavior in a simple way. If they say "I don't know," ask: "Is there anyone you would not go see in concert?" That always gets an answer.
Q8
Do you think your opinion about this famous person will change in the future? Why or why not?
Teacher Tip Will for predictions about personal feelings. "I don't think it will change because..." "Maybe it will change if they do something different." Simple will + reason. Keep it personal and light.
Block 4 · Past Habits + Reflection (Used to)
Q9
Did you use to like a singer or actor when you were young that you think is silly now?
Teacher Tip Used to + past vs. present contrast. "I used to love _____ when I was 15. Now I think..." This is a fun, non-judgmental reflection. Everyone has a slightly embarrassing celebrity crush or phase from their youth. Keep the mood light and funny.
Q10 · Self-Reflection
Is there a famous person that many people dislike — but you actually like? Tell me about them.
Teacher Tip The reversal question — instead of dislike, defend someone. This creates a fun contrast with the whole session. Simple present: "I like _____ because _____." "I think he is talented even if many people don't agree." Encourages them to give a reason and use adjectives from the vocab section.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. Record your answer in English.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · A2.2 Famous People I Don't Like
Imagine you are going to have dinner with one famous person you don't like. You have to sit with them for two hours. Who is it — and what are you going to talk about?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. Tell me: who is it, why don't you like them, and what are you going to say at dinner?
Retention Hook The dinner scenario is light and imaginative — perfect for A2.2. It forces the student to use "going to" for plans and simple present to describe the person. The "what are you going to say?" part creates a fun, memorable voice note because it's both creative and personal. End: "Send me the voice note. I want to know who you picked — and if you're polite or not."
