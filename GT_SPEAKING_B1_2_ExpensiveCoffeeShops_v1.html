Expensive Coffee Shops
vs. Local Places

Starbucks, specialty cafés, street food, neighborhood markets — what you choose says something about you.

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
Forbes · Consumer Trends 2024
"Starbucks now charges an average of $7 for a specialty drink in the US — yet its lines remain long. Analysts say customers are not paying for coffee. They are paying for identity."
Teacher script: Read the stat. Then ask: "Do you go to Starbucks or similar places? What do you order? And be honest — why do you go there when there are cheaper options two streets away?" The identity angle hooks the conversation immediately.

The Opinion Wall
Phase 3 · Who do you agree with?
10 min
👩
Andrea, 31 — Designer, Guadalajara
"When I travel, I always try to find local places — small restaurants, market stalls, neighborhood bakeries. The best meal I've had in my life was 35 pesos at a market in Oaxaca. Chain restaurants and international coffee shops are just a safety net for people who are afraid of the real place."
👨
Carlos, 38 — Accountant, Monterrey
provocateur
"I've been judged for going to Starbucks when I travel and I'm tired of it. I work hard, I know what I like, and I'm not going to eat something that makes me sick just to seem more 'authentic.' Enjoying familiar things doesn't mean I don't respect the culture. It means I'm on vacation, not on a research project."
👩
Lucía, 44 — Business Owner, CDMX
"I've been living in CDMX for 20 years and I've watched local taquerías and mercados close because specialty coffee shops and brunch restaurants took their customers. Every time someone chooses a trendy place over a local one, a neighborhood changes. It sounds dramatic, but it's what's happening."
👨
Tomás, 29 — IT Analyst, Puebla
"I use coffee shops as my second office. I need fast wifi, power outlets, a comfortable chair, and an environment where nobody asks me to leave after 30 minutes. I'm not paying for the coffee — I'm paying for the workspace. Local places usually can't offer that, and that's just a fact."
💬 Choose one opinion. Which one describes your real behaviour when you travel — even if you're a little embarrassed to admit it? Tell your partner.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever found an amazing local place while travelling — a restaurant, market, or café that you still think about?
Teacher tip: Get students to be specific: "Where was it? What did you eat? How much did it cost? Did you go back?" The specificity produces great language and creates connection in the room.
Q2
Have you ever gone to a Starbucks, McDonald's, or similar chain when travelling abroad? Were you a little embarrassed?
Model answer: "Yes, in Tokyo. I was jet-lagged, I didn't speak Japanese, and I just needed something familiar and fast. I felt a little guilty — but honestly it was exactly what I needed in that moment."
Q3
Have you ever paid too much for food or a drink in a tourist area and regretted it immediately?
Teacher tip: The Rome near the Colosseum trap. The beach bar drink. The airport sandwich. Everyone has one. Let students compete for the most overpriced item.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think people who always go to chain restaurants when they travel are missing the point of travel?
Teacher tip: Reference Carlos's opinion. Some students will be defensive — they recognize themselves. Let the conversation develop without judgment. Push: "What is the point of travel, exactly?"
Q5
In your opinion, is there a difference between a tourist who eats at McDonald's in Paris and one who goes to a high-end French restaurant? Or are they the same thing?
Model answer: "I think they're both avoiding the real local experience, just at different price points. The authentic thing is to eat where the people who actually live there eat."
Q6
What do you think about gentrification — when expensive coffee shops and restaurants replace local businesses in a neighbourhood? Is it progress or a loss?
Teacher tip: Reference Lucía's opinion. This question has a strong local angle for CDMX, Guadalajara, and Monterrey students. Push: "Has this happened in your city? In your neighbourhood?"
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you were visiting a new city and a local person invited you to eat somewhere that looked completely unhygienic to you?
Model answer: "Honestly, it depends. If it's someone I trust and they eat there regularly, I would try it. If it's a stranger recommending a random place, I might make an excuse and say I already ate."
Q8
What would you choose: a 4-day trip to an expensive city where you can afford only tourist traps, or 10 days in a cheaper destination where you can eat and live like a local?
Teacher tip: Push students to commit to a choice and justify it. The "it depends" answer is not allowed here. Follow up: "Which city are you imagining? Why?"
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Have you ever judged another traveller for their food choices — or felt judged yourself for yours?
Teacher tip: This surfaces the social pressure around "authentic travel." Some students have judged. Some have been judged. Both sides create honest conversation about food snobbery and cultural performance.
Q10
Be honest: when you travel, do you actually want an authentic local experience — or do you want to be comfortable and just say you had an authentic experience?
Teacher tip: The most honest question of the session. Give 15 seconds of silence. The best answer is usually a mixture — and students who admit that produce the richest conversation of the lab.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
You are travelling alone in a foreign city for 5 days. You have a budget of $50 per day for food. A Starbucks grande latte costs $7. A full local breakfast at the market costs $3. You could eat 16 local meals for the price of 7 Starbucks drinks. At the end of the trip, which version of you tells a better story — and which one actually enjoyed it more?
