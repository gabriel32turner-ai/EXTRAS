The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocation & Opening
⏱ 4 min
Gallup Health Survey · 2023
"In Mexico, the average adult waits 2.3 years between doctor visits — and 61% say they only go when the pain is so bad they have no other choice."
Teacher script: Read it. Then ask simply: "When was the last time you went to the doctor? And why did you go — because you planned to, or because something was already bad?" Wait. Then: "Is there something you know you should get checked but haven't done yet?" This second question always lands.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever waited too long to go to the doctor — and later wished you had gone sooner? What happened?
Teacher Tip Almost everyone has a story. Push for details: "How long did you wait? What were you hoping would happen? Was there a moment when you knew you had to go — what was it?"
Q2
Have you ever Googled your symptoms and scared yourself? What did you search — and what did you find?
Model Answer "Yes. I had a headache for three days and Googled it. In 10 minutes I was convinced I had a brain tumor. I was very stressed for a week. Then the headache went away. It was stress. From Googling my headache."
Q3
Have you ever had a bad experience at a doctor or hospital in Mexico? What happened?
Teacher Tip IMSS stories are very common and very vivid. "How long did you wait? Was the doctor helpful? Did you go back? Or did that experience stop you from going to the doctor again for a while?" Real stories generate real language.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is it OK to take medicine from the pharmacy without seeing a doctor first — or is that always a bad idea?
Teacher Tip Very Mexican topic. "Have you ever bought antibiotics from a pharmacy without a prescription? What was the situation? Do you think that's dangerous or just practical?" No judgment — push for honest answers.
Q5
Do you think men avoid the doctor more than women in Mexico? Why?
Model Answer "Yes — definitely. My dad, my uncle, my brother — all the same. They say they're fine when they're clearly not. I think it's a machismo thing. Going to the doctor feels like admitting weakness. It's a very old and very dangerous idea."
Q6
Is it the government's job to make healthcare easy and affordable — or is it each person's own responsibility?
Teacher Tip Great for opinion vocabulary at B1.1. Keep it simple: "If someone is poor and can't afford a doctor — is that their fault? What should the government do?" Push for a clear position without academic vocabulary.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if a family member refused to go to the doctor even when they were clearly very sick?
Model Answer "I would try to talk first. Then I would ask someone else in the family to help. Then I would make the appointment for them and say 'I'm coming with you.' Sometimes people need someone to make the decision with them — not for them, but with them."
Q8
What would you do if a doctor told you something serious — something you were not expecting? How do you think you would react?
Teacher Tip A question about self-knowledge and fear. "Would you tell your family immediately? Or wait until you knew more? Would you cry? Get angry? Go quiet?" Give real time — these answers reveal a lot.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Is there something you know you should go to the doctor for — right now — that you are still avoiding? What is your real reason for not going?
Teacher Tip The most personal question of the session. Give real space. Common answers: fear of the result, no time, no money, "I'll go next month." Don't rush. The discomfort of the silence is valuable.
Q10
If you think about your health honestly — not how you feel today, but the general picture — are you taking care of yourself the way you should be?
Model Answer "No. And I know it. I sleep badly, I eat fast food too often, I haven't had a blood test in 3 years. I always say 'I'll start being more careful next month.' Next month has been coming for a long time."

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · Final Dilemma · No right answer
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot
Your doctor calls you. Your last blood test showed something that needs to be checked further. It might be nothing. It might be something serious. The appointment to find out is next week. For 7 days, you know something could be wrong but you don't know what. How do you live that week — and do you tell your family while you wait?
📱 WhatsApp Homework Record 60 seconds in English: How do you handle that week? Do you tell your family — yes or no?
