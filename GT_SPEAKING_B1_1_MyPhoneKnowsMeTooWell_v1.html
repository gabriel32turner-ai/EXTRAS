My Phone Knows Me Too Well
You open an app — and it already knows what you want. You talk about something — and the ad appears. Is your phone listening to you, or does it just know you better than you think?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One creepy fact. See who nods first.
⏱ 4 min
The New York Times · 2023
"Your phone app can predict what you want to buy, who you will vote for, and whether your relationship is in trouble — often before you know it yourself."
Modern apps collect hundreds of data points per user per day: where you go, how long you look at something, what you buy, who you call, and what you type. Researchers at Cambridge University showed they could predict personality, politics, and personal problems with over 85% accuracy — using only Facebook likes.
Teacher Script Read the quote slowly. Then ask: "Has this ever happened to you? You talk about something — and then you see an ad for it?" Let 2–3 students share quickly. Then: "Check your phone right now. Look at your last 5 Instagram or TikTok ads. How many are for something you actually want?" That exercise opens the conversation immediately — no warm-up needed.

😏
Fernando, 40
IT Manager · Querétaro
⚡ Provocateur
"Your phone isn't listening to you. It doesn't need to. It knows your age, your neighborhood, your job, your shopping history, and 200 other things. It can guess what you want without hearing a single word. You are more predictable than you think — and that should scare you more than a microphone."
Discussion prompt: "Which of these four people sounds most like you right now? Which one surprised you the most?" Fernando is the provocateur — his argument shifts the fear from "my phone is listening" to "you are predictable." That's more unsettling, not less. Most B1.1 students haven't thought about it this way.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real stories. No perfect answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever seen an ad for something you only talked about — not searched? What was it?
Teacher Tip Almost everyone has a story. Let them share it without interrupting. Then ask: "Did you click on the ad?" Push for the present perfect structure in their answer: "I have seen an ad for..." or "It has happened to me before..."
Q2
Have you ever checked how many hours you spend on your phone each day? What was the number — and how did you feel?
Teacher Tip If they haven't checked, have them do it right now — Settings → Screen Time. The number is always higher than people expect. The feeling after is the conversation: surprise, guilt, "that makes sense," etc. No judgment from you.
Q3
Have you ever tried to spend less time on your phone? Did it work?
Teacher Tip Present perfect for the attempt + past simple for the story of what happened. Push: "What made it hard? What happened when you were bored?" The moment they reach for their phone when they're not even thinking about it is the key detail.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Do you think it's okay for apps to collect your information if it makes them more useful to you?
Teacher Tip The basic trade-off question: convenience vs. privacy. Most B1.1 students will say "I don't mind" or "it depends." Push them to say what it depends on — what type of information? Who sees it? What do they use it for?
Q5
Is it creepy when your phone recommends something very specific — or is it just useful? Where's the line for you?
Teacher Tip "Creepy" is a great B1.1 word — they probably know it and can use it. Push them to name a specific example of when a recommendation crossed from useful to creepy. The line is different for everyone — that difference is the conversation.
Q6
Do you believe "I have nothing to hide" is a good reason not to worry about privacy?
Teacher Tip This is the most important opinion question. A simple counter: "You have nothing to hide — but do you want your boss to see your messages? Your doctor? The government?" Privacy isn't about hiding bad things — it's about controlling who sees what. See if they can make that argument themselves.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you found out one specific app was recording your conversations without permission?
Teacher Tip Second conditional: "What would you do if you found out..." Push for a specific action: "I would delete it," "I would tell my friends," "I would report it." Then ask: "Would you actually do that — or would you just feel angry for a day and keep using it?" The honest answer is usually the second option.
Q8
What would your life look like if you deleted all your social media apps for one month?
Teacher Tip Second conditional — and very personal. Push for concrete details: "What would you do with those extra 3 hours a day? Would you feel better or worse? Would you feel lonely?" The specificity reveals how much of daily life is now organized around these apps.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Be honest: when you wake up in the morning, what's the first thing you do? And are you happy about that?
Teacher Tip For most people, the answer is "check my phone." The follow-up is what matters: "Are you happy about that?" Most people aren't — but they do it anyway. That gap between what we want to do and what we actually do is the heart of this conversation.
Q10 · Self-Reflection
Does your phone know you better than your best friend does? Think about it seriously before you answer.
Teacher Tip The closing question — and the one that lands hardest. Your phone knows your location, your schedule, what you buy, what you watch, what you search at 2am. Your best friend probably doesn't. Give them time to sit with that. Don't rush to the next thing.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. Record your answer in English.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · B1.1 My Phone Knows Me Too Well
You discover that your phone has been recording your location 24 hours a day for the last 3 years. There is a map of everywhere you have been — your home, your work, your doctor, your friends' houses. The company says it's normal and legal. What do you do?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. Are you angry? Are you surprised? And be honest: does it actually change what apps you use?
Retention Hook This is already happening — Google and Apple have had exactly this feature for years. The "it's normal and legal" line is the twist that creates real discomfort: there's no one to blame, no crime, no solution. The student has to sit with that. End: "Send me your voice note. And check your location history on Google Maps tonight — it's in Settings. Tell me what you find."
