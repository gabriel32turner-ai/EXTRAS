§ 01 · The Trigger
🪝 The Data That Started a Culture War

4 min
💤

1 in 3

$411B

Sources: McKinsey Health Institute (2024) · Matthew Walker, "Why We Sleep" (2017, updated data 2023) · Arianna Huffington, "The Sleep Revolution" · RAND Corporation sleep economics report
🪝 Opening Trigger — Read this, then point at someone
"Arianna Huffington collapsed from exhaustion, hit her head on her desk, and woke up in a pool of blood. She ran one of the world's most successful companies. She was sleeping 4 hours a night and thought she was winning. Was she?"
Teacher Mechanics — 4 min Read the Huffington story once, slowly. Then point directly at one student: "First reaction — was she winning or losing?" Do not validate. Do not explain. Wait 5 seconds if silence. The tension is the activation. After 2–3 responses, say: "Hold everything you just said — it all comes back in §4."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The Huffington story is deliberately personal and physical — "pool of blood" creates visceral impact. Let it land without softening it.
  • If students immediately say "of course she wasn't winning" — push back: "But her company was worth $350 million. She was making decisions that worked. Explain that."
  • Don't let anyone give a one-word answer here. Minimum: one sentence with a reason. "Because…" is your redirect tool.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Most Mexican professionals in manufacturing/corporate settings normalize 5–6 hours of sleep as "just how it is." This stat challenges that directly — expect defensiveness. That's the goal.
  • If someone says "I only sleep 5 hours and I'm fine" — smile and say: "That's exactly what we're going to talk about." Don't argue. Save it for §4 Q4.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • The $411B stat is shareable on WhatsApp. After the session: "Send one of those stats to a colleague today — in English. That's real-world B2 output."
  • Retention hook: "In 50 minutes, you're going to have a position on something that's quietly reshaping how companies think about performance. That's the conversation."

Challenge Mode Available

💡 10 Nouns — Sleep, Performance & Corporate Reality
🔵 10 Verbs — What People Do About Sleep (or Don't)
⚡ Production Challenge — No preparation
Each person picks 3 words. One sentence each, about your real sleep habits right now. Be honest. No writing. No thinking time. Just English.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Pairs mechanic: Person A covers the word, reads the definition aloud, Person B says the word from memory. Then switch roles. This is retrieval practice — far more effective than passive reading.
  • If a student gets stuck on "burnout" vs "fatigue" — clarify: burnout is chronic and psychological; fatigue is physical and can be fixed with one good night. Both are in today's vocab for that reason.
  • Production challenge tip: If someone says "I sleep well" — push: "Use 'recharge' and 'peak performance' in the same sentence about a real situation at work."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • These words show up in real corporate wellness conversations: HR wellness programs, executive coaching, productivity reports. Say it: "These are the words your HR department in Chicago or New York is using right now."
  • Best production challenge trigger: "Which 3 words describe your Monday morning at work? Go."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Homework trigger (optional): "Before next session — use one of these words in a real conversation at work. In English. It can be in writing. Bring the screenshot."
  • "These 20 words connect directly to performance reviews, wellness initiatives, and leadership conversations. You will hear them in meetings with American or European counterparts."
§ 03 · Opinion Wall
🧱 4 Real Voices. 4 Different Realities.
Read or project each card · Pairs discuss 90 sec · Group shares · Same processing question each time
03
10 min
Processing Question — Same for all 4 voices
"Who do you agree with most — and who makes you most uncomfortable? Why?"
Don't vary the question
🔁 Processing Question — Pairs → Group (ask after all 4)
"Who do you agree with most — and who makes you most uncomfortable? Why?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Read all 4 cards before opening discussion — don't stop after each one. The contrast between all 4 is more powerful than reacting to each individually.
  • The provocateur is Rodrigo. His opinion will be the most uncomfortable for anyone who normalizes overwork. Watch for students who dismiss him too quickly — push: "But what if he's right? What's your evidence?"
  • Don't let discussion become a health lecture. Redirect with: "This isn't a doctor's appointment. We're talking about what this means at work, for your career, for your team."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • If the group is shy: read each card in a slightly dramatic voice — the characters have personality. Play Rodrigo as slightly arrogant. Play Daniela as genuinely tired. The contrast is the spark.
  • Best follow-up: "Which one of these four people do you actually know at your company?"
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Testimonial trigger: "In one sentence — which of these four voices has described how you've been living the last 6 months?" The honest answer is usually a great testimonial in disguise.
  • Retention: "Next session we go deeper into wellbeing and leadership. Today was the personal. Next time it gets systemic."
§ 04 · The Conversation Engine
🗣 10 Questions. 4 Blocks. 23 Minutes.
Personal → Opinion → Hypothetical → Provocative · No round-robin · Point and push
04
23 min
⚡
Rule of the Engine: Minimum 2 sentences per answer. First sentence = position. Second sentence = why or example. If a student stops at one sentence, hold up two fingers. That's your redirect.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Block 3 (Hypothetical) is where B2 grammar naturally appears — second conditional and "would." Don't correct in the moment. Note the errors and recycle them at the end: "Three of you said 'If I would sleep more' — the correct form is 'If I slept more, I would.' Heard it?"
  • Q7 and Q8 (Provocative block) should feel uncomfortable. If the student tries to answer academically — push back: "I didn't ask what you think in theory. I asked what you would actually do."
  • Timing: 2–2.5 min per question. If a question generates organic debate, let it run 1 extra minute max. Then cut.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Best moment to celebrate: when a student uses a word from §2 naturally in their answer. Point it out immediately: "You just used 'recharge' in a real sentence. That's the goal."
  • Q9 (self-reflection) often produces the most honest answers. Don't rush it. Let the silence after the question work.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • After Q10: "You just had a conversation that most people can't have in their first language — because they've never been asked to think this carefully about it. You did it in English."
  • Upsell trigger: "Next Speaking Lab goes into a topic that connects directly to this one: stress and how high-performers manage it. Same format, same depth."
§ 05 · The Exit Shot
💣 One Question. No Easy Answer.
5 min · No tips · No chips · Just position + reason · Then the loop
05
5 min
EXIT
💣 Exit Shot
Your company offers you a 40% salary raise — but as part of a new "high performance culture," you're expected to be reachable by 10pm and work through at least one day every weekend. You have a partner and two kids, ages 6 and 9. You haven't slept more than 6 hours in two years. Do you take the deal?
Answer in at least 3 sentences. Start with your decision. Then explain the real reason — not the one that sounds best, the one that's true. No right answer. No wrong answer.
📌 B2.1 Model Answer — Reveal AFTER student speaks
"I wouldn't take it — and honestly, I've been in a version of that deal for the last year without the raise. What I've noticed is that the 40% doesn't fix anything when you're running on empty every day. My kids are at an age where they actually notice when I'm not present — not just physically, but mentally. If I took that deal, I'd be buying things with money I don't have time to spend. I'd rather negotiate a smaller raise with clear boundaries than accept a number that costs me more than it gives me."
🔁 Retention Loop — The WhatsApp Homework "Before our next session — record a 60-second voice note in English on WhatsApp. Your answer to today's Exit Shot. Don't write it first. Don't rehearse it. Just talk. That's B2 output. Send it to [teacher number]."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The question has real numbers (40%, age 6 and 9, two years) because vague dilemas produce vague answers. The specificity forces a real decision, not a theoretical one.
  • If a student says "it depends" — respond immediately: "You have 30 seconds. Make a decision. It depends is not a sentence."
  • After the student answers: ask one follow-up only — "What would have to change for your answer to be different?" This activates the second conditional naturally.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The WhatsApp homework works because it's asynchronous and private — students are more honest in a voice note alone than in the group. The loop also keeps them thinking in English between sessions.
  • If a student says they would take the deal: "Okay. What do you tell your 6-year-old on a Sunday when you're working?" Don't moralize — just make it concrete.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • The voice note homework is the single most powerful retention tool in this format. Students who send it come back the next session with something to talk about. Students who don't send it feel the gap — and that's also a retention driver.
  • Closing line: "You just spent 50 minutes thinking, arguing, and deciding — in English. Not translating. Thinking. That's the difference between B2.1 and everyone else."
  • Referral trigger: "If a colleague of yours is stuck in a basic English program doing grammar exercises, this is what they're missing. You can tell them that."
