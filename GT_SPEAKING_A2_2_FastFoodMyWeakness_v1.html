Fast Food:
My Weakness
You know it's not good for you. You eat it anyway. And it tastes amazing. Every single time.
A2.2Health & Wellness🟡 Medium50 min

🪝 Phase 1 — The Trigger
The Question
Read it. Then ask the student directly.
4 min
01
A question to start
"What is your number one fast food weakness — the one thing you always want when you are tired or hungry?"
— Tacos, pizza, burgers, chips, ramen... everyone has one. What is yours?
A simple fact
"In Mexico, people eat fast food an average of 3 times per week. Most people say they eat it because it is fast, cheap, and delicious — not because they don't know it's unhealthy."
— We know. We eat it anyway. And it's okay to talk about it.
Launch line: "I'm going to be honest — what was the last fast food you ate? Not yesterday's home-cooked meal. The last burger, taco, pizza, or chips." [Model yours first.] "Mine was... [yesterday/last week]. Today we talk about fast food — our favorite, our weakness, and our relationship with it. No judgment." At A2.2, food topics always generate immediate participation.

5 Colloquial Phrases — Real Food Talk
Simple phrases · Click to hear · Use today
Colloquial
"I'm so hungry."
The feeling that makes you order fast food instead of cooking
"Oh my God, this is so good!"
The reaction to a really delicious taco or burger — involuntary and universal
"Just one more."
Said before the fifth chip, the second taco, or the extra slice of pizza
"Forget about it!"
When the diet is impossible — "I tried to eat healthy but my coworkers ordered pizza. Forget about it!"
"Same here!"
When someone describes your own relationship with food — "I can't say no to tacos!" / "Same here!"
After vocab, ask: "Which verb describes your food life best — order, cook, or eat out?" Most students pick "order" and laugh. Then: "And which colloquial phrase is most true for you?" This bridges perfectly to the Opinion Wall. Keep the energy fun and non-judgmental — food is one of the warmest topics at A2.2.

🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Short questions. Honest and fun answers. Use simple sentences.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
What was the last fast food you ate? Describe it — what was it, where was it from, and was it good?
Past simple — recent food story. This always generates immediate, enthusiastic answers. Model: "Yesterday I ate tacos from the corner restaurant near my office. I ate three. They were amazing. Too many — but amazing." Simple sentences, descriptive past simple. Activate "Oh my God, this is so good!" naturally.
Q2
When was the last time you cooked at home instead of ordering food? What did you cook?
Past simple — contrasting experience. The pause before answering is funny and honest. Model: "Last Sunday. I cooked rice and chicken. It was okay. Not as good as tacos. But I cooked it." Or: "Two weeks ago. I'm not a good cook." Both answers are perfectly A2.2.
Q3
Did you ever eat too much fast food in one day and feel bad after? What happened?
Past simple — the "too much" story. Everyone has one. Model: "Yes! Last year at a birthday party. I ate pizza, then cake, then chips, then more pizza. At night I felt terrible. But the pizza was really good." Activate "Forget about it!" and "Just one more" naturally.
Block 2 — Opinion (Simple Present)
Q4
Do you think fast food is a big problem — or is it okay if you eat it in moderation?
Simple opinion — Patricia's "5 days healthy" approach vs. Sofía's "never." Most A2.2 students say "in moderation." Model: "I think it's okay. If you don't eat it every day, it's fine. Life is short. Enjoy your food." Or: "I think it's a problem. I eat it too much. I want to eat less." Both are valid and grammatically appropriate.
Q5
What do you think is better — cooking at home or ordering food? Why?
Simple comparison opinion. Target structure: "I think... is better because..." Model: "I think cooking at home is better. It's healthier and cheaper. But ordering is easier and I don't cook very well." Or: "I think ordering is better. I am very busy. I don't have time to cook." Both answers are honest and natural.
Q6
Is it important for children to eat healthy food? Or are kids okay to eat fast food sometimes?
Simple opinion about children and food. Very relatable for parents. Model: "I think it's important for children to eat healthy food. But sometimes fast food is okay. Life is not perfect." Or: "I have two kids. They love burgers. I try to cook, but sometimes we order pizza. It's fine." Natural and personal.
Block 3 — Future Plans (Going to / Will)
Q7
Are you going to change what you eat this year? What are you going to eat more of — or less of?
Going to — future food plan. The honest answer is often funny. Model: "I'm going to eat less fast food. I'm going to cook more at home. I'm going to start on Monday." Or: "I'm not going to change. I'm happy with my food. Maybe I'm going to eat a little more vegetables. Maybe." Both are excellent A2.2 production.
Q8
Will you eat your favorite fast food this week? When and where?
Will — near-future prediction, very personal. Model: "Yes, I will eat tacos on Friday. I always eat tacos on Friday. It's my tradition." Or: "Probably yes. I will order pizza on the weekend. I always do." This question produces the most natural "will" production at A2.2 because it's genuinely true for most students.
Block 4 — Past Habits + Reflection (Used to)
Q9
Did you use to eat different food when you were a child? What did you love to eat?
Used to — childhood food memories. Very warm and personal. Model: "When I was a child, I used to eat my grandmother's tamales every Sunday. They were the best food in the world. Now I eat them only at Christmas. I miss them." Or: "I used to eat a lot of fruit. Now I don't. I eat more chips." Nostalgic and fun.
Q10
Did you use to eat more fast food before — or do you eat more now than when you were younger?
Used to + comparison with present. Light closing question. Model: "I used to eat less fast food when I was young. My mother cooked every day. Now I live alone and I order food a lot. My mother was a better cook than me." Or: "I used to eat more! When I was a student I ate fast food every day. Now I'm older and I try to eat better." Both directions are interesting.
A2.2 grammar targets: Past simple (Q1-3), simple present opinions (Q4-6), going to / will (Q7-8), used to (Q9-10).

Energy note: This is the highest-energy topic in the A2.2 Health lineup. Food is universally engaging, nobody feels judged, and everyone has stories. Q8 often produces the most natural "will" of the session because the answer is simply and honestly true. Celebrate every complete sentence with warmth.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. Simple and delicious. Answer in 2-3 sentences.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
If you are going to eat your favorite food tonight — no rules, no diet, no guilt — what are you going to order or cook? Describe it. Make me hungry.
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Tell me about your relationship with food. What is your favorite food? What is your weakness — the food you can't resist? And are you going to eat healthy or delicious this week?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: "Going to order / going to cook" is the target grammar embedded in the most enjoyable possible context. "Make me hungry" is a playful instruction that encourages descriptive language without explicitly demanding it. Students leave class thinking about food — and usually text or tell someone what they decided to eat. The conversation continues after class.

Homework note: Three personal questions that activate simple present + going to in a completely non-academic, fun context. Very high completion rate expected — this homework feels like fun, not work.

Upsell moment: "Next session we're going to talk about something completely different — a health topic but much more serious. Or maybe something from your personal life. You choose."
