The Best and Worst
Boss I Had

Every boss is different. Some teach you everything. Some make you want to quit. Let's talk about yours.

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
Gallup · Work & Wellbeing
"People don't leave bad jobs. They leave bad bosses. 50% of workers quit a job at some point because of their boss — not the company."
Teacher script: Read the stat slowly. Then: "50%. Half. Think about all your bosses — every job you had. Who was the best? Who was the worst? Close your eyes for 5 seconds and think..." Then open to the group: "Who wants to tell me about the worst one first?"

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Past Experience
Q1
Describe your worst boss. What did they do? Give me 3 specific things.
Teacher tip: Push for specifics with simple past: "He yelled / She never listened / He was always late / She didn't explain things." Collect 3 behaviors. If student says "he was bad," ask: "What exactly did he do? Give me an example."
Q2
What did your best boss do that made them special? Give me 2 things.
Model answer: "My best boss was very calm. She never yelled. And she always explained why — not just what to do. When I made a mistake, she helped me fix it. She was patient. I learned a lot from her."
Q3
Did a boss ever say something to you that you still remember today — good or bad?
Teacher tip: Very rich question. Give thinking time. Common answers: a compliment that changed their confidence, a criticism that still hurts, or a piece of advice they still follow. Push: "Why do you still remember those words?"
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
What is more important in a boss — fair or friendly? Why?
Teacher tip: Force a choice. If student says "both," push: "But if you have to choose only one — which is more important for you at work every day?" Model: "I think fair is more important. A friendly boss is nice, but a fair boss gives you the same opportunities as everyone else. That's more important."
Q5
Do you think a boss can be your friend — or is that always complicated?
Model answer: "I think it's complicated. When the boss is your friend, it's difficult to say no. And other coworkers think you get special treatment. It's better to be friendly — but not real friends. There's a difference."
Q6
Do you think bad bosses know they are bad — or do they think they are good?
Teacher tip: Fun philosophical question at A2.1 level. Most will say "they think they are good." Push: "Why? What do they tell themselves?" Common answer: "They think they are strict, not bad."
Block 3 — Future Plans
Q7
Are you going to stay in your current job — or are you going to look for a new one this year?
Teacher tip: "Going to" practice with real personal stakes. Follow up: "Is your boss part of that decision? Does your boss make you want to stay — or leave?"
Q8
If you are a boss in the future, what are you going to do differently from your worst boss?
Model answer: "I'm going to listen to my team. I'm not going to yell. And I'm going to say 'good job' when someone does good work. My worst boss never said that. I'm going to be different."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — are YOU a difficult person to manage? Do you think your boss has problems with you sometimes?
Teacher tip: Give 10 seconds of silence. The honest students produce the best language here. Common answers: "I ask too many questions," "I disagree sometimes," "I am late to meetings." Don't judge — celebrate the honesty.
Q10
Your current boss leaves the company next month. A new boss arrives. Are you happy or scared? Why?
Teacher tip: Great closing scenario. Push for "going to" and "will": "What do you think the new boss is going to do? What are you going to do in the first week with them?"

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
Your worst boss calls you today. He wants to offer you a job — a very good salary, but you are going to work directly with him again. What are you going to say?
