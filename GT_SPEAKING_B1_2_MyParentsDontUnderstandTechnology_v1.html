My Parents Don't
Understand Technology
You love them. But explaining how to share a photo for the fifteenth time is testing your patience. Is it frustration, love, or something more complicated?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
A real headline. A real conversation.
⏱ 4 min
The Guardian · 2023
"Studies show that older adults who feel excluded from digital technology also report higher levels of loneliness and depression. The digital divide isn't just a tech problem — it's becoming a mental health crisis for millions of parents and grandparents who feel left behind."
Teacher Script
"Quick question: how many of you have spent more than 20 minutes helping a parent do something on their phone? Now — how did you feel during that? Patient? Frustrated? Guilty about being frustrated? Let's talk about this honestly today. It's more complicated than it looks."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real talk. No right answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever had to explain something on a phone or computer to your parents — or someone older in your family? What happened?
Get a specific story. "I showed my mom how to do a video call" is fine but "my mom called me crying because she couldn't turn on her phone and she thought it was broken — but it was on airplane mode" is the real conversation. Specific memories open vocabulary naturally.
Q2
Have you ever felt frustrated explaining technology to someone older — and then felt guilty about that frustration?
The double feeling is the honest answer. "Frustration + guilt" is the emotional vocabulary for this topic. Don't let them say "no, I'm always patient" without gently challenging it. Almost everyone has had this moment.
Q3
Have you ever had to stop your parents from clicking on something dangerous — a scam, a fake message, a suspicious link? What was it?
Digital safety + family emotion together. Almost everyone in Mexico has a story about a WhatsApp scam targeting a family member. Great real-world vocabulary: scam, fake, link, urgent, money transfer. If they haven't had this happen, ask: "Do you ever worry it will?"
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
In your opinion, whose responsibility is it to help older people understand technology — their children, the government, or the technology companies?
Push for a clear position with a reason. Model: "I think it's the technology companies — because they make billions of dollars and could make their products simpler if they wanted. They choose not to because confusion keeps people dependent." That's a strong B1.2 argument.
Q5
Do you think older people who don't want to learn new technology are being stubborn — or do they have a good reason to resist it?
Don Jorge's opinion lives here. Every generation has learned new technology in their lifetime. At what point does it become unreasonable to expect someone to adapt again? Great empathy builder. Ask: "What technology do YOU find annoying and avoid?"
Q6
Is it better to give an older person a simple phone with limited functions — or teach them to use a normal smartphone? What's more respectful?
Mateo's approach vs. Sofía's. The simple phone feels like giving up on the person — but it also stops the stress. Ask: "Would you want someone to give you a simple phone when you're 70? Or would you want them to have patience with you?" Personal stakes change the answer.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you found out your parents had shared personal information online — their address, their bank details — with a scammer? How would you handle that conversation?
Real scenario, real stakes. "I would be very calm and..." — push: "Really? Even if they lost money?" The emotional management of a crisis with a parent you love is a rich conversation. Great use of would + infinitive for hypothetical planning.
Q8
What would you do if your parents asked you to come every weekend to help them with their technology — but you had no free time and were already exhausted from work?
The guilt + limits question. What's the balance between being a good child and having your own life? Model answer: "I would try to teach them one thing per visit — really teach it, not just do it for them. That way I'm investing, not just fixing."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
When you explain technology to your parents, do you treat them with the same patience you would give a colleague at work — or less? Why?
Most people admit less patience with family. Why? Because with family the emotional stakes are higher, the expectations are different, and there's no professional filter. That's not wrong — but naming it is important. "I am less patient with the people I love most." That's real.
Q10
One day you will be the one asking your children for help — with technology, with a form, with something that seems obvious to them. How do you want them to treat you in that moment?
The mirror closer. Everything they say about how they want to be treated is exactly what their parents need right now. Let that connection land slowly. Don't rush it. The silence after this question is often the most powerful moment in the class.

💣 Exit Shot
Your mom calls you on a Saturday morning, crying. She received a WhatsApp message that said: "Mom, I'm in an accident, I need 3,500 pesos right now, don't call me, just send it." She almost sent the money. She didn't — because she decided to call you first. You have 5 minutes to explain what happened and what to do in the future. What do you say — and how do you say it without making her feel stupid?
