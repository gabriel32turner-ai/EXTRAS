Salary Transparency
Should everyone know what everyone earns? The conversation companies fear most.
🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocation & Opening
⏱ 4 min
Harvard Business Review · 2023
"Pay transparency laws are spreading fast — and companies that resist them are losing top talent to competitors who don't hide the numbers."
Teacher script: Read the headline aloud. Then ask: "Have you ever found out a colleague earns more than you? What happened?" Pause. Let the silence work. Then: "Today we're going to talk about whether salaries should be public information — at your company, in your industry, everywhere."

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever accidentally found out a colleague's salary — or told someone yours? What happened?
Teacher Tip Almost everyone has a story here. Push for details: "How did you find out? How did it make you feel about your job after that?"
Q2
Has your company ever given you a raise without explaining why? Or denied one without a clear reason?
Teacher Tip This often leads to stories of frustration. Useful vocab to push: "Did you push back? Did you negotiate? Did you feel underpaid?"
Q3
Do you know what your direct colleagues earn? Would you want to?
Model Answer "Honestly, no — and I'm not sure I'd want to. I think it would change how I feel about my work if I knew someone doing the same job made significantly more. But I also think I deserve to know if I'm being underpaid."
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Do you think salary transparency is fundamentally a fairness issue, or does it create more problems than it solves?
Teacher Tip Push them to take a side. "Which one more — fairness or problems? Give me a percentage." Forces commitment and richer language.
Q5
Should companies be legally required to publish salary ranges for every position — like they are in Colorado and the UK?
Model Answer "I think publishing ranges — not exact salaries — makes sense. That way candidates can negotiate from a real baseline, not from a position of total ignorance. But I'm not sure I want my boss knowing exactly what I make either."
Q6
Do you think salary secrecy benefits companies more than employees? Why do most companies prefer it?
Teacher Tip Great for pushing critical thinking. Target phrase: "It benefits them because it lets them exploit people who don't negotiate." See if they can make that argument even if they disagree with it.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If your company made all salaries public tomorrow, how do you think your team would react? Would you be okay with people knowing yours?
Model Answer "Honestly, I think there would be a lot of tension in the first week. People would compare and feel either guilty or resentful. But after a few months? Maybe it would actually make things more honest. I'd be nervous about mine being known, but I think that's exactly the point."
Q8
If you found out a colleague doing the same job earns 20% more than you, what would you do — and what do you wish you had the confidence to do?
Teacher Tip Two-part question — what they'd do vs. what they wish they'd do. The gap between those answers is where the real conversation lives.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Have you ever stayed quiet about a salary injustice at work because you were afraid of the consequences? What did that cost you?
Teacher Tip This is the vulnerability question. Give them space. Don't rush. If they open up, the language they use will be their most authentic of the session.
Q10
If you're being honest — do you think you're paid what you're worth? What would it take for you to actually believe that?
Model Answer "That's hard to answer. I think I've been paid fairly — but I've also never fully pushed for what I'm worth. I've accepted what they offered because I didn't want to seem difficult. Which probably means I've been underpaid and accepted it."

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · Final Dilemma · No right answer
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot
Your company posts all salaries internally. You discover you earn 25% less than a colleague you trained two years ago. HR says it's because they "negotiated better" when they joined. You have two options: negotiate hard and risk being labeled "difficult," or say nothing and keep the peace — but know the number every day. What do you do?
