§ 01 · The Trigger
🪝 The Numbers Behind Family Businesses
Read aloud · First reaction only · No debate yet · 4 min max
01
4 min
👨‍👩‍👧
Harvard Business Review + Family Business Institute 2023
70%
Of family businesses fail before the second generation takes over — most because of family conflicts, not money problems
60%
Of workers say working with a family member has caused at least one serious argument — at work or at home
1 in 3
People who work in a family business say they feel they cannot say "no" to a family member at work
40%
Of people who have worked with family say they would not do it again — even if the business was successful
Sources: Harvard Business Review · Family Business Institute 2023 · Gallup Workplace Survey
🪝 Opening Question — Read this, then point at someone
"Your brother is your boss. He makes a bad decision at work. At dinner that night, your mom asks: 'How is everything going?' What do you say?"
Teacher Mechanics — 4 min Read the question once, slowly. Point at one student: "First reaction — what do you say to your mom?" No judgment. After 2–3 reactions, say: "We come back to all of this in §4."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • In Mexico, family business is extremely common — many students have a family member who is also a colleague or boss. This is not abstract; it is their Monday morning.
  • The 70% failure stat reframes the topic: this isn't just a personal preference issue, it's a real business risk. Use it if the group needs a factual hook.
  • If nobody reacts to the trigger: ask directly — "Has anyone here ever worked with a parent, sibling, or cousin? Raise your hand."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The dinner scenario is the hook — it makes the invisible tension visible. Work problems come home. Home emotions come to work. That's what this session is about.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Retention: "Next session we'll look at what happens when you hate your job but can't leave. Today is the warm-up — family at work. Next time: you at work."

⚡ Production Challenge — No preparation
Pick 3 words. One sentence each about a real situation with a family member — at work or at home. No writing. Just talk.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • "Argue" vs "fight" — clarify: argue = disagree in words; fight can be bigger, more emotional, sometimes physical. Both are common but at B1.1 students often confuse them.
  • Best production challenge trigger: "Which 3 words describe a real situation with a family member — at work or at home? Choose the most honest ones."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "These are real words for real situations. You'll use them the next time you need to talk about your family — in English."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "These words will come back in the next Speaking Lab too — when we talk about hating your job. You'll see the connection."

"Who do you agree with most — and who do you think is wrong? Why?"

🔁 Processing Question — Pairs → Group
"Who do you agree with most — and who do you think is wrong? Why?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Read all 4 before opening. The provocateur is Don Memo — his "family first, always" position will immediately clash with anyone who has had a bad experience working with family. That friction is the session engine.
  • Don't let students stay vague. Push: "Give me one specific reason — not just 'I agree' or 'I disagree.'"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • If the group is quiet, read Don Memo with a slightly proud voice. Read Valentina with tiredness. The contrast will spark a reaction.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Testimonial trigger: "One sentence — which of these four people sounds most like someone in your family?"
§ 04 · The Conversation Engine
🗣 10 Questions. 4 Blocks. 23 Minutes.
Personal → Opinion → Hypothetical → Provocative · Point and push · No round-robin
04
23 min
⚡
Rule: At least 2 sentences per answer. First: your position. Second: why — or a short story from your life.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • At B1.1, the present perfect is newly introduced. If students answer Q1–Q3 in simple past only, gently model: "So you HAVE worked with your dad — tell me more about that." Don't correct mid-flow.
  • Block 3 second conditional: watch for "If I would work with" — note it and recycle at the end only.
  • Keep energy moving — at B1.1, 2 minutes per question maximum. If a student runs dry, help with a chip: "What happened next?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Best moment: when a student tells a real story about a family member at work. Let it go 45 seconds. Then: "Say the most important part of that story — in one English sentence."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • After Q10: "You just talked about family, work, and feelings — in English. That's a real conversation. Next session we go deeper: what do you do when you hate your job?"
§ 05 · The Exit Shot
💣 One Question. No Easy Answer.
5 min · No chips · Position + reason · Then the loop
05
5 min
EXIT
💣 Exit Shot
Your father offers you a job at the family business. The salary is good. But your father is very difficult to work with — and you know it. Do you take the job?
3 sentences minimum. Start with yes or no. Then tell me the real reason — not the polite one.
📌 B1.1 Model Answer — Reveal AFTER student speaks
"No, I don't take it. I love my father, but I have seen what happens when work and family mix — and it's usually bad. I would rather have a good relationship with him than a job where I have to say yes to everything he says."
🔁 Retention Loop — WhatsApp Homework "Before next session — record a 60-second voice note in English. Your answer to today's Exit Shot. Don't write it first. Just talk. Send it to [teacher number]."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The "yes or no first" instruction forces a grammatical commitment — then the explanation can be messy and that's fine at B1.1.
  • Follow-up only: "What would you say to your father when you say no?" Second conditional activated naturally.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The model answer uses simple, direct language — "I would rather have a good relationship." Point out: "That's a B1.1 sentence. Clean, honest, real English."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Closing: "You just made a decision — and explained it — in English. That's progress."
