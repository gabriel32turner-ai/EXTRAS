Mexican Things Foreigners Don't Understand
The food, the family, the time, the noise, the warmth — some things about Mexico are hard to explain, but very easy to love. Tell a foreigner about your world.
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One funny situation to start. Everyone has a story like this.
⏱ 4 min
Reddit · r/mexico · Most Upvoted Post 2023
"My American friend visited Mexico for the first time. I said dinner is at 3pm. He arrived at exactly 3pm. He was the only person there for 45 minutes. He didn't understand why everyone laughed."
People from other countries often have a different idea of Mexico before they visit. They learn about it in movies, on TV, or online — and sometimes the real Mexico is very different from what they expected. Mexican culture has many things that are normal here but surprising, confusing, or strange for foreigners.
Teacher Script Read the story about the American friend. Let them laugh. Then ask: "Did you ever see a foreigner confused by something very normal in Mexico? What happened?" If they don't have a story yet, try: "What is one thing about Mexico that you would need to explain to a foreigner?" The first answers are usually food, time, or family size. Perfect for the session.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Describe your Mexico in simple English. No complicated analysis.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
Did you ever meet a foreigner who didn't understand something about Mexico? What happened?
Teacher Tip Past simple storytelling. Common answers: a foreigner who arrived on time, who was surprised by spicy food, who didn't know how to greet with a kiss. "I met a foreigner and he/she didn't know how to..." Keep it fun and light.
Q2
Did you ever go to another country — or see something on TV — and think: "That is very different from Mexico"?
Teacher Tip Past simple comparison. Many students haven't traveled, but they watch shows and movies. "I saw a show about _____ and it was very different." "In that country, they eat dinner at 6pm. That's strange for me." All observations are valid.
Q3
What did you eat last Sunday with your family? Describe it.
Teacher Tip Past simple + description. This is a warm, personal question that always produces rich answers. Sunday family meal is a very Mexican ritual — pozole, carne asada, tamales, etc. Help with vocabulary if needed. The key: describe the food and who was there.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
What is the best thing about Mexican culture? How do you explain it to a foreigner in simple English?
Teacher Tip Opinion + description. "I think the best thing is the food / the family / the fiestas." Help them describe it: "In Mexico, we always eat together." "We celebrate everything — birthdays, Day of the Dead, Christmas, everything." Simple sentences are perfect.
Q5
Is "Mexican time" — arriving late to everything — a problem or just a cultural difference? What do you think?
Teacher Tip Andrea's provocateur question made simple. "I think it's normal here, but maybe not in other countries." "For me it's fine, but for work it's a problem." Accept short opinions. This is a gentle cultural self-reflection — not a criticism.
Q6
Do you think the image of Mexico in American movies is correct? What is different in real life?
Teacher Tip Simple present opinion. Most students know the Hollywood stereotype: drugs, poverty, sombreros. "In movies Mexico is always dangerous. In real life, my city is safe and beautiful." "They don't show the food, the family, the culture." Keep it descriptive and proud, not political.
Block 3 · Future Plans (Going to / Will)
Q7
If a foreign friend is going to visit your city next month, what are you going to show them first?
Teacher Tip Going to — fun and personal. "I'm going to take them to ______." "First, I'm going to show them the market / the food / the cathedral." Ask follow-up: "What food are you going to give them first?" Almost always: tacos.
Q8
Will foreigners understand Mexico better in the future — with social media and travel? Or will they always have the wrong idea?
Teacher Tip Will for prediction. "I think they will understand more because of social media." "Maybe they will still have the wrong idea from movies." Simple will + reason. No deep analysis needed — a feeling and a reason is enough.
Block 4 · Past Habits + Reflection (Used to)
Q9
Did you use to do something as a child that is very Mexican — a tradition, a food, or a celebration?
Teacher Tip Used to for childhood memories — very rich at A2.2. "I used to go to my grandmother's house every Sunday for caldo." "We used to make tamales every December 24th." These memories are warm and specific. Help them describe with: "In my family, we used to..."
Q10 · Self-Reflection
What is one thing about Mexico that you love — something you would miss if you lived in another country?
Teacher Tip Simple present + emotional response. "I would miss the food — especially my mom's cooking." "I would miss Sunday with my family." "I would miss the warm weather." The word "miss" is the key structure here — introduce it if they don't use it naturally. This is a warm, affectionate closing question.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. Record your answer in English.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · A2.2 Mexican Things Foreigners Don't Understand
A foreigner is going to visit your city for the first time next week. They are a little scared because they think Mexico is dangerous. What are you going to tell them — and what are you going to show them?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. Tell me: what do you say to your foreign friend? What is the first place you go, and what is the first food you eat together?
Retention Hook This exit shot activates cultural pride in a simple, personal way. The student is now the guide — and they use "going to" to plan a real experience. The fear detail is real (many foreigners do worry about safety) and lets the student correct a stereotype they actually care about. End: "Send me the voice note. Be a good guide — describe everything in English."
