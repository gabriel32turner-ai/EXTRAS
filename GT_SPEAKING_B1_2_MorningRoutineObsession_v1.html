Provocateur rationale: Fernando removes the excuse of "I'm just not a morning person" by connecting it directly to nighttime habits and calling it a choice. Most students will feel a mix of "he's right" and "that's not fair." Both reactions are honest and produce good conversation.

Facilitation move: After the student picks their opinion, ask: "And what time did you go to bed last night?" Let that answer connect naturally to Fernando's argument.
🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Build from personal to provocative. Show tips only after the student answers.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever seriously tried to change your morning routine — wake up earlier, exercise, meditate, or do something different? What happened?
Present perfect opener — very natural. Most students have tried and failed at some point. Model: "Yes — I tried waking up at 6am to exercise for about two weeks. It worked great. Then I had a stressful week at work and it collapsed." Activate: routine, discipline, stick to.
Q2
Have you ever felt jealous or guilty when you see someone on social media showing off their perfect morning routine — gym, smoothie, sunrise, all before 7am?
Activate: guilt, pressure, compare. Honest and fun — most students have felt this. The key is to normalize the feeling without reinforcing the guilt. Model: "Yes — and then I put my phone down and hit snooze again." Light and relatable.
Q3
What does your typical morning look like right now — from when you wake up to when you start work? Walk me through it honestly.
Descriptive and very personal. Students often enjoy describing their mornings. Activate all the verbs naturally: rush, skip, wake up, hit snooze. This question generates the most natural, flowing speech at B1.2. Let them go.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think waking up early actually makes you more productive — or is it just something we believe because successful people say it?
References the Trigger headline. Push for their personal evidence. Model: "I think it depends on the person. I've been productive waking up at 7 and useless waking up at 5:30. The hour isn't the thing — the quality of sleep is." Activate: productivity, energy, burnout.
Q5
Is it fair to judge someone for sleeping until 8am if they do their job well? Does the time you wake up say anything about your character?
Fernando's position again. Most students will say no — it's not fair to judge. Push: "But you probably have a small opinion when you see a colleague arriving at 9:30, right? Be honest." Activate: judge, discipline, habit, pressure.
Q6
Some people spend money on apps, coaches, and books to build a morning routine. Do you think that's a good investment — or is it a waste of money on something you could just do yourself?
Light money question — easy for B1.2. Good for modals: "I think it could be useful if..." or "I would never pay for..." Activate: habit, discipline, routine, pressure. Most students have strong opinions on self-help spending.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your new job required you to start at 7am every day — and you're a natural night owl who does your best work in the evenings?
Second conditional. Real scenario — many professionals face this. Model: "If that happened, I would probably try to adapt for a few weeks — but if it kept affecting my performance, I'd talk to my manager about flexible hours." Activate: force yourself, routine, energy, discipline.
Q8
What would a perfect morning look like for you — if you had no work, no schedule, no alarm? Describe it in detail.
Imaginative and very personal. Great for B1.2 "going to" and conditional structures. Let them dream a little. Some students describe peaceful, slow mornings — others describe active, productive ones. Both reveal character. Compare with their real morning from Q3.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Do you think the obsession with morning routines is really about productivity — or is it about showing other people that you are disciplined, healthy, and successful?
This is the real question behind the whole trend. Model: "I think it's both — but mostly the second one. If it was just about productivity, people wouldn't film it and post it." Activate: pressure, compare, guilt, discipline. Great for nuanced opinions at B1.2.
Q10
Be honest — if you could change one thing about your mornings, what would it be? And why haven't you changed it yet?
The self-reflection close. Very personal and direct — B1.2 can handle this well. The second part ("why haven't you changed it?") is the key. Common answers: "Because I'm tired." "Because I always say tomorrow." "Because I actually like my mornings the way they are." All honest. All productive.
B1.2 grammar check: Q1–Q3 should produce clean present perfect. Q7–Q8 should produce second conditional. If students default to "I will do..." instead of "I would do..." in Block 3, model once and continue. Don't over-correct.

Energy note: This is a high-engagement topic — students enjoy talking about themselves. Keep the pace up in Blocks 1–2 and slow down for Q9 and Q10, where the real reflection happens.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. No right answer. Be honest.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
Your doctor tells you that based on your sleep patterns, stress levels, and energy, you need to go to bed by 10pm and wake up at 6am every day for the next 6 months. No exceptions. You currently go to bed at midnight and wake up at 7:30am. That's 2 hours less per night — your evenings, your TV time, your social life. Are you willing to do it?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Describe your perfect morning — if you could design it from zero. What time would you wake up, what would you do, and how would you feel before you started your day?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: The doctor authority removes the "it's my choice" defense. The specific numbers (10pm, 6am, 2 hours less, 6 months) make it concrete and hard to answer vaguely. The evenings detail is the emotional lever — TV, friends, personal time.

Homework note: The recording prompt is creative and personal — easy to record, activates vocab from today (routine, energy, wake up, rush, skip). Students often describe mornings that are the opposite of their current reality.

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about work-life balance — including how to protect your personal time when your job doesn't stop. Exactly the conversation many of you are already having."
