Phase 1
The Trigger
A real fact. A real conversation.
⏱ 4 min
Psychology Today · 2023
"In a survey of 10,000 adults, 87% said: 'My family is complicated.' Only 4% said their family was 'perfectly normal.' The other 9% didn't answer the question."
Teacher Script
"Okay. Quick question. Hand up — who has a normal family? Nobody? Good. That is normal. Today we talk about family. The good things, the crazy things, the things that make us laugh and the things that make us crazy. Ready?"

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real stories. No wrong answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
What did your family do last Sunday? Was it fun or crazy — or both?
Great opener — low pressure, high vocabulary. Push for details: "Who was there? What did you eat? Did anyone fight?" The Sunday family scene in Mexico is almost universal. Model: "Last Sunday my family was at my mom's house. We ate a lot. My uncle talked very loud. It was nice."
Q2
When was the last time your family had a big argument? What was it about?
Past simple, concrete, personal. If they say "I don't remember" — push: "Was it about money? Food? Someone's boyfriend or girlfriend?" Give options at A2.1 level. Model: "Last month my brother and my dad fought about money. It was not good. But the next day, it was okay."
Q3
Did you have a funny moment with your family last month? What happened?
Shift to positive memory — balances the argument question. Funny family stories are gold for fluency. If they struggle: "Did someone fall? Say something wrong? Forget something important?" Small stories are perfect. Vocabulary: laugh, funny, surprised, embarrassed.
Block 2 — Opinion (Present Simple)
Q4
Do you think it's good to talk about family problems — or is it better to keep the peace?
Classic Mexican family dilemma: "guardar silencio" vs. confront. Don't push one answer. Both are valid. Model: "I think it's better to talk. But in my family, we don't. We keep the peace and eat tamales."
Q5
Is it good to live near your parents — or is some distance better for the relationship?
Very relevant for Mexican professionals. Many students live with or very near parents. Great for real-life opinion: "I think some distance is good. I love my mom but she comes to my house every day." Simple present, clear structure.
Q6
What is more important in a family: money or time together?
Simple comparative opinion. Model: "I think time is more important. But money helps a lot. Without money, there is a lot of stress and problems." Push them to give a real reason, not just the expected "nice" answer.
Block 3 — Future Plans (Going to / Will)
Q7
Are you going to see your family this weekend? What are you going to do together?
Concrete future practice. Push for specific plans: "Are you going to eat together? Watch a game? Have a party?" Model: "I'm going to visit my mom on Saturday. We're going to eat pozole. My brother is going to be there too. It will be loud."
Q8
Is there a family trip, party, or reunion in your future? What will it be like?
Future prediction with will — great for A2.1. "It will be big / loud / fun / complicated." Push for at least two sentences. Model: "In December we are going to have a big reunion. There will be 40 people. It will be great but also very tiring."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — do YOU do something that makes YOUR family crazy? What is it?
The mirror question — students always laugh at this one. Every person is also the "crazy" family member for someone. Model: "Yes. I don't answer my phone. My mom calls me five times and I don't answer. She gets very angry. But I need my space." Celebrate honesty!
Q10
Your cousin asks you for money — 3,000 pesos. He didn't pay you back the last time. What do you say?
Simple scenario, real tension. Very Mexican family situation. Options: say yes, say no, say "I don't have money right now." All are valid. Push: "What do you say exactly? Show me." Role-play the answer in English — 2 or 3 sentences is perfect at A2.1.
