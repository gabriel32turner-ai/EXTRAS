Digital Nomads:
Privilege or Lifestyle?

Working from a beach in Bali sounds like freedom — until you ask who actually gets to do it, and what it costs the city you move to.

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocative Headline
⏱ 4 min
The Guardian · "The Digital Nomad Backlash" · 2023
"In Lisbon, Barcelona, and Mexico City, locals are being priced out of their own neighborhoods by remote workers earning US or European salaries and paying rent in local currency. Digital nomads call it freedom. Residents call it something else."
Teacher Script: Read the quote, then ask: "Have you ever seen this happen in a city you know? Or — be honest — have you ever benefited from earning a salary in one currency and spending it somewhere cheaper?" Most Mexican professionals have a nuanced take here: they may earn in pesos but have seen expats reshape their cities. This session works best when the student has to argue both sides.

The Opinion Wall
Phase 3 · Who do you agree with?
⏱ 10 min
🧑‍💻
Sebastián · 31
UX Designer · Remote · Originally from Guadalajara
"I've been a digital nomad for 3 years. I pay my taxes, I respect the places I live in, and I spend money in local businesses. Why should I feel guilty for having a skill that lets me work from anywhere? People moved to cities for better jobs for generations. I just do it differently. Call it 'gentrification' if you want — it's just economic mobility."
👩‍🏘️
Valentina · 43
Architect · Mexico City resident
"My neighborhood in Roma Norte changed completely in 4 years. My friends who grew up there can't afford to live there anymore. The coffee shops are all in English. The rent doubled. I don't hate the nomads personally — but the system that lets them reshape a city without any accountability is deeply unfair. Freedom for some isn't freedom for everyone."
👨‍🌾
Rodrigo · 38
Business Owner · Oaxaca
"Nomads are great for my business. They eat out every day, pay for experiences, book local tours. Before remote work, Oaxaca was invisible to the world. Now we have direct flights and international attention. Yes, some rents went up — but the economic energy nomads bring is real. I'll take the trade-off."
Provocateur
✍️
Daniela · 35
Journalist · Based in Berlin
"Digital nomadism is just colonialism with a laptop. You move to a country because it's cheap — which means it's cheap because it's poor — and you act like that's a lifestyle choice, not an economic imbalance you're exploiting. The 'freedom' you're celebrating is built on someone else's limited options. No amount of spending at local coffee shops fixes that math."
Teacher Note: Daniela is the provocateur because the word "colonialism" is designed to provoke strong reactions — agreement or fierce rejection. Push: "Is that comparison fair or is it an exaggeration?" Then flip it: "As a Mexican professional, are you on Daniela's side, Valentina's side, or Rodrigo's — and why does your answer depend on which city you're from?"

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever worked remotely for an extended period — a week, a month, longer? Where were you, and what surprised you about the experience?
Even students who haven't done it properly have worked from home, a café, or another city. The point is to activate personal experience. Push: "What did you miss about the office — if anything?" Target: present perfect for experience + past simple for the story.
Q2
Have you noticed changes in your city or neighborhood because of foreign workers or expats moving in? What changed — prices, culture, vibe?
This is highly relevant for students in CDMX, Guadalajara, or Monterrey. Most will have an opinion. Push: "Did you feel like those changes were good, bad, or complicated?" Key vocab to activate: gentrification, displacement, local culture, rent prices.
Q3
If you could work from anywhere in the world for 6 months — fully paid, same salary — where would you go and why?
Light question to warm up the personal imagination. Push: "Would you feel like a digital nomad — or would that just be a long business trip?" Transition into: "And would you feel weird spending a Mexican or local salary in a place where that goes much further?"
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is it fair for someone to move to a cheaper country just because their salary allows it — even if it raises costs for the people who live there?
The moral core of the session. There's no clean answer. Push both sides: "Is individual freedom more important than collective impact?" / "Should rich-country workers be blamed for market forces they didn't create?" Target: modals for obligation (should, ought to, shouldn't).
Q5
Should digital nomads pay taxes in the countries they live in — not just where their company is based? Is it fair that they benefit from local infrastructure without contributing to it?
This is a real policy debate — Portugal actually introduced a digital nomad visa with specific tax obligations in 2022. Push: "Is this a problem the government should solve — or the individual nomad?" Good for modals + conditional: "If I lived somewhere for 6 months, I think I should/shouldn't..."
Q6
Daniela says digital nomadism is "colonialism with a laptop." Is that a fair comparison — or is it an exaggeration that shuts down a more nuanced conversation?
Students will split hard here. Some will embrace the comparison; others will find it reductive. Both positions are valid. Push: "What would have to be true for the comparison to be fair? What's different?" Target: present perfect + conditional. Key: don't let the student dismiss it without engaging with the actual argument.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If your company offered you full remote work permanently, would you stay in your city — or move somewhere cheaper and pocket the difference? Be honest.
Target: second conditional. The key word is "honest" — most people's instinct is pragmatic. Push: "Would you feel guilty? Or would you feel you've earned it?" This question makes the student step into the nomad's shoes and realize the appeal is real and personal.
Q8
If your city started attracting thousands of remote workers from the US earning 5x the local salary, what would you want the government to do about it — if anything?
Policy thinking question. Push for specifics: "Price caps? Special visa taxes? Nothing at all?" Target: second conditional + modals for policy (should, could, might). Good moment to bring in Rodrigo's perspective — economic benefit is real, even if costs are unequal.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Is the digital nomad lifestyle actually lonely — or do people just not want to admit it because the Instagram version looks perfect?
Shifts the conversation from political to personal. Research consistently shows nomads report high loneliness after the novelty wears off. Push: "What does 'home' mean to you — is it a place or a feeling?" Target: present perfect + modals for speculation. Good energy shift after the heavier policy questions.
Q10
Uncomfortable question: have you ever judged someone for "escaping" a responsibility — a family, a city, a community — because they could afford to? And are you sure your judgment was fair?
The self-reflection closer. Forces the student to examine their own assumptions about freedom vs. responsibility. Let silence sit. Push: "What's the difference between escaping and evolving?" The goal is real, uncomfortable self-examination in English.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One Final Dilemma
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
Your company goes fully remote. You realize you could move from Guadalajara to a small town in Oaxaca and cut your monthly expenses in half — saving an extra $8,000 MXN per month. But your parents are getting older and live 10 minutes from your current apartment. Your partner is excited. Your siblings think you'd be abandoning the family. Do you go?
Homework Loop: "Record a 60-second WhatsApp voice message. Tell me your decision — and give me the one argument that would change your mind. In English. No script."
