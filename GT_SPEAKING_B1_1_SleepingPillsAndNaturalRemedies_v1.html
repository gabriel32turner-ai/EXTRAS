🪝 Phase 1 — The Trigger
The Headline
Read it aloud. Then ask the first question.
4 min
01
BBC Health · Sleep
"1 in 3 adults doesn't sleep well. The global sleep aid market is now worth $80 billion. We are paying enormous amounts of money to solve a problem that previous generations solved with a boring day and no phone."
— We've made sleep complicated. It used to just happen.
The Guardian · Health
"Half of people who take sleeping pills regularly say they don't actually feel rested the next day — but they can't stop taking them because they're afraid of what the night will be like without them."
— Taking something to sleep but not actually sleeping. A modern paradox.
Launch line: "How did you sleep last night? Not just hours — quality. Did you wake up feeling rested?" [Let them answer.] "And if you had a bad night — what do you do? Chamomile tea? Phone? Pills? Nothing?" [Short answers fine.] "Today we talk about sleep — and all the things we try to fix it."

"I've taken a sleeping pill every night for 3 years. My doctor knows. It's controlled. I sleep 8 hours and I function perfectly. People act like taking a pill is shameful. If your eyesight is bad, you wear glasses. If you can't sleep, you take a pill. Same logic."

Valeria, 34
Chef · Guadalajara
"I had terrible insomnia for two years. Then I stopped drinking coffee after noon, put my phone in another room, and started walking for 30 minutes every day. In three weeks I was sleeping normally. Natural changes are harder but they actually fix the problem. Pills just hide it."
🧓
Rosa, 62
Homemaker · Celaya
"When I can't sleep, I make a tea with manzanilla and a little honey. My mother taught me. My grandmother taught her. It works every time — not because of science, but because it relaxes me. The ritual is part of the remedy. Modern people forgot that."
Provocateur
😤
Fernando, 41
Engineer · CDMX
"If you can't sleep without a pill or a tea or a routine or an app — you have a stress problem, not a sleep problem. Fix your life and you'll sleep. Nobody in a truly happy, calm life needs help falling asleep. The insomnia is just a symptom. Stop treating the symptom."
Provocateur rationale: Fernando reframes all sleep solutions as avoidance — insomnia is a life problem, not a sleep problem. This is partly true and partly oversimplified. Students who have genuinely struggled with sleep will find it harsh. Students who sleep fine will privately agree. Push: "Is Fernando right? Have you ever fixed a sleep problem by fixing something else in your life?"
🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Personal and honest. Show tips only after the student answers.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever had a period in your life when you couldn't sleep well? What was happening in your life at that time?
Present perfect + past simple. The connection to life circumstances is the interesting part — almost always stress, worry, or change. Model: "Yes — when I changed jobs three years ago. I would lie awake thinking about work for hours. It lasted about two months." Activate: insomnia, worry, toss and turn, stress.
Q2
Have you ever tried a natural remedy for sleep — a tea, a routine, exercise, or something your family recommended? Did it work?
Present perfect — Rosa's territory. Very personal in Mexico where family remedies are common. Model: "My mother always said warm milk with honey before bed. It probably didn't do much medically — but it calmed me down enough to sleep." Activate: remedy, tea, routine, try.
Q3
Have you ever taken a sleeping pill or any medicine to sleep? What was the experience like?
Diego's territory. Some students will say yes, some no — both answers are interesting. If yes: "Did you feel rested the next day?" (Reference the second Trigger.) If no: "Would you take one if a doctor prescribed it?" Activate: pill, side effect, dependence, function.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Is it OK to take a sleeping pill regularly — or is that dangerous? What do you think?
Diego's argument vs. Valeria's. Most students say "it depends" — push: "Depends on what? How long? What type? With a doctor's guidance?" Good for modals: "You should only take them if... / You shouldn't rely on them for..." Activate: pill, dependence, side effect, rely on.
Q5
Do you trust natural remedies more than medicine — or the other way around?
Very personal and culturally relevant in Mexico. Most B1.1 students have a mix of both beliefs. Good for "I think... because..." structures. Reference Rosa's position. Activate: remedy, tea, rely on, try. The generational angle is interesting — older students often trust natural more, younger students trust medicine more.
Q6
Do you think your phone is the main reason people don't sleep well anymore — or is it just stress?
References the first Trigger quote. Most students say both — it's the phone AND the stress. Push: "When you use your phone in bed, are you relaxing — or are you adding more information to your already busy brain?" Activate: screen, scroll, worry, routine, avoid.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you had serious insomnia for three months — nothing worked, not the tea, not the routine, not anything?
Second conditional — the escalation scenario. Most say they'd see a doctor eventually. Push: "Would you take a pill if the doctor prescribed it? Or would you still resist?" Activate: insomnia, pill, try, rely on, function.
Q8
What would your daily life look like if you slept 8 perfect hours every night for one full month?
Imaginative second conditional — students often describe feeling like a completely different person. Model: "I think I would be kinder, calmer, and more patient. I would probably be more productive at work. And I'd probably stop craving coffee at 3pm." Activate: rest, function, routine, avoid.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Do you think the real reason you don't sleep well is something in your life that you haven't fixed — a relationship, a job, a worry — and not actually a sleep problem?
Fernando's argument reframed as a personal question. Give students time to think. Some will say yes and name the thing. That is the most powerful moment in the lab. Don't rush past it. Activate: worry, insomnia, stress, remedy.
Q10
Right now — tonight, after this class — what will you do differently to sleep better? Even one small thing.
Closing action question — rare and very B1.1 appropriate. Connects today's conversation to tonight's behavior. Students often name simple things: "leave my phone outside the room," "drink chamomile tea," "stop working at 9pm." Use "going to" future: "I'm going to try..." Great grammar reinforcement naturally embedded.
B1.1 grammar check: Q1–Q3 = present perfect + past simple. Q7–Q8 = second conditional. Q10 = "going to" future — "I'm going to try..." is perfect B1.1 and feels natural in context.

Note on Q9: This is the most personal question of the four labs in this category. Some students will name a real life situation they haven't resolved. That's a success — not a problem. Acknowledge it briefly. Move gently to Q10 which closes on action, not pain.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. Honest answer.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
You haven't slept well for 2 weeks. You're tired, irritable, and you can't focus at work. A friend gives you a sleeping pill and says: "Just take it tonight — you need the rest." You don't have a prescription. Do you take it?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Tell me about your sleep — how many hours do you get, is it good quality, and what is the one thing that most affects how well you sleep?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: The scenario is concrete, realistic, and morally ambiguous. Taking a prescribed pill vs. a friend's pill is a real decision many adults face. The "you need the rest" detail adds pressure. The answer reveals how desperate vs. cautious the student is — both reveal character.

Homework note: The recording prompt is simple and descriptive — perfect B1.1 homework. Most students can speak for 60 seconds about their own sleep without preparation. High completion rate expected.

Upsell moment: "This is our last Health & Wellness lab for now. What topic would you like to explore next? Work? Relationships? Money? Your choice opens the next conversation."
