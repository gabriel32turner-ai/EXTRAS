🪝 Phase 1 — The Trigger
The Question
Read it aloud. Then ask the student directly.
4 min
01
A simple question for today
"Do you have a habit that other people think is strange — but you think is completely normal?"
— We all do things our own way. Today we talk about those small, personal rituals that make us who we are.
Fun fact · Psychology Today
"Almost everyone has at least one small habit that other people find unusual — like checking the alarm three times, or always eating food in a specific order."
— You are not alone. Everybody is a little strange.
Launch line: "Before we start — I want to know one strange habit you have. Just one. Something small." [Model yours first to break the ice: "My strange habit is... I always check that the door is closed three times before I sleep. Always three times. Not two, not four."] Then let the student share. This always gets a laugh and opens the session perfectly.

🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Short questions. Short answers are fine. Use the vocab from today.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
When you were a child, did you have a strange habit? What did you do?
Past simple — describing a childhood habit. Model: "When I was a child, I always organized my crayons by color. Always. If someone moved them, I was very upset." Keep it simple and fun. Correct tense errors gently: "I used to... / I always did..."
Q2
Did your family or friends ever say something about a habit you had? What did they say?
Past simple — reported reaction. Model: "My mom always said: 'You're so strange!' because I put salt on everything, even fruit." Students love sharing these family memories. Activate: "Are you serious?" reaction phrase naturally.
Q3
What was the last strange thing you did? Yesterday, this week — something small.
Past simple — very recent story. This brings the habit to life as a real, current thing. Model: "Yesterday I checked my alarm four times before I slept. Four times. I know it was set. But I needed to check." Activate: check, repeat, count.
Block 2 — Opinion (Simple Present)
Q4
Do you think strange habits are bad? Or are they just personal?
Simple opinion question. Model: "I think they are personal. They are not bad. They make you feel good or calm. That is okay." Keep it simple — "I think... / I don't think..." is the target structure here.
Q5
Is it good to have routines? Or is it boring?
Simple opinion — two clear options. Model: "I think it is good. Routines help me feel calm. But some routines are boring — like cleaning every day." The student can give a short answer of one or two sentences. Don't push for complexity.
Q6
What do you think when someone tells you a very strange habit? Do you judge them?
Self-awareness question — simple and fun. Model: "I don't judge. I think it's interesting! Everyone is different. But sometimes I think — really? That's very strange!" Activate the colloquial phrases: "No way!" / "Are you serious?"
Block 3 — Future Plans (Going to / Will)
Q7
Are you going to change any habit this year? Which one?
Going to — future intention. Model: "I'm going to try to stop checking my phone before bed. I check it every night. It's a bad habit. I'm going to put it in another room." Simple sentence structure. Help student if they say "I will change" — both are acceptable at A2.2 but "going to" is the target here.
Q8
Will you always have this habit — or do you think it will change?
Will — future prediction about a personal habit. Model: "I think I will always check the door. I will probably do it when I am 80 years old. Some habits never change." Fun and personal. Short answer is perfectly fine.
Block 4 — Past Habits + Reflection (Used to)
Q9
Did you use to have a habit when you were younger that you stopped? What was it?
Used to — past habit that is finished. Model: "I used to eat the same lunch every day — the same food, always. My mom made it. When I got my own house, I stopped. Now I eat different things." Help student with "I used to..." if they say "When I was younger, I did..." — gently model the structure.
Q10
Did you use to think your habits were normal — or did you always know they were a little strange?
Gentle self-reflection with used to. Model: "I used to think it was completely normal! I thought everyone checked their alarm many times. Then my friends told me — no, that's strange. I was surprised." Light and fun closing question for this block.
A2.2 pacing: Keep answers short. If the student gives 2-3 sentences, that is excellent. Don't push for more complexity. Correct errors gently and move on — fluency over accuracy at this level.

Grammar targets: B1 = past simple (Q1-3), simple present opinions (Q4-6), going to / will (Q7-8), used to (Q9-10). If student confuses used to with past simple, accept it — the concept is what matters, not perfect form.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. Answer in 2-3 sentences. Simple and personal.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
What is the one habit you have that you are going to keep forever — even if people think it's strange? Why?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Tell me about one strange habit you have. What is it? When do you do it? And does anyone in your family or your work know about it?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: The Exit Shot at A2.2 is not a moral dilemma — it's a personal declaration. "Going to keep forever" uses the target grammar naturally. The "Why?" adds just enough personal dimension without requiring complex English. Students feel proud when they can answer this confidently.

Homework note: The recording task is descriptive and concrete — perfect for A2.2. Three simple questions guide the minute of speech. Expect good completion rates because the topic is fun and personal.

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about the best and worst part of your day — very similar energy, a little more personal."
