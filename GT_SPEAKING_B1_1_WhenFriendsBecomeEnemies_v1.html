When Friends
Become Enemies
You trusted them completely. Then something happened. How do you go from best friends to people who can't look at each other?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
A real fact. A real conversation.
⏱ 4 min
Psychology Today · 2023
"Most adults lose their closest friends not because of one big fight — but because of a small moment they never talked about. The friendship dies slowly, in silence, and nobody knows exactly when it ended."
Teacher Script
"Think of a friend you had — a real friend — that you don't talk to anymore. You don't have to say their name. But can you remember the exact moment things started to change? Today we're going to talk about friendships that went wrong. This one gets personal."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real talk. No right answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever lost a close friend — not because of death, but because something happened between you? What was it?
Give them space to share. If they say "yes, but I don't want to say the details" — that's fine. Ask: "Did it end with a fight — or just with silence?" The silence vs. big fight distinction opens a lot of vocabulary: lose touch, grow apart, cut off, drift away.
Q2
Did you ever find out that a friend was talking about you behind your back? What did you do?
Almost everyone has a story here. The interesting fork: "Did you confront them or disappear?" Both are valid. The choice reveals personality — and opens great discussion about what the "right" response is. Neither is obviously correct.
Q3
Have you ever had to choose sides when two friends had a fight? What happened — and do you think you made the right choice?
Choosing sides is a rich real-life scenario. Model answer: "My two best friends broke up. I tried not to choose. I lost both of them. I think I should have been honest with both instead of trying to stay neutral." The cost of the choice is what matters.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Is it OK to end a friendship without an explanation — just stop responding? Or do you owe people a reason?
The "ghosting" question for friendships. Push for a real position: "Okay, but what if your best friend of 10 years just disappeared — would you want a reason?" The personal application usually changes their abstract answer.
Q5
Do you think you can be real friends with someone who was once your enemy? Or is that too hard?
Has anyone in the group had this experience — an enemy who became a friend? The story is always more interesting than the theory. If not: "Can you imagine it? What would have to change first?"
Q6
Don Ernesto says that in every broken friendship, both people did something wrong. Do you agree — or do some friendships have a clear bad person and a clear good person?
The spiciest opinion question. Softly challenge the "it was all their fault" narrative without dismissing real hurt. "Are there situations where one person really IS 100% wrong?" — the answer is usually yes, which makes the conversation more honest.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your best friend told your biggest secret to other people?
Simple hypothetical with high emotional charge. Push past "I would be angry" — to: "Would you talk to them? Would you end the friendship? Would you want an apology?" Each option leads to a different conversation about what friendship means to them.
Q8
What would you do if a good friend asked you to lie for them — to cover something serious?
The loyalty vs. honesty dilemma. A classic B1.1 hypothetical: simple grammar, huge real stakes. Model answer: "I would say no — because I don't want to be in that position. If they asked me, it means they already know it's wrong."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Is there someone in your life right now who used to be a good friend — and you know you should talk to them, but you haven't? What's stopping you?
The most personal question in the lab. Give them time. Don't fill the silence. If they share — listen first. If they say no — gently push: "Really? Nobody?" The trigger quote says most friendships die in silence. This question lives right there.
Q10
Honestly — have you ever been the bad friend? The one who disappeared, told the secret, or said something you shouldn't have?
The mirror question. This is where Don Ernesto's opinion comes back. Most people have a moment like this — and most people carry it quietly. This is the question that makes the class feel real instead of theoretical. Celebrate honesty. Don't rush to comfort or judge.
