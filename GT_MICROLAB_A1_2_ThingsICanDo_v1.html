Things I Can Do and Can't Do

Everyone has talents — and everyone has limits. What are yours?

🎪 Show & Tell 5 min

Show something on your phone that represents a skill you have.

Your Task

Open your phone. Find a photo of something you can do well — a meal you cook, a sport you play, a place you drive to, a project at work. Show it and explain.

Teacher Script — Model First

"Look — this is a photo of my pasta. I can cook Italian food very well. I make pasta every Saturday. But I can't cook Mexican food — it's difficult! I can't make mole. OK — show me YOUR photo. What can YOU do?"

Sentence Starters

I can _______ very well.
I do this every _______ .
I like it because _______ .
But I can't _______ .

Teacher Tip

Always push for the CAN'T after the CAN. The contrast is where the real conversation lives. If they say "I can drive" — ask "But what CAN'T you do? Can you drive a truck? Can you park in small spaces?" Push for specifics.

"Me neither!" is high-value — teaches negative agreement naturally. Model: "I can't sing." Student: "Me neither!" Drill it 3 times in different contexts. Also drill "recipe" — REH-sih-pee, not "re-CI-pe". "Patience" = PAY-shens, not "pa-TI-en-cia".

🃏 Question Cards 12 min

Teacher models first. Then you personalize.

Question 01
What can you do very well?
Now you: what is YOUR best skill?
Teacher Model

"I can organize things very well. My house is always clean. I can also cook Italian food — I make pasta every Saturday. I practice a lot."

Teacher Tip

Push for TWO skills, not just one. "OK great — what ELSE can you do well?" Doubles output.

Question 02
What can't you do? What is difficult for you?
Now you: what CAN'T you do?
Teacher Model

"I can't sing — it is terrible! My family always says 'please stop!' I also can't draw. My pictures look like a child's pictures."

Teacher Tip

Humor works well here. People enjoy being self-deprecating about their limitations. Let them laugh and produce naturally. Push for "My _______ looks like..." — great structure for fun answers.

Question 03
Can you cook? What do you usually cook?
Now you: describe YOUR cooking ability.
Teacher Model

"Yes, I can cook basic things. I cook eggs and pasta. I can follow a recipe but I can't create food without one. My wife cooks better — she doesn't need a recipe."

Teacher Tip

Compare with family: "Who cooks better in your family — you or your wife/husband?" Generates third person: "She cooks..." / "He can..."

Question 04
Can you speak another language?
Now you: what languages can YOU speak?
Teacher Model

"I can speak English and Spanish. I can understand a little Portuguese but I can't speak it. I study English every day because I like languages."

Teacher Tip

Great meta-moment: they are IN an English class talking ABOUT languages. Push: "Can you speak English well?" This generates honest self-assessment. Celebrate: "You are speaking English RIGHT NOW — that is a skill!"

Question 05
Can you play a sport or an instrument?
Now you: what sport or instrument can YOU play?
Teacher Model

"I can play soccer but not very well. I play on Sundays with my friends. I can't play any instrument — I don't have patience for that. Music is difficult for me."

Teacher Tip

Add qualifiers: "Can you play WELL? Or just a little?" Teaches "not very well" / "a little" / "very well" — great adverb practice naturally embedded in can/can't.

Question 06
Can you fix things at home?
Now you: are YOU good with tools?
Teacher Model

"I can fix simple things — a door, a shelf. But I can't fix electrical things. When something electrical breaks, I call a professional. I don't like danger!"

Teacher Tip

Manufacturing professionals light up here — many can fix complex equipment at work. Push: "Can you fix machines at work? What can you fix?" This bridges personal and professional identity.

Question 07
What can your best friend do very well?
Now you: describe YOUR best friend's skills.
Teacher Model

"My best friend can sing very well. He sings at parties and everybody likes it. He can also cook — he makes incredible tacos. But he can't dance. Nobody is perfect!"

Teacher Tip

Third person practice: "He can... She can..." Watch for errors: "He can sings..." → "He CAN SING." No -s after can. Common error at A1.2.

Question 08
What do you want to learn? What new skill?
Now you: what do YOU want to learn next?
Teacher Model

"I want to learn photography. I can't take good photos now — they are always bad! I also want to learn to cook Japanese food. Sushi is my dream."

Teacher Tip

"I want to learn..." is a bonus chunk. Don't teach it as grammar — just model it. If they use it naturally, celebrate. Great aspirational closer — always ends on a positive note about growth.

✨ My Sentence 5 min

Build one perfect sentence. Practice it. Record it.

I can _______ very well but I can't _______ because _______ .

🎙️ Record this sentence as a WhatsApp voice note (30 seconds).

Teacher Tip

Target: "I can cook Italian food very well but I can't cook Mexican food because it is too difficult." This has can + can't + because — the full A1.2 toolkit in one sentence. Three repetitions, record. Trophy sentence.
