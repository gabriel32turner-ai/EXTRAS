🪝 Phase 1 — The Trigger
The Question
Read it aloud. Then ask the student directly.
4 min
01
A simple fact about habits
"Scientists say it takes 66 days to break a bad habit. But most people give up after 10 days. And then they start the bad habit again."
— We all have habits we want to change. Most of us don't change them. And that's very normal.
The question for today
"What is your worst bad habit? The one you know is bad — but you do it every single day anyway?"
— No judgment here. Just honesty. We all have one.
Launch line: "I'm going to tell you one of my bad habits first." [Share yours — phone before bed, too much coffee, procrastinating, etc.] "Now it's your turn. What's yours?" [Student answers.] "Perfect. Today we talk about bad habits — the ones we know are bad and can't stop. No judgment, just honesty." Model honesty first — it makes the student feel safe to share their own.

🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Honest answers are the best answers. Show tips after the student responds.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
What is your worst bad habit? Tell me about it — what do you do, when do you do it, and why is it bad?
The central confession. At A2.1, students love this because it's personal and no grammar is "wrong." Model: "My worst habit is the phone. I check it at dinner. I check it in bed. I check it when I talk to my family. It's bad. I know. I can't stop." Simple, honest, three short sentences. Activate: scroll, ignore, admit.
Q2
Did you ever try to stop a bad habit? What happened?
Past simple — the attempt-and-fail story. Model: "Yes. Last January I tried to stop eating junk food. I lasted three weeks. Then I went to a birthday party. There was cake. And chips. I ate everything. Then I started again the next Monday." Activate: quit, fail, give up, start again.
Q3
Did anyone in your family or at work say something about your bad habit? What did they say?
Past simple — external feedback. Model: "My wife said: 'Put the phone away at dinner.' I said: 'Yes, you're right.' Then I checked it again five minutes later. She was angry. I said 'I know, I know.'" Activate the colloquial phrase naturally. Very A2.1 appropriate.
Block 2 — Opinion (Simple Present)
Q4
Do you think bad habits are a big problem — or is it okay to have one or two?
Simple opinion. Model: "I think one or two bad habits are okay. Life is short. But some habits — like smoking — are really dangerous. It depends on the habit." Simple present + "I think" + "it depends" = perfect A2.1 structures.
Q5
Is it easy to change a bad habit? Do you need help from other people — or can you do it alone?
Opinion on effort and support. Model: "I think it's very difficult alone. You need someone to say 'stop!' when you want to do the bad thing. Or you need to not have it in the house. No chips at home — no chips problem." Simple solutions in simple language.
Q6
Do you agree with Ricardo — "I can't" really means "I don't want to badly enough"?
Opinion on Ricardo's provocateur position. Model: "I think Ricardo is partly right. Maybe I don't want to stop enough. But habits are also very powerful. Your brain wants the same thing every day. It's not easy." Or: "I agree with Ricardo. I just need more willpower." Both valid.
Block 3 — Future Plans (Going to / Will)
Q7
Are you going to try to change your bad habit? What are you going to do?
Going to — personal plan. The honest answer is often funny. Model: "I'm going to try. I'm going to put my phone in another room at night. And I'm going to ask my wife to help me." Or: "No. I'm not going to try right now. Maybe Monday." Both are perfectly natural A2.1 answers.
Q8
What will you do next time you feel tempted by your bad habit?
Will — future action plan. Model: "Next time I want to check my phone at dinner, I will put it in my bag. I will leave it in another room. I will tell my family: 'Help me.'" Or: "I will probably check it anyway. But then I will feel bad about it." Both honest.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — do you ever hide your bad habit from someone? Who? Why?
Gentle self-reflection. Very A2.1 — everyone hides something. Model: "Yes. I eat junk food in the car before I arrive home. My wife doesn't know. Well — she probably knows. But I don't tell her." Activate: hide, admit, temptation. Gives a laugh and breaks any remaining self-consciousness.
Q10
What is one good habit you want to start — not just stop a bad one, but add something positive?
Closing forward-looking question. Shifts from negative (bad habits) to positive (new habits). Model: "I want to start walking 20 minutes every morning. Not a lot. Just 20 minutes. I think that can change my day." Or: "I want to read more. I spend 2 hours on my phone. I can read 30 minutes of that." Activate: start again, Monday.
A2.1 grammar check: Past simple (Q1-3), simple present (Q4-6), going to / will (Q7-8), simple present + future (Q9-10).

Tone note: This lab works because it's non-judgmental and the teacher models honesty first. Students need to feel safe to share real bad habits. The humor comes naturally when people recognize shared habits. Never pressure for specifics — if a student says "I have a bad habit but I don't want to say it," accept that. The grammar is still the goal.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One scenario. Real. Personal. What are you going to do?
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
Your doctor says: "Your bad habit is a real problem for your health. You need to stop in the next 30 days." This is not a suggestion. Your family is worried. You know you need to change. What are you going to do tomorrow — the first day? And what is going to be the hardest moment?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Tell me your worst bad habit. When did it start? Did you try to stop? What happened? And are you going to try again?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: Doctor authority makes it concrete. "30 days" is specific. Two "going to" questions embedded naturally: day one action + hardest moment. The family detail adds emotional weight without requiring complex language. Students think about this on the way home.

Homework note: Four guided questions create exactly one minute of honest speech. Past simple + going to. Very natural for A2.1. High completion expected because the topic is genuinely personal.

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about something completely different. Your choice — work, travel, family, or something from your life. You decide."
