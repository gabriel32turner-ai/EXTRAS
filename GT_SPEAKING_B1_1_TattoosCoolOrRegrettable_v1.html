Tattoos: Cool or Regrettable?

Permanent art or permanent mistake? What you thought was a great idea at 20 looks very different at 40.

🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
⏱ 4 min
"Around 30% of people with tattoos say they regret at least one of them. The most common regret? Getting a tattoo with a partner's name on it. The tattoo removal industry is now worth over $5 billion worldwide."
— Harris Poll / Statista, 2023
Teacher script: "Quick question — do you have a tattoo? Or do you want one?" [Pause.] "Now — if you have one, do you still love it? Or do you wish you could remove it?" [Short pause.] "Today we are talking about tattoos: why people get them, why they regret them, and what it says about us."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Do you have a tattoo? If yes — what is it and why did you get it? If no — have you ever wanted one?
The most personal opener possible. Both answers are equally interesting. Students without tattoos can talk about what they would get and why — or why they have decided not to. Very low pressure, very high engagement.
Q2
Have you ever looked at a tattoo — yours or someone else's — and thought "that was a bad idea"?
Present perfect + judgment. Rodrigo's story always comes up here. Good for "I think / I thought..." and past simple narrative. Model: "My friend got his university team's name tattooed and now he hates football."
Q3
Have you ever judged someone because of a tattoo — or felt judged because of yours?
Honest self-reflection. Most people have done both. The gap between "I don't judge" and actual behavior is interesting. Elena's HR perspective is useful here. Keep it light — no need for it to get heavy.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think tattoos affect how people see you at work? Should they?
Elena's HR angle — very relevant for professional students. "I think tattoos should/shouldn't matter at work because..." Good for modals of opinion. Many students have lived this directly.
Q5
Is it okay to get a tattoo of a person's name? When is it a good idea — and when is it a disaster?
Rodrigo's catastrophic ex-tattoo is the perfect anchor for this. "I think it is okay if... but it is a bad idea if..." Good for conditional 1. Expect laughs and strong opinions.
Q6
Do you believe that tattoos still have a special meaning — or are they just fashion now?
Roberto's provocateur point. Do tattoos lose meaning when everyone has them? "I believe that..." / "In my opinion..." Good for expressing belief simply. Both sides are defensible — which is the point.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your son or daughter came home with a large tattoo on their neck at age 18?
Second conditional. Very emotional and specific. "If my son came home with a neck tattoo... I would..." Generates strong and funny answers. Age 18 = legal, so they can't stop them. How do parents deal with it?
Q8
What would you get tattooed on your body if you had to get one tattoo tomorrow — and it had to be visible?
Creative and fun second conditional. Forces even anti-tattoo students to engage imaginatively. "If I had to get one... I would get..." Even people who hate tattoos have a funny or surprising answer to this.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Is it fair that some companies do not hire people with visible tattoos? Or is that discrimination?
Ethics and workplace angle. "I think it is fair / unfair because..." Elena's perspective is directly relevant. Keep grammar simple: "It should/shouldn't be allowed to..." Students often have strong opinions about this.
Q10
If you have a tattoo you love right now — do you think you will still love it at 70 years old?
The long-view self-reflection question. Simple grammar, big impact. If they have no tattoo: "Would you regret it at 70 if you got one now?" Forces students to think about permanence. Often ends with laughter — which is perfect.
