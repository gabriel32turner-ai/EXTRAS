Layoffs by Email

Tech giants fired thousands of employees via form email — sometimes with 10 years of loyalty in the balance. Is this the new normal, or a moral failure?

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocative Headline
⏱ 4 min
The New York Times · Tech Layoffs · 2022–2023
"Thousands of Twitter and Meta employees found out they had been laid off when their work laptops suddenly stopped working — or when they received a form email at 3am. No call. No meeting. No goodbye. Just: access denied."
From: hr-noreply@company.com · To: All Team Members
Important Company Update
Dear Team,

After careful consideration of our business needs and current economic conditions, we have made the difficult decision to reduce our workforce by approximately 18%.

If you are impacted, you will receive a separate email within the next 30 minutes with details about your severance package and transition resources. We deeply value your contributions and wish you all the best in your next chapter.

Thank you for everything you have done for this company.

— The Leadership Team
Fictionalized composite based on real layoff communications from 2022–2023. The language and format are real.
Teacher Script: Show the email. Read it slowly. Then ask: "If this email arrived at 3am and it was about your job — how long would it take you to actually process what just happened?" Let that land. Then: "Notice the language: 'difficult decision,' 'deeply value your contributions,' 'next chapter.' What do you feel when you read those words?" Target: emotional vocabulary + critical reading of corporate language.

The Opinion Wall
Phase 3 · Who do you agree with?
⏱ 10 min
👩‍💼
Camila · 33
Senior Developer · CDMX
"I was laid off by email after 6 years. Six years. Not a call, not a meeting — an email. I found out when my laptop stopped connecting. I don't care about the money. I care that the company treated me like a subscription they cancelled. You don't do that to a human being. Ever."
👨‍💻
Javier · 44
CFO · Monterrey
"I've had to lead layoffs. It's the worst part of this job. But there's a real operational reason for doing it by email simultaneously — if you call people one by one, information leaks, panic spreads, people start making decisions before they have the full picture. It's not heartless. It's containment. I hate it — but I understand it."
🧑‍🎓
Fernanda · 27
HR Coordinator · Guadalajara
"The real problem isn't the email — it's that companies build a 'family' narrative for years, and then the moment business conditions change, you're a spreadsheet item. If companies were honest from day one — 'this is a transaction, not a family' — the email would still hurt, but at least it wouldn't feel like a betrayal."
Provocateur
👨‍🏦
Eduardo · 55
Board Member · Querétaro
"Look — when you accepted that salary, you accepted the risk. Employment is a business agreement, not a marriage. The emotional reaction to a layoff email reveals a fundamental misunderstanding: your loyalty was always one-directional. Companies don't owe you a tearful goodbye. They owe you the severance package they promised. Nothing more."
Teacher Note: Eduardo is the provocateur because he attacks the emotional premise of the discussion — the idea that loyalty deserves anything in return. His position is logically defensible but emotionally brutal, which is exactly what makes it useful for debate. Push: "Is Eduardo describing how business actually works, or how it should work?" Then: "Has anyone ever made you feel like Eduardo's perspective is the real one?"

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever been laid off, or have you witnessed someone close to you being let go? What was the experience like — and how was it communicated?
If the student has personal experience, this conversation will run itself — your job is to keep them in English and ask follow-up questions. If they haven't: "What's the worst way you could imagine finding out?" Target: past simple for the narrative, present perfect for the connection to now.
Q2
Have you ever worked somewhere that said "we're a family here"? Looking back, was it true — or was it just a way to get more out of you?
This question targets the "family" trap that Fernanda describes. Most students will smile with recognition. Push: "What specifically made you feel it was — or wasn't — a real family?" Key vocab: loyalty, reciprocal, commitment, disposable. The dissonance between the "family" narrative and a layoff email is the emotional core of this session.
Q3
How much notice do you think an employee deserves before being let go? Does it depend on how many years they've worked there?
Good for modals: "I think you should get at least..." / "After 5 years, a company ought to..." Push: "Is there a number of years after which a layoff by email becomes categorically unacceptable?" Get them to commit to a position.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is there a "right" way to lay someone off? What would a dignified, respectful layoff actually look like — step by step?
Model: "I think a respectful layoff means a private meeting — in person if possible — with a direct explanation of why. Not 'business conditions,' but actual reasons. Give them time to ask questions. Let them keep their dignity." Push: "Is that realistic when you're letting 5,000 people go at once?" Target: would + conditional for prescriptive positions.
Q5
Do you think companies that do mass layoffs by email face real long-term consequences — or do people forget quickly and apply there again in 2 years?
The honest answer: most companies don't face serious long-term consequences for this. Talent markets are tight. People reapply. Push: "Should we as workers do something different? Or is this just how it works?" Target: modals for obligation + present perfect for patterns.
Q6
Is Javier right — is there a legitimate operational reason to do it by email? Or is that just a convenient justification?
This is the "steel man" question — it asks students to defend a position they might not personally hold. Push: "Even if he's logistically right, does that make it ethical?" Target: distinguish between legal, practical, and ethical — three different standards. Rich vocabulary opportunity.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If you were a senior manager and had to lay off 3 people from your own team — people you know personally — would you do it by email, by call, or in person? And what would you actually say?
Target: second conditional + specific language. Push: "Say it out loud — in English — what are the first words you'd actually use?" Role-play if the student is willing. This is the kinesthetic pivot of the session — from abstract opinion to real language production. Key phrases to activate: "I wanted to speak with you directly", "This wasn't about your performance", "I'm sorry this is happening."
Q8
If you knew your company was planning a big round of layoffs, would you start looking for another job immediately — even if your position felt safe? At what point do you stop trusting a company?
Target: second conditional + modals. Model: "If I knew layoffs were coming, I'd definitely update my LinkedIn and have a few conversations — just to keep my options open. I wouldn't wait to feel the impact." Push: "Is that loyalty or pragmatism? Is there a difference?"
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Is employee loyalty dead — or did companies kill it? And does it matter anymore in a world where everyone is always looking for the next thing anyway?
Big picture question — no single answer. Push both directions: "If loyalty is dead, what replaces it as the foundation of a good work relationship?" / "Or do you think there's still a generation of workers — maybe your parents' — who genuinely felt loyal and got burned?" Target: present perfect + present simple for patterns and beliefs.
Q10
Uncomfortable question: if your company offered you a significant raise to be the person who has to write and send those layoff emails — would you take it?
This is the moral accountability question. It forces the student to stop judging companies abstractly and consider their own participation. There's no right answer — but the reasoning matters. Target: second conditional + conditional clause with because/unless/even if. The student who says "never" and the student who says "depends on the raise" both have something real to say.

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One Final Dilemma
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
You've just been offered your dream job — 40% more salary, better title, a company you genuinely admire. The only condition: you have to start in 2 weeks. Your current company has been good to you and your team is in the middle of a critical project that won't be finished for 3 months. Your resignation will put 4 colleagues under enormous pressure. Do you take the job — and how do you tell your team?
Homework Loop: "Record a 60-second WhatsApp voice message. Tell me: do you take the job? And give me the first 3 sentences you'd say to your team — in English. Not a script. Your actual words."
