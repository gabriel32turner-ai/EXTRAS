Modern Dating: Apps vs. Real Life
Swipe right on the algorithm, swipe left on human connection — or are we overthinking a tool that actually works?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One stat. Let them react before they intellectualize.
⏱ 4 min
Pew Research Center · 2023
"53% of Americans say dating apps have had a mostly negative effect on dating and relationships. Yet 3 in 10 adults have used one — and 12% found a committed relationship or marriage through them."
Despite widespread criticism, dating app use continues to grow globally — including in Latin America, where Tinder and Bumble have seen double-digit growth year-over-year. The disconnect between what people say about apps and what they actually do with them is one of the most interesting contradictions in modern relationships.
Teacher Script Put the two numbers up: 53% hate it, 12% found love through it. Then ask: "Which number surprises you more?" Then: "Quick — how many of you have used a dating app? Or know someone who has?" You don't need them to raise their hands — just let them answer. The goal is to make it feel like a normal conversation, not a taboo topic.

Let the class decide if that's brave honesty or just arrogance.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Go wherever the conversation goes — just keep it in English.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever used a dating app — or do you know someone who has? What was that experience actually like?
Teacher Tip This is the opener — make it safe. "Or someone you know" gives people an exit if they don't want to share personally. But most B2.1 adults have a story. Listen for vocabulary opportunities: ghosting, chemistry, red flag, catfishing.
Q2
How do you typically meet new people in your life — at work, through friends, by chance? When was the last time you met someone new in a situation that surprised you?
Teacher Tip Great for past tense and storytelling. Push for specifics — where were they? What happened? This creates natural contrast with the "app vs real life" debate. Some students will realize they haven't met new people organically in years.
Q3
Have you ever clicked instantly with someone — in any context? What made that connection feel different from other interactions?
Teacher Tip This is about chemistry — that ineffable quality apps can't replicate. Get them to describe the actual feeling: what did they notice? What did the other person do? This builds toward the "what apps can't replace" thread of the conversation.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is it possible to really know someone through a profile and text messages — before you've ever been in the same room? Why or why not?
Teacher Tip Push for specifics: what can you know? What can't you know? Interesting vocabulary: idealize, vulnerability, attachment style. The best students will recognize that pre-meeting communication can both help and hurt — you build expectations before reality can correct them.
Q5
Apps let you filter by height, age, job, even political views. Is that more efficient — or does it eliminate the best surprises life has to offer?
Teacher Tip This is a genuine tension. Efficiency is valuable — but many real relationships started because someone broke a "rule" the other person didn't know they had. Great for the "filter out" and "compatibility" vocabulary. Ask: "What would you filter out — and have you ever dated someone who would have been filtered?"
Q6
Do you think ghosting is worse on apps than in real life — or is it the same behavior with a different name?
Teacher Tip Interesting ethical angle. Apps lower the cost of disappearing — you never have to face the other person. But ghosting existed before apps. Push them: "Have you ever been ghosted? Have you ever ghosted someone?" The honesty is the interesting part.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If dating apps completely disappeared tomorrow, how would your generation find partners? Would that actually be better or worse?
Teacher Tip Second conditional: "If apps disappeared, people would…" Push them to think practically: where would they go? What social infrastructure has been lost? This often leads to a surprising realization that we've stopped building real-life social habits.
Q8
In 10 years, will AI-powered matchmaking replace dating apps — or even replace the early stages of dating altogether? Would you use it?
Teacher Tip Will + prediction + conditional. AI dating is already being built — apps that send messages on your behalf, AI companions, etc. Push them: where's the line? Would they use an AI to screen dates? Would they be okay knowing the other person did? Great for the "algorithm" vocabulary.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Have apps made people's standards unrealistically high — or have they just given people the courage to finally ask for what they actually want?
Teacher Tip This is a real debate. "Paradox of choice" research shows that more options often leads to less satisfaction. But the counter-argument is that people — especially women — historically settled for less because they had fewer options. Both arguments are defensible. Don't resolve it — let it sit.
Q10 · Self-Reflection
Honestly — when you think about what you look for in a partner, how much of that list is based on who you actually are, and how much is based on who you think you should be with?
Teacher Tip The deepest question of the session — and the one most connected to the apps debate. Apps make the list explicit and public, which forces a kind of honesty about our criteria that real life doesn't. This question is about self-awareness, not just dating. Give them time. Don't rush.
💣
Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. No right answer. Record your response.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · B2.1 Modern Dating
Someone you've been talking to on an app for 3 weeks — great conversations, real connection, shared values — tells you they've been using AI to help write their messages. The feelings are real. The words weren't entirely theirs. Do you keep going?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. Is this cheating — or just using the tools available? And be honest: have you ever been more articulate in writing than in person? Is this really so different?
Retention Hook This exit shot works because it's already happening — AI writing assistants in dating apps are a real and growing phenomenon. It connects the whole session (apps, authenticity, connection) into one concrete personal decision. End with: "Send me your voice note. I want to hear your argument — not ChatGPT's."
