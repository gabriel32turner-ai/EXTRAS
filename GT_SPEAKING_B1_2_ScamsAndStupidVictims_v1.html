Scams and
"Stupid" Victims
Someone loses $50,000 pesos to a phone scam. We say: "How could they fall for that?" But could it happen to you?

The Trigger
Phase 1 · Provocation & Opening
⏱ 4 min
Forbes · 2024
"Scam victims in Mexico lost an average of $47,000 pesos in 2023 — and 68% of them said they felt more ashamed than angry. Most never told their family."
Teacher script: Read it. Then ask: "Do you know anyone who has been scammed? What happened?" Wait. Then add: "Why do you think the victim felt more ashamed than angry? Does that seem fair to you?" This question opens the real conversation immediately.

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever received a scam message, call, or email? What happened — and how did you know it was fake?
Teacher Tip Everyone has a story. Push for the specific detail: "What did they say exactly? What made you suspicious? Was there a moment when you almost believed it?"
Q2
Have you ever known someone personally who was scammed and lost money? How did they feel — and how did the people around them react?
Model Answer "My uncle lost $30,000 pesos. Someone called saying he had won a car and just needed to pay a small fee first. He was embarrassed for months. My family didn't say much — but I could see they thought he should have known better. That was hard to watch."
Q3
Have you ever seen a scam story online or on the news and thought: "I would never fall for that"? Be honest — why did you think that?
Teacher Tip This is the overconfidence question. Most people will admit they've thought this. "And after today's class — do you still think that? What would it take to actually trick you?"
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Do you think it is fair to say a scam victim was "stupid"? Where does responsibility begin — with the criminal or with the victim?
Teacher Tip Push them to commit to a position. "Is the victim ever responsible at all? Is it 100% the criminal's fault? Or is it somewhere in between — and if so, what percentage?" Numbers force clarity.
Q5
In your opinion, why do so many scam victims feel ashamed instead of angry? Is that a normal reaction?
Model Answer "I think it's because we live in a society that values being smart. If you were scammed, you feel like you failed some kind of intelligence test. But that's completely wrong. Scammers are professionals. Feeling ashamed is the wrong emotion — but I understand why people feel it."
Q6
Should banks and companies do more to protect their customers from scams? Or is it the customer's personal responsibility?
Teacher Tip Great for pushing the collective vs. individual responsibility debate at a B1.2 level. "What specifically should banks do? Call you? Block suspicious transfers? Or would that be too controlling?"
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if you received a call right now saying your son or daughter was in a serious accident and needed $15,000 pesos immediately — or they couldn't receive treatment?
Teacher Tip The most powerful question in the session. Take your time with it. "In that exact moment of fear — would you call back first? Would you stop to verify? Or would you just send?" Most people admit they would feel the urge to act immediately.
Q8
What would you do if you found out a family member sent $50,000 pesos to a scammer — and the money is gone? How would you talk to them about it?
Model Answer "I hope I would be kind first and practical second. I would not say 'How could you do this?' — that helps nobody. I would say 'OK, what do we do now?' and then later, when they feel better, maybe talk about what to watch for next time."
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
If you were scammed and lost $40,000 pesos, would you tell your family and friends — or would you keep it secret? Why?
Teacher Tip The shame question turned personal. "What would stop you from telling people? Fear of judgment? Pride? Or something else?" This is where the most honest answers of the session come out.
Q10
Mateo from the Opinion Wall said: "The smarter you think you are, the easier you are to scam." Do you agree — and does that idea scare you a little?
Model Answer "Yes — and it bothers me. I always thought I was too smart for scams. But now I think my confidence is exactly the thing that would make me vulnerable. A scammer who knows you think you're smart will use that. That's a very uncomfortable idea."

The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · Final Dilemma · No right answer
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot
Your 68-year-old mother calls you crying. She says she sent $60,000 pesos — all her savings — to someone who told her you were in a car accident and needed surgery. She found out it was a scam an hour later. The money is gone. She says: "Please don't tell your father. I am so ashamed." What do you do — and what do you say to her right now, in this moment?
📱 WhatsApp Homework Record 60 seconds in English: What do you say to your mother? And do you tell your father — yes or no?
