Phone-Free
Restaurants
Some restaurants are banning phones at the table. Is this a brilliant idea — or a business committing social suicide?
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
A real headline. A real debate.
⏱ 4 min
The New York Times · 2024
"Restaurants across the U.S. are experimenting with phone-free dining rooms. Some report the best reviews they've ever received. Others report walkouts at the door. The same policy is dividing customers straight down the middle."
Teacher Script
"Imagine you sit down at a restaurant and the server says: 'We ask all guests to put their phones away for the duration of your meal.' What's your first reaction — relief, annoyance, or something in between? Be honest. That reaction tells us everything about today's topic."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Real talk. No right answers.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
At your last family dinner or dinner with friends, how many times did someone check their phone? Did it bother you — or were you the one doing it?
Force the honest answer. "Everyone else was on their phone" is the easy answer. Push: "What about you specifically?" The self-awareness gap is the most interesting part of this topic.
Q2
Have you ever been in a conversation where the other person was clearly half-listening because of their phone? How did it feel?
Almost everyone has. Great vocabulary activation: "dismissed," "invisible," "disrespected." Ask: "Did you say something — or just let it go?" The letting-it-go is socially interesting.
Q3
Have you ever tried to do a phone-free dinner voluntarily — even for an hour? What happened?
Most people haven't. If they have: what was the result? If they haven't: "Why not?" — is it logistical, social (fear of what others will think), or just never occurred to them?
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Does a restaurant have the right to ban phones on its premises — the same way it bans smoking or requires shoes? Where does a business's authority end?
Model answer: "A business can set any rule it wants — as long as it's communicated clearly before you sit down. The issue isn't legality, it's trust. If I know the rule and still choose to go, that's on me."
Q5
Is banning phones at a restaurant actually a class thing — something only upscale places can afford to do? Would it work at a family restaurant in a Mexican city?
Sharp cultural angle. The phone-free restaurant trend is mostly high-end. Would it work in Celaya, Querétaro, Monterrey? Would your parents follow the rule — or walk out? Great local anchoring moment.
Q6
Tomás says we were already bad at being present before phones existed. Is he right — or is the phone a genuinely new level of problem?
The most intellectually rich question. Push them to distinguish between "distraction always existed" and "this distraction is uniquely designed by engineers to be irresistible." That difference matters.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
If your favorite restaurant announced a phone-free policy starting next month, would you stop going — or would you try it at least once?
Force a real decision. "I'd try it once" is the interesting answer — because it reveals they're curious but not committed. Follow up: "And if the meal was incredible — would you go back?"
Q8
If you had young kids at home and the restaurant banned screens entirely — no phones, no tablets — would that be a deal-breaker for you?
Practical objection with real stakes. Great for parents in the group. The tablet-as-babysitter dynamic is real and worth discussing without judgment. Ask: "What did parents do before screens at restaurants?"
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
If your dinner companions are boring you — is your phone actually the problem, or is it just the symptom of a deeper issue with the people you're choosing to eat with?
The sharpest question in the lab. A phone-free rule doesn't fix a bad relationship or a forced work dinner. It might even make it worse. Let the discomfort land. Don't rush to soften it.
Q10
Be completely honest: when you're at dinner with someone, how much of what they're saying do you actually absorb — even when your phone is away?
The closer — about attention, not technology. The phone is a convenient villain. But mental distraction, planning what to say next, zoning out — those don't need a device. This question opens the door to a bigger conversation about human connection.
