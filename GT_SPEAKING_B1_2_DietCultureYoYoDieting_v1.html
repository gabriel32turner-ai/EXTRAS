§ 01 · The Trigger
🪝 The Industry That Profits From Failure
Read aloud or project · First reaction only · No debate yet · 4 min max
01
4 min
⚠️ Sensitivity Note — Read Before Starting Food, weight, and body image can be personal topics. Keep the conversation at the level of habits, culture, and industry — not personal appearance or numbers. Never comment on a student's body. If someone shares something vulnerable, acknowledge the honesty and redirect to the language immediately.
🥗
Global Wellness Institute + Journal of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics 2023
$254B
Global diet industry revenue in 2023 — one of the few industries that profits directly from its customers failing repeatedly
95%
Of people who lose weight through restrictive dieting regain it within 1–5 years — most gain back more than they lost
4 in 5
Women and nearly 2 in 3 men say they have tried at least one diet in the last 3 years — most tried more than three
$60B
Spent globally on weight-loss supplements annually — the majority with no clinical evidence of effectiveness
Sources: Global Wellness Institute 2023 · Journal of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics · McKinsey Health Institute · FTC Weight Loss Advertising Study
🪝 Opening Trigger — Read this, then point at someone
"The diet industry makes more money when its products fail than when they succeed. If everyone lost weight and kept it off, the industry would collapse. Is that an accident — or is it the business model?"
Teacher Mechanics — 4 min Read the statement once, slowly. The phrase "is that an accident — or is it the business model?" is the hook. Point at someone: "First reaction — what do you think?" No judgment. 2–3 reactions max. Say: "Hold everything. We come back to this in §4."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The 95% failure rate of restrictive diets is the most provocative stat — it reframes personal failure as a structural outcome. That reframe is the pedagogical engine of the session.
  • The $254B revenue + failure correlation is the cognitive dissonance to activate: the industry is enormous AND its products mostly fail AND people keep buying. Why? That's Q5 and Q6 in Block 2.
  • If nobody reacts: "Has anyone here ever done a diet — lost weight, then gained it back? Raise your hand. Now keep your hand up if it happened more than once." The second ask always gets a different response.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • This topic is personally universal — food, weight, and diets are present in almost every family and workplace. The emotional activation is immediate and doesn't require much push.
  • Frame it early: "This is not about judging how anyone eats or looks. It's about the industry, the culture, and the habits — and why the cycle is so hard to break."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Retention: "This topic connects directly to how corporate wellness programs are designed — and how they often fail for the same reasons personal diets fail. That's a real professional conversation."

🔵 10 Verbs — What People Do With Food and Diets
⚡ Production Challenge — No preparation
Each person picks 3 words. One sentence each about your real relationship with food or diets — honest. No writing. Just talk.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • "Yo-yo dieting" — explain if needed: losing weight, gaining it back, losing again, gaining again — the up-down cycle. The image of a yo-yo is the key. Most students know this pattern even if they don't know the English term.
  • "Cravings" — very common experience, often under-expressed in English. Activate: "What food do you have cravings for most often? Say it in English." Immediate and engaging.
  • Best production challenge: "Which 3 words describe your eating habits during a stressful week at work?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "These words appear in corporate wellness programs, HR surveys, and conversations with international colleagues about health benefits. You'll need them."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "Knowing how to talk about nutrition and wellness in English is increasingly important in multinational companies — especially when US or European headquarters design health programs that affect Mexican teams."

🔁 Processing Question — Pairs → Group
"Who do you agree with most — and who sounds most like someone you know?"
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Read all 4 before opening. The contrast between Gabriela (dieting survivor, now at peace) and Paula (chronic dieter who keeps trying) is the emotional core. Students will recognize people they know — or themselves — in both.
  • The provocateur is Dr. Salinas. His position — that yo-yo dieting is more dangerous than stable overweight — is medically documented but socially counterintuitive. Push: "He says staying at your current weight might be healthier than losing and gaining repeatedly. Does that change how you think about dieting?"
  • Javier's voice (food as culture and pleasure, not enemy) is the most warmly received by Mexican students — food as family, celebration, and identity. Let that resonance happen.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • Best activation: "Quick question before we read — how many of you have a family member who is always on a diet? Keep your hand up if it's been the same diet more than once." Always generates recognition and a laugh.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Testimonial trigger: "One sentence — which of these four people describes your honest relationship with food right now?"
§ 04 · The Conversation Engine
🗣 10 Questions. 4 Blocks. 23 Minutes.
Personal → Opinion → Hypothetical → Provocative · No round-robin · Point and push
04
23 min
⚡
Rule: Minimum 2 sentences per answer. Position first. Real example second. No generic answers — connect it to your life.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Block 1 present perfect — watch for "I have try" or "I have went on a diet." Correct once: "So you have tried a diet — tell me about it."
  • Q9 is the most personal — give it full silence. Students who have struggled with yo-yo dieting may need extra space. Don't rush.
  • Block 3 second conditional: watch for "If I would eat" — note and recycle at end.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • When "craving," "restrict," or "guilt" appear naturally — point it out: "You just used a word from today's list without thinking. That's the goal."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • After Q10: "You just talked about the food industry, personal habits, and cultural identity — in English. That's not a beginner conversation. That's B1.2."
  • Upsell: "Next Speaking Lab: The Burnout Generation — when hard work stops being a virtue and starts being a trap."
§ 05 · The Exit Shot
💣 One Question. No Easy Answer.
5 min · No chips · Just position + reason · Then the loop
05
5 min
EXIT
💣 Exit Shot
Your doctor tells you: "Your weight is affecting your health. You need to make a change." You have tried 4 different diets in the last 6 years. You lost weight each time and gained it back. You know what to eat — the problem is consistency. What do you actually do this time that is different?
Answer in 3 sentences. Be specific and honest — not motivational. What would you actually change, and why haven't you changed it before?
📌 B1.2 Model Answer — Reveal AFTER student speaks
"I would stop calling it a diet. Every time I've called it a diet, it had an end date — and when it ended, I went back to everything before. This time I would change one habit — just one — and keep it for six months before changing anything else. I've always tried to change everything at once. That's why it never sticks."
🔁 Retention Loop — The WhatsApp Homework "Before our next session — record a 60-second voice note in English. Your answer to today's Exit Shot. Don't write it first. Don't rehearse. Just talk. Send it to [teacher number]."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The scenario removes the "I don't know what to eat" excuse — the doctor already confirmed there's a problem, and the student has tried before. The only question left is: what makes this time different? That precision forces real reflection.
  • Follow-up only: "What would you do differently if you had a friend doing the same change at the same time?" Second conditional activates naturally.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The model answer introduces a key insight — "every time I called it a diet, it had an end date." That framing is worth discussing: "What's the difference between a diet and a habit? One sentence."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Closing: "You just described a real pattern of behavior — and diagnosed why it fails — in English. That's not translation. That's thinking."
