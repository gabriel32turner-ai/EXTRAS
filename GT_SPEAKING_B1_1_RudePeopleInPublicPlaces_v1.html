Rude People in Public Places

Someone is rude to you on the street, in a line, or on the bus. Do you say something — or do you just stay quiet?

🪝
The Trigger
Open the Conversation
⏱ 4 min
BBC — Social Behavior Study, 2023
"68% of people say they see rude behavior in public every week. But only 1 in 5 people say anything. Most people prefer to stay quiet and avoid conflict — even when they are angry."
🎙 Teacher Script

Read the stat, then ask immediately: "Are you in the 1 in 5 — or do you usually stay quiet?" Go around the room fast. Don't analyze yet — just collect answers. Then ask: "Tell me about the last time someone was rude to you in public. What happened?" Stories start flowing right away with this topic.

The Conversation Engine
23 Minutes of Real Talk
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever had a bad experience with a rude person in public? What happened?
Every student has a story. Let them tell it with detail — where, what happened, how they felt. The storytelling itself is the speaking practice. Don't interrupt for corrections here — let it flow.
Q2
Have you ever said something to a rude stranger — or did you stay quiet? How did you feel after?
Model answer: "I stayed quiet. I was so angry in the moment I couldn't think. But later I felt bad — I wished I had said something. Like I let them win."
Q3
Have you ever seen someone else be rude to another person in public? Did you do anything?
The bystander question. Most people do nothing — and feel guilty. Valeria's principle applies here: "Was it a stranger or a vulnerable person?" Let them reflect on when they would or wouldn't intervene.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Is it better to say something to a rude person — or ignore them? What do you usually do?
The core debate. Carolina vs. Mateo. Let students defend their position — and challenge each other. There is no right answer and students usually know it, which makes the discussion more honest.
Q5
Do you think people are more rude today than before? Why do you think that?
Don Roberto's question in conversation form. Common answers: phones, stress, less community, COVID. No wrong answers — push for their real personal opinion, not the "safe" answer.
Q6
Is being rude sometimes OK? For example, if you are very tired, very stressed, or if someone did something really wrong first?
The empathy question. Most people have been rude once and know it. This allows students to be honest without judging others too harshly. Model answer: "I think we all have a bad day sometimes. But there is a difference between being a little short with someone and being really disrespectful."
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if someone cut in front of you in a long line at the supermarket?
Very concrete and relatable — everyone has been in this situation. Encourage specific answers: "Would you say something directly? What exactly would you say?" Role-play the interaction briefly if the group is ready.
Q8
What would you do if a waiter in a restaurant was very rude to you in front of your family?
Model answer: "I would speak to the manager, not the waiter. I don't want to argue in front of my family. But I wouldn't leave a tip and I would never go back."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Have you ever been rude to someone in public — maybe without wanting to? Be honest.
The self-reflection question. Be warm here — normalize it. Almost everyone has snapped at a waiter or been short with a stranger. The honest admission is what makes this question valuable.
Q10
If someone is rude to you and you say nothing, are you being polite — or are you just afraid?
The uncomfortable closer. Push them to be honest about the difference. "Polite" and "afraid" often look exactly the same from the outside — but feel very different inside.

The Exit Shot
One Question. No Easy Answer.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Final Dilemma
You are on the bus. A man is very rude to an old woman — he shouts at her because she was slow. Nobody says anything. You are nervous, but you feel it's wrong. You have 5 seconds to decide: do you say something — or do you stay quiet like everyone else?
