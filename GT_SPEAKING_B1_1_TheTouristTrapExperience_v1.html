§ 01 · The Trigger

Sources: TripAdvisor Traveler Insights 2023 · Which? Holiday Scams Report · Booking.com Hidden Charges Study
🪝 Opening Question — Read this, then point at someone
"You spend 600 pesos on a restaurant near a famous attraction. The food is bad. The service is slow. The waiter brings you extra things you didn't order and adds them to the bill. What do you do?"
Teacher Mechanics — 4 min Read with a slightly frustrated tone. Let "600 pesos" land — it's real money. Point at one student: "What do you do — right now, with that bill in your hand?" 2–3 reactions. "Everything you said comes back in §4."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • Tourist traps happen in Mexico too — Cancún, Cabo, Teotihuacán, the beach towns. Students who have traveled domestically will have their own versions of this story. Use it.
  • The 3× price stat is the most immediately useful — it reframes avoiding tourist traps as practical intelligence, not just cultural snobbery.
  • If nobody has traveled: "Has anyone been to a restaurant or attraction in Mexico — Cancún, Tulum, Guadalajara — where they felt cheated? Same thing."
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The "extra things on the bill" detail is a real and specific scam that generates immediate recognition and anger. Let the emotion fuel the session.
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Retention: "This is the last of our three Travel labs. Next lote we move to a completely different topic — but same honesty, same depth."

⚡ Production Challenge — No preparation
Pick 3 words. One sentence each about a time you paid for something that wasn't worth it — anywhere, not just travel. No writing. Just talk.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • "Scam" vs "tourist trap" — clarify: a scam is intentional deception (illegal); a tourist trap is legal but deliberately overpriced for tourists (not illegal, but disappointing). Both happen to visitors.
  • "Complain" at B1.1 — model the distinction: complain = say something is wrong (to the waiter, to the hotel); leave a review = write about it later. Both are valid responses.
  • Best production trigger: "Which 3 words describe the last time you felt cheated — anywhere, not just on a trip?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • "These words will help you write a real complaint in English — to a hotel, to TripAdvisor, to a customer service desk. Real useful skills."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • "In any international travel or business context, knowing how to complain professionally in English is a serious skill. Today you're building the vocabulary for that."

§ 04 · The Conversation Engine
🗣 10 Questions. 4 Blocks. 23 Minutes.
Personal → Opinion → Hypothetical → Provocative · Point and push · No round-robin
04
23 min
⚡
Rule: At least 2 sentences. Say what happened. Say what you did — or didn't do. And say how much it cost. The details make the story.
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The conv-bar rule adds "how much it cost" — this forces students to use numbers and specific language, which is great for B1.1 vocabulary production.
  • Q9 is the most self-revealing question — students rarely admit they let it go without saying anything. The honesty is the vocabulary moment.
  • Block 3: second conditional. Watch for "If I would see" — note and recycle at end only.
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • When a student tells a tourist trap story with specific amounts: celebrate it — "You used a number! '800 pesos for a beer' — that's a sentence that tells the whole story. That's English."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • After Q10: "You just talked about money, expectations, and anger — in English — with real stories and real feelings. That's communication. Not translation."
§ 05 · The Exit Shot
💣 One Question. No Easy Answer.
5 min · No chips · Position + reason · Then the loop
05
5 min
EXIT
💣 Exit Shot
You are at a restaurant in a tourist area. The waiter brings the bill — it is double what the menu said. He says it was a "service charge" and looks at you. You have your partner and your kids with you. Everyone is waiting. Do you pay — or do you argue?
3 sentences minimum. Pay or argue — first. Then tell me what you really feel in that moment, with your family watching.
📌 B1.1 Model Answer — Reveal AFTER student speaks
"I ask for the menu and I point to the price. Calmly. I say: 'This is not what the menu says.' If he still insists, I pay — but I take a photo of the bill and the menu first. Then I leave a review on TripAdvisor that night. I don't make a scene in front of my kids, but I don't just accept it either."
🔁 Retention Loop — WhatsApp Homework "Before next session — record a 60-second voice note in English. Your answer to today's Exit Shot. No preparation. Just talk. Send it to [teacher number]."
Capa 1 · Pedagogical
  • The "family watching" detail adds social pressure — it's not just a personal decision, it's a moment of modeling behavior for your children. That pressure generates the most honest answers.
  • Follow-up: "What would you say to your kids after — to explain what happened?"
Capa 2 · Engagement
  • The model answer introduces a practical B1.1 strategy: photo + calm words + TripAdvisor. Point out: "That is a complete English plan for a real problem. Not just grammar — a strategy."
Capa 3 · Growth & Retention
  • Closing: "You just navigated a real travel conflict — in English — with calm language and a plan. That's the goal of this entire Travel series."
  • Referral trigger: "This is the kind of conversation that most English classes never have. Your colleagues who are taking grammar courses have never practiced this. That's the difference."
