Ozempic and Easy Weight Loss

A drug designed for diabetes is now a celebrity shortcut for weight loss. Is that a medical breakthrough — or are we cheating our way to health?

🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
⏱ 4 min
"Ozempic has created a global shortage of a life-saving diabetes medication — because wealthy people are using it for cosmetic weight loss. Novo Nordisk, the manufacturer, became Europe's most valuable company in 2023. Diabetic patients in several countries are being rationed their medication."
— The Guardian / Bloomberg, 2023
Teacher script: "Before we start — raise your hand if you've heard of Ozempic." [Pause.] "Now — do you know anyone who's taken it? Or wanted to?" [Pause.] "Here's the twist: it was originally made for type 2 diabetes patients. Let's talk about what happened when Hollywood found out about it."

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever tried to lose weight — or been around someone who did? How long did it take, and did it last?
Very personal but universal. Most Mexican adults have experience with this topic — either personally or through family. Listen for present perfect + past simple contrast: "I've tried... but it didn't work."
Q2
Have you ever felt pressure to look a certain way at work, in social situations, or because of social media?
Surfaces the real driver behind Ozempic demand: social pressure, not health. Follow up: "Where does that pressure come from most — Instagram, your company, your family?"
Q3
Do you know anyone who has tried Ozempic or a similar medication? What was your reaction when you found out?
Very current in Mexico — Ozempic is widely discussed. If they say "no," ask: "Would you tell anyone if you were using it?" That follow-up is often more honest.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Is using Ozempic for weight loss "cheating" — or is that an unfair judgment of people who struggle with their weight?
The "cheating" framing is the core moral debate. Push them: "Do you judge people who take medication for anxiety? For high blood pressure? Why is weight different?" Very generative.
Q5
Should access to Ozempic be restricted only to people with a clinical diagnosis — or should anyone who wants it be able to use it?
Regulation debate. "Should it be restricted?" triggers modals: "It should / shouldn't be available to..." Connects to the shortage crisis and fairness issue.
Q6
Do you think weight discrimination is a real problem at work in Mexico? Could that explain why people feel desperate about their weight?
Daniel's provocateur point. Many professionals have seen or experienced this. "Discrimination" is a strong word — some students will push back, others will have direct experiences to share.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
If Ozempic were affordable, safe, and available to everyone, would you take it to lose weight — even if you didn't "need" to medically?
Second conditional — clean and honest. "If it were safe... I would / wouldn't..." The honesty often surprises students themselves. No wrong answer; the goal is real reflection.
Q8
If you found out a colleague got a promotion partly because they lost 20 kilos and "looked more professional," how would you react?
Connects the weight discussion directly to professional life. "I would feel..." triggers conditional naturally. This scenario is not far-fetched and most students will have an instinct about it.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Is it ethical for wealthy people to use a drug that creates shortages for diabetic patients who actually need it for survival?
This is the hardest moral edge of the topic. "Ethical" is the key word — push them to define it. Many students haven't considered the shortage angle. This is where the conversation gets real.
Q10
If you could take a pill that made you look exactly how you wanted — but you had to take it every day for the rest of your life and stop eating the foods you love — would you?
The real Ozempic deal — the weight comes back when you stop. This question forces students to weigh appearance vs quality of life. Designed to leave them thinking after class. Let the silence land.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
Your doctor offers you Ozempic. You are not diabetic. You are not clinically obese. But you have gained 18 kilos since your 30s and you feel bad about it. The injection costs $400 a month. You can afford it — but just barely. Do you take it?
