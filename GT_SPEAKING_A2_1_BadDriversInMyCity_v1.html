Bad Drivers in My City

Every city has them. They go too fast, they don't stop, they cut in front of you. What do YOU do?

🪝
The Trigger
Open the Conversation
⏱ 4 min
A question for the class
"This morning, on your way here — did you see a bad driver? What did they do?"
🎙 Teacher Script

Ask the question and point at one student immediately. Don't wait — go around fast. Just the story, no analysis: "What did the driver do?" Let 2 or 3 students share. Then say: "OK — bad drivers. Let's talk." This topic activates everyone instantly because it happened to them today or yesterday.

The Conversation Engine
23 Minutes of Real Talk
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
What did the worst driver you ever saw do? Tell me the story.
Let them tell it in past simple — "He went...", "She didn't stop...", "I was very angry." Help sequence: First... then... after that. Don't correct mid-story. Note errors and recycle later.
Q2
Did a bad driver make you late for work or an important appointment? What happened?
Model answer: "Yes! Last Tuesday. A truck blocked the road for 20 minutes. I called my boss. I was very angry. The driver didn't care." Short sentences, past simple, real emotion.
Q3
Did you ever honk at another driver or yell from your car? What did you say?
Fun self-reflection question. Most students will laugh and admit they did. Model answer: "Yes, one time. A man cut in front of me. I honked three times. He ignored me. I was so angry." The laugh creates connection and lowers anxiety.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
What is the worst thing a driver can do? What is more dangerous — going too fast or using your phone?
A vs. B debate — easy to answer at A2.1. Push: "Why is that more dangerous?" Help them build a short reason: "Because when you..." Most students choose phone — it's personal and they know they do it.
Q5
Do you think bad drivers need more classes — or bigger punishments?
Two clear options. Model answer: "I think bigger punishments. Classes are not enough. People need to feel consequences. In my city, nobody follows the rules because nothing happens to them."
Q6
Do you think people in your city drive better or worse than in other cities?
Opens up personal comparison. Many students have a strong opinion about CDMX vs. their city, or about drivers in the US or Europe. Simple: "In my city, drivers are... because..." Great for present simple practice.
Block 3 — Future Plans
Q7
The next time a bad driver does something dangerous, what are you going to do?
Direct "going to" practice. Help students be specific: "I'm going to..." Options: ignore, honk, call the police, take a photo of the plates. Fun to compare who takes which approach.
Q8
Are you going to drive differently after today's class — or do you think you are already a good driver?
Fun self-reflection closer for Block 3. Many students laugh — it's personal. Model answer: "I'm going to use my phone less. I know it's bad. But I'm not going to change my speed. I drive fast but safely!"
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — do YOU do anything bad when you drive? Use your phone? Go too fast? Cut in sometimes?
Carolina's question — most students will admit something. Celebrate the honesty. Model answer: "OK... I use my phone at red lights. I know it's wrong. But only for 2 seconds!" The confession creates group energy and real conversation.
Q10
A bad driver cuts in front of you and almost hits your car. You are very angry. The driver looks at you and smiles. What do you do?
Scenario question — vivid and specific. Let students be dramatic. Model answer: "I honk three times. I don't get out of the car — that's dangerous. But I am very angry for the rest of the day." Present simple + past simple combo works perfectly here.

The Exit Shot
One Situation. Your Decision.
⏱ 5 min
🎯 Exit Shot
A driver parks in front of your driveway. You can't get your car out. You are going to be late for work. You know the driver — it's your neighbor. What are you going to do?
🎙️
Voice Note Homework
Record a 60-second voice note in English on WhatsApp. What are you going to do with your neighbor? Talk to them? Call the police? Be specific. Your teacher will listen and give you feedback before the next session.
