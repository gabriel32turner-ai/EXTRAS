Pets vs. Kids:
Different Love
Some people love their dog like a child. Some people say that's crazy. Today we find out which side you're on.
A2.2Daily Life & Lifestyle🟡 Medium50 min

🪝 Phase 1 — The Trigger
The Question
Read it. Let the student react. Then start talking.
4 min
01
A fun fact about pets
"In Mexico, more than 60% of homes have a pet. Many people say their pet is 'part of the family' — some say their pet is their best friend."
— And some people without kids say their pet IS their child. Is that crazy? Or is it completely normal?
The question for today
"Do you think it's normal to love a pet like a child? Or is a child a completely different kind of love?"
— No wrong answer. Just your honest opinion.
Launch line: "Quick question — do you have a pet? Do you have kids? Do you have both?" [Let them answer.] "Perfect. Today we talk about love — for pets and for children. Very different? Or not so different?" This topic always generates immediate reactions at A2.2. Almost everyone has a strong opinion on it.

Provocateur note: Jorge is the "a pet is just a pet" person — the most gently provocative position at A2.2 level. His position is direct, not mean. Ask: "Do you agree with Jorge? Or do you think pets are more than just animals?" Students who have pets will have a strong reaction. Students who don't have pets may agree more with Jorge. Both generate great conversation.

Inclusion note: If the student has neither pets nor children, ask: "Which person do you understand the most?" and "Would you like to have a pet? Or children? Or both?"
🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Short questions. Describe your experience. Use simple sentences.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience (Past Simple)
Q1
Did you have a pet when you were a child? What animal was it? Tell me about it.
Past simple — childhood pet memory. Very warm topic. Model: "Yes, I had a dog. His name was Max. He was brown and very big. I played with him every day after school. He died when I was 15. I was very sad." Keep sentences simple. Correct tense gently if needed.
Q2
When was the last time you saw an animal you really liked? What happened?
Past simple — recent animal encounter. Fun and easy. Model: "Last week I saw a dog on the street. A little white dog. Very cute! I stopped and said hello to the dog. The owner laughed." Activate "Oh my God, so cute!" naturally. Short answer is fine.
Q3
Did you ever do something special for your pet — or for a child in your family? What did you do?
Past simple — a kind action toward a pet or child. Model: "Last month my cat was sick. I took her to the vet. It was expensive! But I was very worried. She is fine now." Or for a child: "My nephew's birthday was last week. I bought him his favorite toy." Simple and personal.
Block 2 — Opinion (Simple Present)
Q4
Do you think a pet is part of the family? Or is it just an animal in the house?
Simple opinion — Valeria vs. Jorge. Model: "I think a pet is part of the family. They live with you. They love you. They are not just animals." Or: "I think a pet is an animal. I like animals. But family is family — people." Both are valid. Target structure: "I think..."
Q5
Is it good to buy clothes for a pet? Or is that too much?
Fun, light opinion. Jorge's position comes back here. Model: "I think it's too much! Animals don't need clothes. But if someone wants to — okay, it's their pet." Or: "I think it's fun! My dog has a small jacket for winter. He looks very cute." Activate "No way!" and "Are you serious?" reactions.
Q6
What do you think is more difficult — to have a pet or to have children?
Comparison opinion — Carolina's territory. Model: "I think children are more difficult. A pet is simpler. You feed them, you walk them, you love them. Children need more — more time, more money, more everything." Or: "I think pets are also difficult! My cat wakes me up at 4am. Forget about it!" Activate the phrase naturally.
Block 3 — Future Plans (Going to / Will)
Q7
Are you going to get a pet in the future — or do you already have one? What animal do you want?
Going to — future plan about pets. Model: "I'm going to get a dog next year. My apartment is small now. But I'm going to move to a bigger place. Then I'm going to adopt a dog. A big one." Simple and personal. Students who already have pets: "I'm going to get another cat. My cat needs a friend."
Q8
Will your relationship with animals change in the future? Will you love them more or less?
Will — future prediction. Simple and reflective. Model: "I think I will always love animals. I think I will have a pet for the rest of my life. Animals make me happy. That will not change." Or: "I think my feelings will change when I have children. I will probably love them more than a pet." Both are honest.
Block 4 — Past Habits + Reflection (Used to)
Q9
Did you use to think pets were just animals — or did you always love them like family?
Used to — change in attitude toward pets. Model: "I used to think pets were just animals. When I was a child, I liked animals but I didn't think about it much. Then I got my own cat and everything changed. Now she is my family." Natural and personal.
Q10
Did you use to want a different pet when you were young — not the one you have now?
Used to — childhood dream about pets. Light and fun. Model: "When I was a child, I used to want a horse! I loved horses. I thought they were beautiful. My mother said no. We lived in an apartment." Or: "I used to want a rabbit. Now I have two cats. I think cats are better." Gentle and funny closing question.
A2.2 grammar targets: Past simple (Q1-3), simple present opinions (Q4-6), going to / will (Q7-8), used to (Q9-10).

Inclusion note: Not all students have pets or children. Adapt questions naturally: "Do you have a friend with a pet?" / "What about your family's pet?" The vocabulary and grammar are the same — the personal connection just shifts slightly.

Energy note: This topic generates warm energy and usually a lot of laughter. Keep it light and personal. Don't push for complexity — 2-3 sentence answers are excellent at A2.2.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. Simple and personal. Answer in 2-3 sentences.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
If you could have any animal in the world as a pet — and money is not a problem — what animal are you going to choose? And why?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Tell me about a pet you have — or a pet from your family. What is the animal? What is its name? What does it do? And what do you love most about it?" If you don't have a pet: "Tell me about an animal you love and why." Send before our next session.
Why it works: "Going to choose" is the target grammar embedded naturally. "Money is not a problem" opens imagination without complex English. The question is fun, personal, and generates strong reactions — especially when students choose unexpected animals like a tiger, an elephant, or a penguin. Always produces laughter and a memorable moment.

Homework note: Two clear options for students with or without pets. The description task (what does it do? what do you love most?) activates simple present + vocabulary from today. Very doable for A2.2.

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about something completely different — habits that are difficult to break. Very similar energy — personal, honest, a little funny."
