My Last Vacation:
Good or Bad?

The hotel, the food, the trip, the people — everything that happened and how you really feel about it.

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Open the conversation
4 min
A simple fact about vacations
"Studies show that people talk about their bad vacations 4 times more than their good ones. We love to tell the disaster stories."
Teacher script: Read the stat. Then smile and say: "OK. Think about your last vacation. On a scale of 1 to 10 — how good was it? Tell me the number." Go around the room fast. Collect the numbers. Then: "Who gave a low number? Tell me in 2 sentences what went wrong."

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 questions · 4 blocks
23 min
Block 1 — Past Experience
Q1
Where did you go on your last vacation? Tell me 3 things you did there.
Teacher tip: Past simple structures: "I went to..." / "I ate..." / "I visited..." / "I stayed at a hotel..." Push: "What was the best moment? What was the worst moment?"
Q2
Did something go wrong on your last vacation — a problem with the hotel, transport, food, or the weather?
Model answer: "Yes. My flight was 4 hours late. I waited at the airport for a very long time. I was tired and angry. When I arrived at the hotel, they gave my room to another person. It was a terrible start."
Q3
When was your best vacation ever? What did you do and who went with you?
Teacher tip: Let students describe freely in past simple. Push for sensory detail: "What was the weather like? What did you eat? How many days?" The more specific, the better the language production.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
What is more important on a vacation — a good hotel or a good location?
Teacher tip: Force a choice. Model: "I think location is more important. If the place is beautiful, I don't care about the hotel. I can sleep anywhere. But a bad location — no hotel can fix that."
Q5
Do you think expensive vacations are better than cheap vacations?
Model answer: "No, I don't think so. My best vacation was very cheap. I slept in a small hostel and I ate street food. It was fantastic. Expensive means comfortable — but not always happy."
Q6
Is it OK to look at your phone a lot when you are on vacation?
Teacher tip: Everyone has a strong opinion. Push: "Do YOU look at your phone on vacation? How many times per day?" Most will admit they do. That's the honest conversation you want.
Block 3 — Future Plans
Q7
Are you going to take a vacation this year? Where are you going to go?
Teacher tip: Direct "going to" practice. If they have no plans: "OK — where do you WANT to go? Imagine you have the money and the time. Where?"
Q8
Next vacation — are you going to travel with family, with friends, or alone?
Model answer: "I'm going to travel with my partner — without the kids. We never have time alone. This year I'm going to plan a trip for just the two of us. Maybe 4 days. Something simple. But just us."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Be honest — do you ever post photos on social media to make your vacation look better than it really was?
Teacher tip: Most people laugh — that means yes. Don't judge. Ask: "What did you post? What was the reality behind the photo?" Great moment of honesty and humor in the session.
Q10
Your family wants to go on vacation to a place you don't like. What are you going to do — go or stay home?
Teacher tip: Fun scenario. Push with "going to": "Are you going to say something? Are you going to complain the whole trip? Are you going to have a good time anyway?"
💣
The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One final question
5 min
💣 Exit Shot
You booked a hotel online. The photos were beautiful. You paid a lot of money. You arrived — and the room is terrible. Small, dirty, nothing like the photos. Check-out is in 5 days. What are you going to do?

Homework — WhatsApp Voice Note
Record a 60-second voice message in English. Describe your last vacation. Where did you go? What did you do? Was it good or bad? Use past simple: "I went to...", "I ate...", "The hotel was...", "I felt..."
