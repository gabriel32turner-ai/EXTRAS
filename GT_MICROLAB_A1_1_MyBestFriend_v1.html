Phase 4
Question Cards
The teacher answers first. Then it's your turn.
12 MIN
Question 1
What is your best friend's name? Where is he or she from?
Teacher: "My best friend is Laura. She is from Guadalajara. She is 38 years old. She is a teacher — like me! But she is a math teacher."
Now you: "My best friend is _______ . He/She is from..."
Tip: Build a full identity — name, city, age, job. If student says only the name, push: "Where is he/she from? How old?" Every detail is a sentence.
Question 2
Is your best friend similar to you or different?
Teacher: "Laura and I are very different! She is calm, I am intense. She is patient, I am impatient. But we are similar in one thing — we are both honest."
Now you: "We are similar / different. He/She is _______ and I am _______ ."
Tip: The contrast structure "He is X, I am Y" is powerful at A1.1. Push for 2-3 pairs. This is where students discover they can make complex comparisons with just "to be."
Question 3
What is the best thing about your friend?
Teacher: "The best thing about Laura is that she is honest. When I am wrong, she is direct with me. That is not easy, but it is important."
Now you: "The best thing about _______ is that he/she is..."
Tip: If student says "he/she is nice" only — push: "OK, nice HOW? Nice with money? Nice with time? Nice with words?" Force the adjective into a real situation.
Question 4
Is there something annoying about your friend?
Teacher: "Yes! Laura is always late. Always! That is annoying. And sometimes she is too direct — her words are strong. But that is also why she is a good friend."
Now you: "Yes, sometimes he/she is _______ . That is annoying because..."
Tip: Students usually laugh here. Good — humor means comfort. Let them be honest. If they say "nothing is annoying," push gently: "Come on! Nobody is perfect. What is ONE small thing?"
Question 5
Where is your best friend right now? Is he or she near you or far?
Teacher: "Laura is in Guadalajara. That is far from Celaya — maybe 4 hours. It is sad, but she is always in my phone. We are always in contact."
Now you: "My best friend is in _______ . That is _______ from here."
Tip: Good moment for emotional vocabulary: "Is that sad for you? Or is it OK?" Simple follow-up that produces real feeling in English.
Question 6
Who is a better friend — you or your best friend?
Teacher: "Hmm, this is difficult! I think Laura is a better friend than me. She is more patient and more generous. I am more fun, but she is more reliable. We are a good team."
Now you: "I think _______ is a better friend because he/she is..."
Tip: This question forces self-reflection — powerful for adults. If student says "me," push: "Why? What are you better at?" If they say "my friend," push: "What is he/she better at than you?" Both paths produce rich language.
