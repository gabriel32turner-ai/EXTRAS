The Guardian · Lifestyle
"Studies show that a messy desk can actually increase creativity. But your coworkers probably still hate it."
— On research from the University of Minnesota finding that disorder can encourage creative thinking
BBC Worklife
"40% of people say a colleague's messy desk has affected their opinion of that person professionally — before ever speaking to them."
— We judge people by their spaces. A lot.
Launch line: "Quick question — before we start. Look at your desk or workspace right now. Is it clean, messy, or somewhere in between?" [Let them answer.] Then: "Would you be embarrassed if your boss walked in and saw it right now?" That single question opens the whole conversation.

Facilitation move: "Which opinion makes you feel a little uncomfortable? That's probably the one closest to a truth you don't want to admit."
🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Build from personal to provocative. Show tips only after the student answers.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
Have you ever cleaned your desk or home specifically because someone was coming over — and felt embarrassed about how it normally looks?
Almost everyone has done this. It's a great opener because it's personal and a little funny. Model: "Yes — I cleaned my whole apartment in 2 hours before my boss came for a meeting. Normally it's... not like that." Activate: clutter, tidy up, stress.
Q2
Have you ever shared a workspace or a room with someone who was much messier — or much cleaner — than you? How did you deal with it?
Very relatable for people who have shared offices, apartments, or hotel rooms on business trips. Activate: bother, drives me crazy, judge, force. Push for how they resolved it — or didn't.
Q3
Have you ever lost something important — a document, a key, your phone — because of a messy space? What happened?
Light and funny — everyone has a story. Good for present perfect practice ("Have you ever lost..."). The follow-up: "Did that make you want to organize more — or did you just accept it?" Activate: pile, pile up, distraction.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think a person's desk or home really says something about their personality? Or is it just a stereotype?
This is Rodrigo's argument from the Opinion Wall. Push for nuance: "What about creative people? What about very busy people who don't have time to clean?" Model: "I think it says something, but not everything. My car is always clean — my desk, not so much." Activate: judgment, control, system.
Q5
At work, should companies have rules about how clean or organized desks need to be? Is that fair — or too controlling?
Opinion territory. Most students will say "it depends on the job" — which is the right answer. Push: "What if it's a shared desk? What if clients can see it?" Activate: force, control, organize, system.
Q6
Some people say a messy environment helps them be more creative. Do you believe that — or does it just sound like an excuse?
Reference the Trigger headline here. It has real scientific support — but most tidy people will still reject it. Model: "I think it's true for some people. But I think 80% of the time it really is just an excuse." Activate: clutter, distraction, focus, habit.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if your new roommate or office partner was extremely messy — left dishes in the sink, papers everywhere, never cleaned up? Would you say something or stay silent?
Second conditional. Model: "If that happened, I would wait maybe one week — and then I'd say something calmly. But if they didn't change, I'd be really frustrated." Push for what they'd specifically say. This is great spoken English practice: confrontation language.
Q8
What would you do if your boss asked you to clean your desk because a client was coming — and you thought it was fine the way it was?
Workplace dynamics. Most will say they'd just clean it without arguing. Push: "Would you feel annoyed? Would you say anything?" Activate: judge, control, force, bother. Good for modals: "I would probably just... but I might feel..."
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Do you think people who are obsessed with cleanliness and organization sometimes use it to control other people — or to feel better about something they can't control in their life?
This flips Rodrigo's argument — instead of attacking messy people, it challenges clean freaks. Some students will immediately say yes. Others who identify as clean will feel defensive. Both reactions are productive. Activate: control, stress, habit.
Q10
Be honest — what does your desk or home look like right now? Are you happy with it, or is there a part of you that knows you should change something?
The self-reflection close. Light but personal. Almost everyone has a small guilt here — either "I should be cleaner" or "I know I'm too obsessive about this." Give the student space to be honest. Don't judge either answer.
B1.2 grammar check: Blocks 1–2 should activate present perfect naturally ("Have you ever..."). Blocks 3–4 activate second conditional ("What would you do if..."). If the student defaults to simple past in Block 3, gently model the correct form and move on.

Pacing note: This topic tends to be lighter than other labs — students enjoy it. If energy is high, let it run. The Exit Shot will land well regardless.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. Personal. No right answer.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
You get a new job — great salary, great team, great location. On your first day, you find out that your desk is shared with a colleague who leaves food, papers, and personal items everywhere. HR says the company has no policy about shared desks. Your colleague seems nice. Do you say something or just adapt?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Describe your desk or workspace right now — is it messy or clean? And tell me: has it always been like that, or has it changed over time?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: The scenario is concrete and realistic — shared desk, nice colleague, no rules. It forces a real decision between comfort and conflict. The "nice colleague" detail makes it harder to be direct.

Homework note: The recording topic is descriptive and personal — easy to record, but requires real vocabulary from today (clutter, pile, tidy up, organized, messy). Great for B1.2 output practice.

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about how to have difficult conversations at work — including how to tell a colleague something uncomfortable without damaging the relationship. That's exactly the English skill you'd need for today's Exit Shot."
