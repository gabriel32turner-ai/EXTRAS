Launch line: "Quick question — what's something you enjoy that you would never post on Instagram? Not because it's bad. Just because it's... a little embarrassing?" [Pause.] "That thing? Ricardo, 48
Engineer · Guadalajara
"I don't really believe in guilty pleasures. If you enjoy something and it doesn't hurt anyone, why feel guilty? The whole concept exists because we care too much about what other people think. Stop calling it 'guilty.' Just call it a pleasure. Own it."
Provocateur rationale: Ricardo challenges the concept itself — not the pleasure, but the guilt. His position makes students ask: "Why do I feel embarrassed about things I enjoy alone?" It's philosophically simple but emotionally disarming. Students who identify with Lucía or Carolina often quietly agree with Ricardo.

Facilitation move: After they pick their opinion: "And Ricardo — is he right? Should we stop using the word 'guilty'?" Let them decide.
🗣 Phase 4 — The Conversation Engine
10 Questions · 4 Blocks
Confessional and fun. Show tips only after the student answers.
23 min
04
Block 1 — Personal Experience
Q1
What is your guilty pleasure? The thing you enjoy that you would be a little embarrassed to tell people at work?
The big opener — and the most fun question of the session. Model first to break the ice: "My guilty pleasure is reality cooking shows. I watch them alone and I take them very seriously. Nobody at work knows." Once you model, students open up immediately. Activate: guilty pleasure, secret, enjoy, binge.
Q2
Have you ever been caught enjoying your guilty pleasure — by a family member, a friend, or a coworker? What happened?
Present perfect — personal and funny. Model: "Yes — my partner walked in while I was watching a dating reality show. I tried to change the channel. Too late." The embarrassment story is gold for B1.1. Activate: embarrassment, hide, pretend, judgment.
Q3
Did you have a guilty pleasure as a teenager that you laugh about now? What was it?
Past simple — nostalgic and light. Great for B1.1 storytelling. Common: terrible music, soap operas, video games, cheesy movies. Model: "When I was 16, I collected stickers. Like, hundreds of stickers. In albums. I was very proud of them. Now I find it hilarious." Activate: enjoy, secret, laugh at yourself.
Block 2 — Opinion
Q4
Do you think the word "guilty" is the right word — or is it okay to just enjoy things without feeling bad about it?
Ricardo's argument. This is the philosophical heart of the lab. Most B1.1 students haven't thought about this before. Both answers are valid — let the student explore. Model: "I think the guilt is real — not because the thing is bad, but because we compare ourselves to who we think we should be." Activate: judgment, admit, deserve.
Q5
Is it OK to judge someone for their guilty pleasure — even a little bit? Or should we always say "no judgment"?
Honest and light. Most people judge privately and pretend they don't. Model: "I think everyone judges a little. If someone tells me their guilty pleasure is something really strange, I have a reaction — even if I don't say it." Good for modals: "We should accept people's choices, but..." Activate: judge, pretend, admit.
Q6
Do you think guilty pleasures are more important when life is stressful? Why?
Simple and personal. Almost every student will say yes. Push: "Can you think of a really stressful week in your life — and what was your guilty pleasure that week?" Connecting stress to the pleasure makes it concrete. Activate: comfort, mood, treat, stress.
Block 3 — Hypothetical
Q7
What would you do if someone you respect — your boss, your partner, a good friend — saw your guilty pleasure and reacted badly?
Second conditional. Would they defend it or be embarrassed? Model: "If my boss saw me watching reality TV, I think I would laugh and say 'yes, this is my thing.' But if it was someone I really wanted to impress — I might feel embarrassed." Activate: embarrassment, judgment, admit, pretend.
Q8
What would you do if you had one full day — nobody watching, no responsibilities — to enjoy your guilty pleasure without any guilt at all?
The most fun hypothetical of the lab. Students describe their ideal "guilty pleasure day" — this produces the most energetic, flowing B1.1 speech. Let it run. Use "going to" structures: "I would wake up late, I'd order... I'd binge-watch... I'd eat..." Activate: binge-watch, enjoy, comfort, treat.
Block 4 — Provocative
Q9
Do you think your guilty pleasure says something true about you — something you don't usually show other people?
The most reflective question of the lab. Mateo's chocolate in the car reveals a need for silence and privacy. Lucía's reality TV reveals exhaustion and a need for simple entertainment. What does the student's guilty pleasure reveal? Give them a moment to think. Don't rush.
Q10
Is there a guilty pleasure you had before that you stopped — not because you wanted to, but because you felt too old or too serious for it? Do you miss it?
Nostalgic closing question. Very personal and sometimes unexpectedly touching. Common: video games abandoned after becoming a parent, cartoon shows, particular music, hobbies that felt "childish." Model: "I used to draw cartoons for fun. I stopped around 28. I thought it was too childish. Sometimes I miss it." Activate: regret, enjoy, secret, laugh at yourself.
B1.1 grammar check: Q2–Q3 = present perfect + past simple. Q7–Q8 = second conditional. Q8 is especially good for "would + verb" practice in a fun context.

Energy note: This is the highest-energy lab in the B1.1 lineup. Students laugh, confess, and connect. Q8 (the full day of guilty pleasure) often produces the longest, most spontaneous speech of the session. Let it happen. Q9 and Q10 bring the energy down gently — both are worth the slowdown.
💣 Phase 5 — The Exit Shot
The Final Question
One question. No judgment. Honest answer only.
5 min
05
💣 Exit Shot
Your closest friend asks you: "Tell me your real guilty pleasure — the one you've never told anyone." Do you tell them? And if yes — what do you say?
🎙 WhatsApp Homework: Record a 60-second voice message in English: "Tell me your guilty pleasure. Describe it, explain when you do it, and tell me: do you feel guilty — or actually not?" Send before our next session.
Why it works: The Exit Shot has two layers — the decision to share, and then the actual content. "Do you tell them?" tests trust and vulnerability. "What do you say?" tests self-knowledge. It's warm and funny, but genuinely personal.

Homework note: The easiest homework in the B1.1 lineup — students enjoy recording this one. The topic is fun, personal, and requires no research. Expect high completion rates.

Upsell moment: "Next session we talk about things that made us happy as kids — but we've forgotten as adults. Very similar energy to today, but going back in time. I think you'll like it."
