Describe Your Best Friend
(Honest Version)

Your best friend is great — but they also do things that drive you crazy. Today we describe the real person, not the perfect one.

🪝
The Trigger
Phase 1 · Let's Talk Starter
⏱ 4 min
A simple question to start
"Your best friend is the person you call when something is wrong. But they are also the person who sometimes makes you angry, says the wrong thing, or forgets important things. Can you describe your best friend — the REAL version, not the perfect version?"
Teacher Script: Ask directly: "Who is your best friend? Tell me one GREAT thing about them — and one thing they do that is a little annoying." The "honest version" concept breaks the ice immediately. Students love describing real people with both good and bad qualities. Keep follow-ups short: "How long are you friends?" / "Do you see them every week?"

The Conversation Engine
Phase 4 · 10 Questions · 4 Blocks
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience — Past Simple
Q1
When was the last time your best friend did something that made you laugh?
Target: past simple for the story. Push: "What happened? What did they do?" Model: "Last Saturday. We were at a restaurant and he talked to the wrong person. He thought it was his cousin. It was a stranger. Very funny." Short, clear, past simple narrative.
Q2
Did your best friend do something that made you angry last month? What happened?
Target: past simple. Push: "What did you do? Did you tell them?" Model: "Yes. She forgot my birthday. I was angry. I didn't say anything that day. But the next day I told her. She felt very bad. She bought me dinner." Simple story with a result.
Q3
When did you meet your best friend? Where were you?
Origin story — very rich for A2.1 description. Push: "How old were you? What was the first thing you talked about?" Target: past simple + time/place context. Model: "I met him at work. Ten years ago. He was my coworker. We ate lunch together every day and then we became friends."
Block 2 · Opinion — Simple Present
Q4
What is more important in a best friend — funny or honest?
Simple binary choice. Push: "Why? Can a person be both?" Target: I think + because. Model: "I think honest is more important. A funny person is nice. But an honest person is necessary. I need someone to tell me the truth, not just make me laugh." Gets clear opinions fast.
Q5
Do you think it is OK to be angry with your best friend — or do you always need to be nice?
Good for: I think it's OK / I think you need to. Push: "What do you do when you're angry with a friend?" Target: present simple opinion + can. Model: "Yes, I think it's OK. A best friend is not a boss. You can be honest. You can say 'that was not nice.' Real friends can be angry and then continue."
Q6
Is it a problem if your best friend and your partner don't like each other?
Very personal and relatable. Push: "Did this happen to you?" Target: present simple + it is/it isn't. Model: "Yes, it is a problem. You can't enjoy time with both of them. You always feel in the middle. It is very uncomfortable." Good for 2-3 exchanges.
Block 3 · Future Plans — Going to / Will
Q7
Are you going to see your best friend this week or this weekend?
Target: going to + present continuous for plans. Push: "What are you going to do? Where are you going to go?" Model: "Yes, I'm going to see her on Saturday. We are going to eat at a new restaurant. She is going to pay because she forgot my birthday last month." Keeps it personal and fun.
Q8
Is there something you want to tell your best friend — but you didn't say it yet? Are you going to say it?
Emotionally rich but grammatically simple. Target: going to + will for intention. Push: "What is it? When are you going to say it?" Model: "Yes. I want to tell him that his new girlfriend is not good for him. But I don't know how. I'm going to talk to him soon. I think. Maybe." The uncertainty is real and valid.
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Be honest — do YOU do annoying things to your best friend sometimes? What do you do?
The honest self-reflection question. Most students will laugh and admit something. Push: "Does your friend say anything — or do they accept it?" Target: present simple + I do / I sometimes. Model: "Yes. I always cancel plans at the last minute. She hates that. I say 'I'm tired' and I don't go. It's not nice. I know."
Q10
A friend asks you: "Is my new haircut nice?" — but it's terrible. What do you say?
Simple scenario, universally relatable. Push: "Do you tell the truth — or do you lie?" Target: present simple + direct speech practice. Model: "I say 'it's different! Very interesting!' I don't say 'terrible.' That's too honest. But I also don't say 'beautiful' because that's a lie." Get the student to say the actual words they would use.
💣
The Exit Shot
Phase 5 · One Final Scenario
⏱ 5 min
Final Question
Your best friend is going to make a big mistake — a new job, a new relationship, or a big purchase. You can see the problem. They are excited and happy. They didn't ask your opinion. Are you going to say something — or are you going to wait?
Homework Loop: "Record a 60-second WhatsApp voice message. Tell me: what are you going to do — say something or wait? And what are you going to say? In English. Simple words are perfect."
