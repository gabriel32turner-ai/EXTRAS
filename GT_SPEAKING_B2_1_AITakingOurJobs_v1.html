B2.1 Technology & Media 🔥 Medium 50 min
AI Taking Our Jobs
Not a distant sci-fi scenario — it's already happening in accounting, design, law, and journalism. The question is whether your job is next.
🪝
Phase 1
The Trigger
One number. Let it land before anyone starts rationalizing.
⏱ 4 min
Goldman Sachs Research Report · 2023
"Generative AI could automate up to 300 million full-time jobs globally — roughly 18% of work worldwide. White-collar and knowledge workers face the highest exposure."
Unlike previous waves of automation that primarily displaced manual and repetitive labor, AI is targeting the jobs that require language, analysis, creativity, and decision-making — the skills that professionals spent years developing. Goldman Sachs estimates that roughly two-thirds of current jobs will be partially automated, with 7% fully replaced in the near term.
Teacher Script Put the number up: 300 million jobs. Then ask: "Before we discuss whether this is real — what's your gut reaction to that number? Panic? Skepticism? Something else?" Let 2–3 students react without interruption. Then: "How many of you have already used AI to do part of your job? Be honest." That second question almost always reframes the whole conversation — they're already doing it.

"AI can't replace what I do."
Said confidently by every professional — right before their industry gets disrupted.
"I use AI to work faster, not to replace myself."
The optimistic position that most forward-thinking professionals are landing on right now.
"If you don't learn AI, someone who does will take your job."
The line that every LinkedIn thought leader has posted at least once this year.
"That's above my pay grade."
Said when someone is asked to make a decision or take a responsibility they're not equipped for — or don't want.
"We're doing more with less."
What management says after layoffs. What employees hear: "We expect the same output with fewer people."

Phase 3
The Opinion Wall
Four positions on the same question. Pick one and defend it.
⏱ 10 min
😰
Fernanda, 34
Graphic Designer · Monterrey
"I've watched AI tools go from novelty to genuinely threatening in under two years. Clients who used to pay me $800 for a campaign now generate something 'good enough' with Midjourney in 20 minutes. My industry isn't transforming — it's collapsing for people at my level. And nobody wants to say it because it sounds like complaining."
😎
Carlos, 41
Software Engineer · CDMX
"Every major technology shift in history — electricity, the internet, personal computers — eliminated some jobs and created more. The people who got displaced were the ones who refused to adapt. I'm not worried about AI. I'm learning how to build with it. The engineers who will lose their jobs are the ones waiting to see what happens."
🤨
Valeria, 47
HR Director · Guadalajara
"My concern isn't the technology — it's the speed. Historically, disruption took decades. This is happening in years. We don't have the education systems, the policy frameworks, or the social safety nets to absorb this transition at the pace it's actually moving. The technology might be fine. The human infrastructure around it isn't ready."
🧊
Rodrigo, 52
CEO · Manufacturing · Querétaro
⚡ Provocateur
"I'll be honest: as a business owner, AI has given me options I didn't have before. I can now do the work of 15 people with 8. Is that good for society? I don't know. Is it good for my company? Absolutely. And if I don't do it, my competitor will — and then I won't have any jobs to offer at all. This isn't cruelty. It's survival."
Discussion prompt: "Which of these four people do you trust the most? Which one makes you most uncomfortable — and why?" Rodrigo is the provocateur because he says the quiet part out loud: business owners are already making these decisions, and the logic is hard to argue with even if the consequences are painful. His honesty is the discomfort.

Phase 4
The Conversation Engine
10 questions. Their actual jobs are in the room. Keep it real.
⏱ 23 min
Block 1 · Personal Experience
Q1
Have you started using any AI tools at work — even casually? What do you use them for, and what do you still do yourself?
Teacher Tip Almost every B2.1 professional has used ChatGPT, Copilot, or a similar tool by now. Push for specifics: writing emails? Summarizing reports? Generating code? The gap between "what I use AI for" and "what I'd be embarrassed to admit AI does for me" is where the real conversation is.
Q2
Has AI already changed your workload, your team size, or how projects get done at your company? Give me a specific example.
Teacher Tip This grounds the abstract debate in their actual workplace. Listen for: roles that were eliminated, tasks that got absorbed, timelines that compressed. If they say "nothing has changed," push: "Has your company talked about AI at all in the last 12 months?"
Q3
Have you ever felt that a tool, a system, or a colleague made part of your role feel unnecessary? How did you handle that feeling?
Teacher Tip This is about job security anxiety — a real and underexplored emotion at work. Good vocabulary moment: redundancy, pivot, upskilling. Some students will be very honest here. Others will deflect. Both are useful conversation starters.
Block 2 · Opinion
Q4
Is there a meaningful difference between AI automating a factory worker's job and AI automating a lawyer's or accountant's job? Why does it feel different?
Teacher Tip This is about class and identity. White-collar workers historically felt immune to automation — and now they don't. The feeling of difference is partly about status, not just skill complexity. Push them to name that honestly.
Q5
Do you think companies have a responsibility to retrain workers they displace with AI — or is that the individual's problem to solve?
Teacher Tip Corporate vs. individual responsibility. Most students will say "both" — push them to assign a percentage. "If it's both — how much is the company's job? 70/30? 50/50?" The number reveals what they actually believe.
Q6
Carlos said people who lose their jobs to AI "refused to adapt." Is that fair — or does it ignore very real barriers to reskilling?
Teacher Tip The survivorship bias argument. Carlos speaks from a position of privilege — not everyone has the time, money, or access to reskill. Push: "What barriers would make it impossible for you personally to pivot right now?" Concrete answers are better than abstract sympathy.
Block 3 · Hypothetical
Q7
If your company offered to pay for any training you wanted — but you had to choose within 30 days — what would you learn, and why?
Teacher Tip Second conditional + future planning. This reveals what they actually think is valuable and future-proof — not what they think they should say. Common answers: data analysis, AI tools, project management. Ask: "Would any of that have been your answer 5 years ago?"
Q8
In 15 years, will your current job title still exist? If not — what will have replaced it?
Teacher Tip Will + future speculation. The title might survive even if the content transforms completely — push them to distinguish between the label and the actual work. "Your title might say 'analyst' in 2040. But what will you actually be doing all day?"
Block 4 · Provocative
Q9
Be honest: have you ever used AI to do something at work that you were paid to do yourself — without telling anyone?
Teacher Tip The ethics question that nobody talks about openly. Many professionals have. The silence or laughter in the room is the answer. Don't push for confession — just let them reflect on where the line is between "using tools" and "being paid for work you didn't do."
Q10 · Self-Reflection
What part of your job do you think only you can do — the part no algorithm could replicate? And are you absolutely sure?
Teacher Tip The "are you absolutely sure?" ending is the key — it's an invitation to doubt a comfortable belief. Most people name relationship-building, judgment, or creativity. Push gently: "Five years ago people said the same thing about writing, coding, and design." Don't resolve the discomfort. End there.

Phase 5
The Exit Shot
One question. No comfortable answer. Record your response.
⏱ 5 min
💣 Exit Shot · B2.1 AI Taking Our Jobs
Your company announces it will use AI to reduce headcount by 20% over the next 18 months. Your job is safe — but 3 of your teammates will lose theirs. Your boss asks you to help train the AI system that will replace them. Do you do it?
Homework · WhatsApp Voice Note (1 min)
Record your answer in English. Do you say yes — and keep your job? Or do you push back — and risk becoming one of the 20%? And what does your answer say about your actual values?
Retention Hook This scenario is already happening in real companies — engineers and analysts are being asked to document their own processes to train AI replacements. The moral collision between self-preservation and solidarity is unresolvable, and that's exactly what makes it stay with the student. End: "Send me your voice note. I want to know if your answer changes between now and Thursday."
